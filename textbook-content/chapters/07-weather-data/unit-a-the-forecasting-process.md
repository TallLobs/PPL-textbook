# Unit A: The Forecasting Process

Picture yourself at 3,000 feet with deteriorating visibility ahead—your ability to interpret weather data could mean the difference between a routine flight and a dangerous situation. As a pilot, you're not just a consumer of weather information; you're an active participant in the forecasting process, constantly gathering, interpreting, and applying meteorological data to make critical flight decisions. Understanding how weather data is collected, processed, and disseminated forms the foundation of sound aeronautical decision-making.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this unit, you will be able to:

- Identify the various aviation weather services and their roles in the forecasting process
- Explain how surface weather observation systems collect and transmit meteorological data
- Describe upper air weather observation methods and their importance to aviation forecasting
- Analyze weather information obtained from radar and satellite observation systems
- Locate appropriate weather service outlets and understand briefing procedures
- Distinguish between different types of weather briefings and select the appropriate briefing for your flight
- Decode aviation weather reports using standard meteorological abbreviations and symbols
- Interpret aviation forecasts and understand the methods used in weather prediction

## INTRODUCTION TO AVIATION WEATHER SERVICES

Aviation weather services represent one of the most critical safety systems supporting flight operations worldwide. The ability to accurately forecast and communicate weather conditions directly impacts every flight decision, from initial planning through landing. Understanding how these services operate, their capabilities, and their limitations forms the foundation for safe aviation weather decision-making.

Modern aviation weather services result from decades of technological advancement and international cooperation. Today's pilots have access to unprecedented amounts of weather data, delivered through multiple channels and updated continuously. However, this wealth of information requires proper understanding and application to be truly effective.

### Weather Service Organizations and Responsibilities

Aviation weather services operate through a **combined effort** of multiple government agencies, international organizations, and private companies. In the United States, four primary organizations form the backbone of aviation weather services.

The **National Weather Service (NWS)**, operating under the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA), serves as the primary meteorological authority. The NWS operates weather forecast offices nationwide, maintains the national network of weather observation stations, and produces aviation-specific forecasts including Terminal Aerodrome Forecasts (TAFs) and Area Forecasts.

The **Federal Aviation Administration (FAA)** provides aviation-specific weather services and operates specialized weather detection systems. The FAA maintains Terminal Doppler Weather Radar (TDWR) systems at major airports, operates the Flight Service Station network, and coordinates weather information distribution to pilots and air traffic controllers.

The **Department of Defense (DOD)**, through the Air Force Weather Agency, contributes significant resources to weather observation and forecasting. Military weather stations worldwide provide critical data, particularly in remote areas where civilian facilities may be limited. The DOD also operates specialized weather reconnaissance aircraft that gather data from severe weather systems.

**Foreign weather organizations** play increasingly important roles as aviation becomes more global. International cooperation ensures consistent weather standards and data sharing across national boundaries. The International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) coordinates global aviation weather standards, ensuring that weather products meet consistent international specifications.

> **Key Point**: No single organization provides all aviation weather services. Pilots must understand that their weather information comes from multiple sources, each with specific strengths and coverage areas.

Private weather companies supplement government services by providing specialized forecasting, enhanced graphics, and customized briefing services. These organizations often repackage government data into more user-friendly formats or provide specialized analysis for specific aviation operations.

### National Weather Service (NWS) and FAA Coordination

The relationship between the NWS and FAA represents a carefully coordinated partnership essential to aviation safety. This coordination ensures that meteorological expertise combines effectively with aviation operational knowledge.

**NWS responsibilities** include operating the national weather observation network, producing all official weather forecasts for aviation use, and maintaining the NEXRAD (WSR-88D) weather radar system. The NWS employs meteorologists trained specifically in aviation weather, ensuring that forecasts address aviation-specific concerns such as turbulence, icing, and low-level wind shear.

**FAA responsibilities** focus on weather information dissemination and aviation-specific weather systems. Flight Service Stations provide weather briefings to pilots, while Air Traffic Control facilities relay current weather observations and pilot reports. The FAA also operates airport weather stations and specialized detection systems for phenomena like wind shear and microbursts.

The coordination process operates through formal agreements and real-time communication systems. Weather warnings and advisories flow from NWS forecast offices to FAA facilities within minutes of issuance. Similarly, pilot weather reports collected by FAA facilities immediately feed into NWS forecasting systems.

**Automated Weather Observing Systems (AWOS)** and **Automated Surface Observing Systems (ASOS)** exemplify this coordination. While the NWS maintains quality control and standardization for these systems, the FAA often operates and maintains the equipment at airports. This shared responsibility ensures consistent, reliable weather observations across the national airspace system.

Emergency weather coordination becomes critical during severe weather events. NWS meteorologists may be stationed directly in Air Route Traffic Control Centers during significant weather events, providing real-time consultation to air traffic controllers. This direct coordination enables immediate adjustments to air traffic flow and enhanced safety warnings to pilots.

### International Weather Cooperation and Data Sharing

Modern aviation weather services depend heavily on international cooperation and data sharing. Weather systems do not respect national boundaries, making global coordination essential for accurate forecasting and flight safety.

The **World Meteorological Organization (WMO)** coordinates global weather observation and data sharing standards. Through WMO protocols, weather observations from thousands of stations worldwide flow into international databases within hours of observation. This global data network enables accurate weather modeling and forecasting for international flight operations.

**International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO)** standards ensure consistency in aviation weather products worldwide. ICAO Annex 3 specifies international standards for aviation meteorological services, including observation procedures, forecast formats, and dissemination requirements. These standards enable pilots to interpret weather information consistently regardless of location.

**Radiosonde observations** provide critical upper-air weather data through international cooperation. Approximately 75,000 weather balloons launch worldwide each year, providing temperature, humidity, pressure, and wind data from the surface to 115,000 feet. This global network enables accurate modeling of atmospheric conditions affecting international aviation.

**Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay (AMDAR)** represents international cooperation in automated weather observation. Commercial aircraft equipped with AMDAR systems automatically transmit weather observations during flight, providing approximately 220,000 to 230,000 observations daily worldwide. This data proves particularly valuable over oceanic areas where surface observations are unavailable.

Satellite weather data sharing enables global coverage of weather phenomena. Multiple nations operate weather satellites, with data sharing agreements ensuring comprehensive global coverage. This cooperation provides critical weather imagery and atmospheric sounding data for areas where surface observations are limited or unavailable.

> **International Standards**: ICAO standards require that aviation weather services meet specific accuracy, timeliness, and availability criteria. These standards ensure that pilots receive consistent, reliable weather information regardless of location.

**Volcanic Ash Advisory Centers (VAACs)** exemplify international cooperation for specific aviation hazards. Nine VAACs worldwide coordinate volcanic ash detection and forecasting, ensuring that volcanic ash advisories reach pilots and airlines globally within established timeframes.

### Accuracy Limitations and Forecast Reliability

While modern weather forecasting capabilities continue advancing, pilots must understand the inherent limitations and reliability characteristics of weather services. No weather forecast achieves 100 percent accuracy, making understanding of forecast limitations critical for safe flight operations.

**Forecast accuracy** varies significantly with forecast period and weather phenomena. Short-term forecasts (0-6 hours) generally achieve higher accuracy than longer-term forecasts. Current weather observations typically maintain high accuracy, while 24-hour forecasts show decreased reliability. Pilots should weight weather information according to these accuracy characteristics.

Temperature forecasts generally achieve the highest accuracy, typically within 3-5 degrees Fahrenheit for 24-hour forecasts. Wind forecasts show moderate accuracy for direction and speed, though sudden changes associated with frontal passages or thunderstorm outflows may not be accurately predicted in timing or intensity.

**Precipitation timing and intensity** represent significant forecasting challenges. While meteorologists can reliably predict the general occurrence of precipitation, precise timing and intensity often vary from forecasts. This limitation particularly affects flight planning for airports with low visibility minimums or contaminated runway concerns.

**Thunderstorm forecasting** shows both strengths and limitations. Meteorologists reliably predict general areas of thunderstorm development and can identify atmospheric conditions favorable for severe weather. However, predicting exact thunderstorm locations, timing, and intensity remains challenging, particularly for air-mass thunderstorms.

Ceiling and visibility forecasts critical for aviation operations show variable accuracy depending on the underlying weather phenomena. Forecasts for ceilings and visibility associated with large-scale weather systems generally achieve good accuracy. However, localized phenomena such as radiation fog or sea breeze effects prove more difficult to predict accurately.

**Computer modeling capabilities** have dramatically improved forecast accuracy over recent decades. Modern numerical weather prediction models process millions of atmospheric observations and calculate atmospheric behavior using sophisticated mathematical equations. However, these models still require meteorologist interpretation and cannot account for all local effects.

The **"Butterfly Effect"** or sensitive dependence on initial conditions inherently limits long-range forecast accuracy. Small errors in initial atmospheric observations can amplify over time, limiting detailed forecast accuracy beyond approximately one week. This fundamental limitation affects all weather forecasting systems regardless of technological advancement.

**Model resolution limitations** affect forecast detail and accuracy. Global weather models typically use grid spacing of 10-25 miles, meaning they cannot resolve weather features smaller than this scale. Local terrain effects, sea breezes, and other mesoscale phenomena may not be adequately represented in model forecasts.

Pilots must understand that **forecast confidence** varies with meteorological situations. High confidence exists for well-organized, large-scale weather systems such as major storm systems or cold fronts. Lower confidence applies to convective weather, fog formation, and other localized phenomena.

> **Forecast Reliability**: Terminal Aerodrome Forecasts (TAFs) achieve approximately 85-90% accuracy for major weather elements during the first 6 hours, decreasing to 70-80% accuracy for 12-24 hour forecasts. Pilots should consider these accuracy statistics when making flight decisions.

**Real-time weather information systems** provide current observations with high accuracy but limited geographic coverage. METARs represent actual conditions at specific airports but may not reflect conditions between reporting points or rapid weather changes between observation times.

Understanding forecast limitations enables better flight decision-making. Pilots should use multiple weather sources, maintain awareness of forecast uncertainty, and prepare contingency plans when operating near forecast weather minimums. Conservative decision-making becomes increasingly important as forecast confidence decreases or when operating with limited alternate airport options.

Weather services continue improving through technological advancement and increased understanding of atmospheric processes. However, the fundamental challenges of predicting a chaotic system like the atmosphere ensure that some forecast limitations will persist. Successful pilots understand these limitations and incorporate them into comprehensive flight planning and decision-making processes.

## SURFACE WEATHER OBSERVATION SYSTEMS

Surface weather observations form the foundation of all aviation weather services, providing the critical data that meteorologists use to analyze current conditions and create forecasts. These observations are compiled into **METARs** (Aviation Routine Weather Reports) that pilots rely on for both preflight planning and inflight decision-making. Understanding how these observations are collected, transmitted, and distributed is essential for safe flight operations.

The surface observation network consists of thousands of reporting stations across the United States, operated by government agencies, private contractors, and automated systems. This comprehensive network ensures that pilots have access to current weather information for airports and regions throughout their route of flight.

### Manual Surface Observations and Reporting Stations

**Manual surface observations** are conducted by trained weather observers at airports and weather stations who visually assess current conditions and record meteorological data. These human observers provide detailed assessments that automated systems cannot always capture, particularly regarding weather phenomena like cloud types, precipitation characteristics, and visibility restrictions.

Manual observations are typically conducted at major airports where commercial operations require the highest level of weather service. The observer records elements including wind direction and speed, visibility, present weather, sky conditions, temperature, dew point, and altimeter setting. These observations are transmitted hourly, with special observations issued whenever significant weather changes occur.

**Weather reporting stations** are strategically located to provide comprehensive geographic coverage. The **National Weather Service (NWS)** operates many of these facilities, while others are run by FAA personnel or contract weather observers. Each station is assigned a unique four-letter **ICAO identifier** that begins with "K" in the contiguous United States, such as KORD for Chicago O'Hare or KLAX for Los Angeles International.

Manual observers undergo extensive training to ensure consistent and accurate reporting. They must be able to estimate cloud heights, assess visibility in various weather conditions, and identify different types of precipitation and obscurations. The human element in weather observation remains valuable because trained observers can detect subtle changes in conditions and provide context that automated systems might miss.

> **Observer Training Requirements**: Manual weather observers must complete comprehensive training programs covering meteorological principles, observation techniques, and reporting procedures. They must demonstrate proficiency in estimating cloud heights up to 12,000 feet and visibility distances from less than one mile to greater than ten miles.

### Automated Weather Observing Systems (AWOS)

**Automated Weather Observing Systems (AWOS)** represent a significant advancement in weather observation technology, providing continuous, real-time weather data without requiring human observers. These systems are particularly valuable at airports where maintaining full-time weather personnel would be cost-prohibitive.

AWOS installations use multiple sensors to measure atmospheric conditions automatically. **Wind sensors** provide direction and speed information, while **visibility sensors** use light scattering techniques to determine prevailing visibility. **Ceilometers** emit laser pulses upward to detect cloud base heights, and **temperature and humidity sensors** provide precise measurements for calculating dew point and density altitude.

There are several levels of AWOS capability. **AWOS-A** provides only altimeter setting information, while **AWOS-1** adds wind data and usually includes temperature, dew point, and density altitude. **AWOS-2** systems include visibility and variable wind direction information. The most comprehensive **AWOS-3** installations add cloud/ceiling data and can identify precipitation types.

AWOS systems broadcast continuous weather information on dedicated VHF radio frequencies, typically in the 118.0 to 136.975 MHz range. The broadcasts update every minute, with a complete weather observation transmitted approximately every 20 seconds. Pilots can access this information by tuning to the published AWOS frequency for their departure or destination airport.

The computer-generated voice announcements follow a standard format, beginning with the airport identifier and observation time, followed by wind, visibility, weather and obstructions to vision, sky condition, temperature, dew point, and altimeter setting. The system concludes with remarks and advisories as appropriate.

> **AWOS Limitations**: AWOS systems cannot detect all weather phenomena that human observers can identify. They may have difficulty distinguishing between rain and snow during mixed precipitation events, and cannot assess conditions beyond their immediate sensor locations.

### Automated Surface Observing Systems (ASOS)

**Automated Surface Observing Systems (ASOS)** represent the most sophisticated automated weather observation technology deployed in the United States. ASOS is the primary surface weather observing network for the NWS and serves as the foundation for aviation weather services at most airports.

ASOS installations are more comprehensive than AWOS systems, incorporating additional sensors and enhanced data processing capabilities. These systems can identify **precipitation type and intensity**, including the ability to distinguish between rain, snow, and freezing precipitation. Advanced algorithms analyze multiple sensor inputs to provide more accurate assessments of visibility and present weather conditions.

The **ASOS network** consists of more than 900 automated stations across the United States, with installations at virtually all airports that serve commercial aviation. Each ASOS station transmits observations every minute, with routine METAR reports generated every hour at 15 minutes before the hour (e.g., 1755Z, 1855Z, 1955Z).

**ASOS sensors** include sophisticated equipment for measuring multiple atmospheric parameters simultaneously. The **present weather identifier** can detect and classify precipitation, fog, haze, and blowing snow. **Freezing rain sensors** specifically identify this hazardous condition that is critical for aviation operations. Some ASOS installations include **thunderstorm detection** capability through lightning sensors.

When significant weather changes occur, ASOS automatically generates **Special Weather Reports (SPECI)** without waiting for the next routine observation time. These special reports ensure that pilots and meteorologists receive timely information about rapidly changing conditions that could affect flight safety.

ASOS observations include a **backup facility indicator** in the remarks section. "AO1" indicates a station without a precipitation discriminator, while "AO2" identifies stations with enhanced precipitation detection capability. This information helps pilots understand the reliability of precipitation type identification at specific locations.

### Surface Observation Network Coverage and Limitations

The surface weather observation network provides extensive coverage across the United States, but pilots must understand both the capabilities and limitations of this system when making flight planning and operational decisions.

**Geographic coverage** varies significantly between different regions of the country. The eastern United States has dense station spacing, with observations available every 50-100 miles in most areas. Western states, particularly in mountainous regions, have much larger gaps between reporting stations, sometimes exceeding 200 miles between observations.

**Temporal coverage** also varies by station type and location. Major airports typically provide continuous automated observations with hourly METAR reports, while smaller airports may have limited reporting schedules. Some remote locations provide observations only during daylight hours or when airport operations are active.

Each surface observation represents conditions at a **specific point and time**. Weather conditions can vary significantly over short distances, particularly in areas of complex terrain or during rapidly changing weather situations. A METAR from an airport 50 miles away may not accurately represent conditions at the pilot's location.

**Automated systems** have inherent limitations in weather detection and classification. They may miss localized phenomena such as virga, brief rain showers, or patchy fog that occur between sensor locations. Wind measurements represent conditions at the sensor site, which may not reflect wind patterns throughout the airport or surrounding area.

The network's ability to capture the **complete weather picture** depends on the density of reporting stations and the sophistication of their equipment. Pilots flying in areas with sparse station coverage must rely on additional weather sources, including pilot reports and radar data, to supplement surface observations.

> **Network Density**: The surface observation network provides approximately 1,900 reporting locations across the United States, including ASOS, AWOS, and manual stations. This represents roughly one station per 2,000 square miles, though distribution is heavily weighted toward populated areas and major airports.

**Maintenance and reliability** issues can temporarily reduce network effectiveness. Automated stations require regular calibration and maintenance to ensure accurate measurements. Sensor failures or communication problems can create temporary gaps in coverage that may not be immediately apparent to pilots accessing weather information.

Understanding these coverage patterns and limitations helps pilots make informed decisions about weather information sources and the need for additional data when planning flights in areas with limited surface observation coverage.

## UPPER AIR WEATHER OBSERVATION METHODS

Upper air weather observations provide critical three-dimensional weather data that surface observations cannot capture. Unlike surface weather stations that report conditions at ground level, upper air observation systems gather meteorological information throughout the vertical column of the atmosphere. This data is essential for understanding weather patterns, forecasting, and providing pilots with accurate information about conditions they will encounter during flight.

Four primary methods collect upper air weather data: **radiosonde balloon operations**, **Pilot Weather Reports (PIREPs)**, **Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay (AMDAR)** systems, and the **Meteorological Data Collection and Reporting System (MDCRS)**. Each system contributes unique capabilities and coverage to the comprehensive upper air observation network.

### RADIOSONDE BALLOON OPERATIONS AND DATA COLLECTION

**Radiosondes** represent the backbone of upper air weather observation worldwide. A radiosonde is a small cubic instrumentation package suspended below a six-foot hydrogen- or helium-filled balloon that provides detailed atmospheric profiles from surface to extreme altitudes.

The **balloon flight characteristics** are precisely engineered for optimal data collection. Upon release, the balloon ascends at approximately **1,000 feet per minute (fpm)**, maintaining a consistent climb rate that allows instruments to sample atmospheric conditions at regular intervals. The initial balloon diameter of six feet expands continuously as atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude.

During ascent, the balloon can reach altitudes as high as **115,000 feet** and drift as far as **125 miles** horizontally from the launch point. Flight duration typically lasts **2 hours or more**, depending on atmospheric conditions and balloon expansion rates. The extreme conditions encountered during these flights include temperatures as low as **-130°F** and pressures as low as a few thousandths of sea level pressure.

The instrumentation package continuously gathers critical atmospheric data including **air temperature**, **moisture content**, **barometric pressure**, **wind speed**, and **wind direction**. A **300-milliwatt radio transmitter** relays this information to ground stations in real-time, creating a continuous profile of atmospheric conditions.

> **Balloon Expansion Physics**: As the balloon rises through decreasing atmospheric pressure, it expands according to Boyle's Law. The balloon reaches its elastic limit when the diameter exceeds **20 feet**, at which point it bursts and the radiosonde begins its descent.

**Altitude capabilities** of radiosondes extend well into the stratosphere, providing data crucial for aviation weather forecasting. The comprehensive vertical profile obtained from surface to 115,000 feet captures information about jet stream locations, wind shear layers, temperature inversions, and moisture distribution that directly affects flight operations.

The **data collection process** operates continuously worldwide. Each year, over **75,000 balloons** are launched from radiosonde stations globally. The descent phase is controlled by a parachute system that protects people and property on the ground while allowing the instrument package to return to Earth safely.

**Recovery and reconditioning** programs maximize the economic efficiency of the radiosonde network. Approximately **20 percent** of launched radiosondes are recovered and returned to the National Weather Service for reconditioning and reuse. Return instructions printed on each radiosonde encourage public participation in the recovery program.

### PILOT WEATHER REPORTS (PIREPS) REQUIREMENTS AND PROCEDURES

**Pilot Weather Reports (PIREPs)** provide the only real-time source of information regarding actual flight conditions, particularly **turbulence**, **icing**, and **cloud heights**. PIREPs fill critical gaps in the weather observation network by reporting conditions that cannot be detected by ground-based instruments or automated systems.

**PIREP requirements** are established by Federal Aviation Administration regulations and Air Traffic Control procedures. When ceiling is below **5,000 feet** or visibility is at or below **five miles**, ATC facilities are **required to solicit PIREPs** from pilots in the affected area. This requirement ensures that current condition reports are available to other pilots and weather forecasters during marginal weather conditions.

**Filing procedures** for PIREPs follow a standardized format that ensures consistent and complete information transmission. The **standard reporting form** requires specific elements in a prescribed sequence, with items 1 through 5 being mandatory information when making any report.

**Required elements** include:
- **UA or UUA** (Routine or Urgent PIREP designation)
- **Location** (OV) - position relative to navigation aids
- **Time** (TM) - four digits in UTC
- **Altitude** (FL) - three digits for hundreds of feet
- **Aircraft type** (TP) - four digits maximum

Additional **weather phenomena** must include at least one observed condition such as turbulence, icing, cloud layers, visibility, or precipitation. This ensures that every PIREP provides actionable weather information to other pilots and air traffic controllers.

**Transmission methods** for PIREPs include direct radio contact with Air Traffic Control facilities, Flight Service Stations, or company dispatch. PIREPs are immediately entered into the weather distribution system, making them available to briefing personnel and other pilots within minutes of filing.

> **PIREP Urgency Classification**: Urgent PIREPs (UUA) are reserved for reports of severe weather conditions that pose immediate hazards to flight safety, including severe turbulence, severe icing, or tornadoes. Routine PIREPs (UA) cover all other observed conditions.

**Real-time turbulence reporting** through PIREPs provides critical safety information that no other observation system can deliver. Pilots report turbulence intensity using standard terminology: **light**, **moderate**, **severe**, or **extreme**. These reports allow air traffic controllers to advise other aircraft of potential hazards and help pilots make informed decisions about altitude changes or route deviations.

**Icing reports** from PIREPs inform other pilots about the presence, intensity, and altitude of icing conditions. Standard reporting includes icing type (**rime**, **clear**, or **mixed**), intensity (**trace**, **light**, **moderate**, or **severe**), and the altitude range where icing was encountered.

**Cloud height reporting** provides ground truth verification of ceiling and cloud layer information from automated observation systems. PIREPs often reveal multiple cloud layers, cloud tops, and bases that surface observations cannot detect, particularly important for VFR pilots planning flights above cloud layers.

### AIRCRAFT METEOROLOGICAL DATA RELAY (AMDAR) SYSTEMS

**Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay (AMDAR)** is an international program that utilizes commercial aircraft as automated weather observation platforms. AMDAR systems transform routine airline operations into a comprehensive global weather observation network, providing approximately **220,000 to 230,000 aircraft observations per day** worldwide.

**Automated aircraft weather observation systems** use onboard sensors and probes already installed for flight operations. These systems measure **wind**, **temperature**, **humidity/water vapor**, **turbulence**, and **icing data** without requiring pilot input or affecting normal flight operations. The automation ensures consistent data collection regardless of crew workload or flight conditions.

**AMDAR vertical profiles** provide exceptional value during aircraft climb and descent phases. These profiles capture atmospheric conditions through multiple altitude levels, creating detailed snapshots of the vertical structure of the atmosphere along flight paths. The concentration of commercial flights near major airports creates high-density observation networks in critical aviation areas.

**En route observations** from AMDAR systems provide continuous weather monitoring along heavily traveled air routes. The global nature of commercial aviation means AMDAR data covers oceanic areas and remote regions where traditional observation stations cannot be established.

> **AMDAR Benefits**: The program enhances aircraft safety and operating efficiency through improved weather analysis and forecasting. AMDAR data contributes to improved short and medium-term numerical weather forecasts for aviation, marine, defense, public weather, and environmental monitoring services.

**DataLink transmission methods** for AMDAR data utilize existing aircraft communication systems. The primary transmission method is **Very High Frequency (VHF) communications** through the **Aircraft Communications Addressing and Reporting System (ACARS)**. When ACARS coverage is unavailable, data can be transmitted via **satellite link** through **Aircraft to Satellite Data Acquisition and Relay (ASDAR)** systems.

**International coordination** ensures AMDAR data is shared globally among weather services. The World Meteorological Organization coordinates AMDAR programs to maximize coverage and standardize data formats. This international cooperation provides seamless weather observation coverage across national boundaries, particularly important for oceanic and polar flight routes.

The **quality control** process for AMDAR data includes automated checks for data consistency and plausibility. Advanced algorithms compare aircraft-reported conditions with surrounding observations and expected atmospheric conditions to identify and flag potentially erroneous data before distribution.

### METEOROLOGICAL DATA COLLECTION AND REPORTING SYSTEM (MDCRS)

The **Meteorological Data Collection and Reporting System (MDCRS)** serves as the United States implementation of automated airborne weather observation programs. MDCRS collects and disseminates real-time upper air weather observations from participating domestic airlines, providing dense weather observation coverage along major U.S. air routes.

**MDCRS operations** involve more than **1,500 participating aircraft** that automatically report wind and temperature data. A subset of these aircraft also provides **turbulence** and **humidity/water vapor** information, creating a comprehensive atmospheric monitoring network that operates 24 hours per day.

**Data transmission** occurs through the **Aircraft Communications Addressing and Reporting System (ACARS)**, managed by **Aeronautical Radio, Inc. (ARINC)**. ARINC processes the raw aircraft data and forwards it in **Binary Universal Form for the Representation of Meteorological Data (BUFR)** format to the National Weather Service. Raw data is simultaneously provided to the **Earth Science Research Laboratory (ESRL)** and participating airlines.

**Equipment programming** varies by airline and aircraft type. Each participating airline works with avionics manufacturers to program their equipment to provide specific levels of meteorological data collection. This customization allows airlines to balance their contribution to the weather observation network with operational requirements and system capabilities.

**Flight Data Acquisition and Monitoring System (FDAMS)** integration enables MDCRS data collection without interfering with normal flight operations. The FDAMS continuously monitors flight parameters for safety and maintenance purposes, and MDCRS systems tap into this existing data stream to extract meteorological information.

**Data collection phases** include **climb**, **en route**, and **descent** segments of flight. Climb and descent data provide vertical atmospheric profiles similar to radiosonde observations, while en route data creates detailed horizontal coverage along flight paths. The combination provides three-dimensional weather coverage in high-traffic airspace areas.

> **ACARS Range Limitations**: When aircraft operate beyond ACARS communication range, weather reports are temporarily buffered in aircraft systems. Once the aircraft returns to ACARS coverage, buffered reports are automatically downloaded, ensuring minimal data loss even during extended oceanic flights.

**ASDAR backup capability** provides data transmission options when ACARS systems are unavailable. The **Aircraft to Satellite Data Acquisition and Relay (ASDAR)** system uses satellite communication to transmit weather data from aircraft in remote areas or during ACARS system outages.

**Quality assurance** procedures for MDCRS data include automated range checks, consistency verification, and comparison with surrounding observations. Data that fails quality control checks is flagged but not discarded, allowing meteorologists to evaluate potentially extreme but valid atmospheric conditions.

The **operational impact** of MDCRS data extends beyond weather forecasting to direct flight safety applications. Real-time turbulence reports from MDCRS systems provide air traffic controllers with current information for pilot advisories and routing recommendations. Temperature and wind data improve flight planning accuracy and fuel consumption calculations.

**Integration with numerical weather prediction models** represents a critical application of MDCRS data. The high-resolution, real-time nature of MDCRS observations provides validation data for forecast models and helps meteorologists identify forecast errors quickly. This rapid feedback loop improves forecast accuracy for subsequent aviation weather products.

## RADAR AND SATELLITE WEATHER OBSERVATION SYSTEMS

Weather radar and satellite observation systems represent the most sophisticated tools available for detecting and monitoring weather phenomena that affect aviation safety. These systems provide real-time and near real-time weather data that complement surface and upper air observations to create a comprehensive weather picture for pilots and meteorologists. Understanding the capabilities and limitations of these systems is essential for proper interpretation of weather data and safe flight operations.

### WSR-88D NEXRAD Doppler Radar Operations

The **Weather Surveillance Radar-1988 Doppler (WSR-88D)**, commonly known as **NEXRAD** (Next Generation Weather Radar), forms the backbone of the United States weather radar network. This sophisticated Doppler radar system consists of 159 sites strategically positioned throughout the United States and selected overseas locations. The NEXRAD system operates as a joint venture between the National Weather Service (NWS), Air Force Weather Agency (AFWA), and the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA).

NEXRAD operates in two distinct operational modes based on atmospheric conditions. **Clear air mode** represents the radar's most sensitive operational configuration, designed to detect atmospheric phenomena when precipitation is absent. In this mode, the antenna rotates slowly, allowing extended sampling time of approximately 10 minutes per complete scan. This extended sampling period enables detection of subtle atmospheric features such as wind patterns, temperature inversities, and even bird migrations.

**Precipitation mode** activates automatically when the system detects significant moisture targets. The faster antenna rotation in this mode, typically 4 to 6 minutes per scan, provides more frequent updates when weather conditions are changing rapidly. This mode prioritizes the detection and tracking of precipitation intensity and movement patterns.

> **NEXRAD Coverage**: Each NEXRAD site provides weather coverage out to approximately 250 nautical miles, with precipitation detection effective to about 125 nautical miles due to earth curvature limitations.

The system measures **reflectivity** in **decibels of Z (dBZ)**, which correlates directly to precipitation intensity. Light precipitation typically registers less than 30 dBZ, moderate precipitation measures between 30-40 dBZ, heavy precipitation ranges from 40-50 dBZ, and extreme precipitation exceeds 50 dBZ. These measurements are color-coded on radar displays, with green indicating light intensity, yellow showing moderate intensity, red depicting heavy precipitation, and magenta representing extreme conditions.

NEXRAD's Doppler capabilities enable detection of **radial velocity**, measuring the motion of precipitation particles toward or away from the radar site. This capability proves invaluable for identifying rotation within thunderstorms, wind shear conditions, and gust fronts that pose significant hazards to aircraft operations.

### Terminal Doppler Weather Radar (TDWR) Applications

**Terminal Doppler Weather Radar (TDWR)** systems provide specialized weather detection capabilities for major airports across the United States. These dedicated radar systems operate specifically to protect aircraft during the critical phases of takeoff, approach, and landing. TDWR installations focus on detecting hazardous weather phenomena within approximately 30 nautical miles of the airport.

TDWR excels at identifying **microburst activity**, **wind shear**, and **gust fronts** that represent the greatest weather-related threats to aircraft in terminal airspace. The system's high resolution and rapid scan capabilities enable air traffic controllers to provide timely wind shear advisories and low-level wind shear alerts to pilots.

The radar provides automatic detection and alerting for **low-level wind shear (LLWS)** conditions when wind changes exceed specific thresholds. Controllers receive visual and audio alerts when LLWS conditions develop along approach and departure corridors. These alerts include specific runway information and the location where wind shear has been detected.

**Gust front detection** represents another critical TDWR capability. Gust fronts, the leading edge of thunderstorm outflow, can produce sudden wind direction changes exceeding 45 degrees and speed variations of 25 knots or greater. TDWR tracks these phenomena and provides controllers with precise location and movement information.

> **TDWR Integration**: TDWR data integrates with Low Level Wind Shear Alert System (LLWAS) and Integrated Terminal Weather System (ITWS) to provide comprehensive terminal area weather detection capabilities.

### Airport Surveillance Radar Weather Detection

**Airport Surveillance Radar (ASR)** systems, while primarily designed for aircraft detection and tracking, also provide significant weather detection capabilities. These radar systems operate in the terminal environment and can display precipitation intensity and coverage to air traffic controllers.

ASR weather detection operates in the **S-band** frequency range, typically around 2.7-2.9 GHz, which provides good precipitation detection while maintaining aircraft tracking capabilities. The system displays weather information as varying intensities that controllers can select for display based on operational requirements.

Controllers can adjust weather display intensity levels to balance aircraft tracking visibility with weather information. Higher intensity settings reduce display clutter but may mask lighter precipitation areas. Lower intensity settings show more complete weather coverage but can obscure aircraft data blocks and position symbols.

**Precipitation intensity** on ASR displays correlates to the standardized light, moderate, heavy, and extreme classifications. Controllers use this information to vector aircraft around weather areas and coordinate with pilots regarding precipitation intensity along their route of flight.

The **Airport Radar Weather Display** provides controllers with the capability to overlay weather information on the standard aircraft tracking display. This integration enables efficient traffic management around weather areas while maintaining positive aircraft separation.

### Airborne Radar and Satellite Weather Services

**Airborne weather radar** systems provide pilots with real-time weather detection capabilities independent of ground-based systems. Most commercial and many general aviation aircraft carry airborne radar systems operating in either **C-band** (approximately 6 GHz) or **X-band** (approximately 10 GHz) frequencies.

C-band radar provides superior precipitation penetration capabilities, allowing pilots to assess the internal structure of thunderstorms and determine areas of reduced precipitation intensity within larger storm systems. X-band radar offers higher resolution for detecting smaller weather features and precipitation gradients but has limited penetration through heavy precipitation.

**Tilt control** enables pilots to sample different altitude levels of weather systems. Proper tilt management allows assessment of storm tops, precipitation cores, and potential turbulence areas. Pilots must understand that airborne radar detects only precipitation-sized particles and cannot directly identify turbulence in clear air.

> **Radar Limitations**: Airborne radar cannot detect turbulence directly, ice crystals, or clear air turbulence. Pilots must infer turbulence potential from precipitation intensity and structure patterns.

**Commercial satellite weather services** provide comprehensive weather data through satellite uplink systems. These services deliver real-time weather information including NEXRAD imagery, surface analysis charts, pilot reports, and meteorological advisories directly to aircraft-mounted displays.

**Satellite-based weather systems** operate through either geostationary or low earth orbit satellite networks. Geostationary systems provide continuous coverage for specific geographic regions but may have limited coverage at extreme latitudes. Low earth orbit systems offer global coverage but with intermittent connectivity periods.

**Data latency** represents a critical consideration for satellite weather services. Weather information displayed on aircraft systems may be 5-20 minutes older than actual conditions due to processing, transmission, and display update cycles. Pilots must account for this delay when using satellite weather data for tactical weather avoidance decisions.

**Flight Information Service-Broadcast (FIS-B)** provides weather data through the ADS-B network on 978 MHz UAT frequency. This service includes NEXRAD precipitation maps, METARs, TAFs, AIRMETs, SIGMETs, pilot reports, and temporary flight restrictions. FIS-B requires ADS-B equipped aircraft and provides coverage primarily within the United States airspace system.

Weather data integration capabilities allow pilots to overlay multiple weather products on electronic flight displays. Common overlays include NEXRAD precipitation, surface analysis information, freezing level data, and significant weather advisories. Pilots must manage information display levels to prevent cognitive overload while maintaining situational awareness of relevant weather conditions affecting their specific flight route.

## WEATHER SERVICE OUTLETS AND BRIEFING PROCEDURES

Weather service outlets serve as the critical link between meteorological data collection systems and flight operations. These government, government-contracted, and private facilities provide aviation weather services through multiple channels, ensuring pilots have access to essential weather information 24 hours a day. Understanding how to access and utilize these services is fundamental to safe flight operations and regulatory compliance.

The coordination between various agencies including the FAA, National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA), and the National Weather Service (NWS) creates a comprehensive network of weather information delivery systems. These outlets complement the data collection systems by making processed weather information readily available to pilots in formats suitable for flight planning and in-flight decision making.

### Flight Service Station (FSS) Services and 1-800-WX BRIEF

**Flight Service Stations (FSS)** represent the primary source for preflight weather information in the United States. These facilities serve as centralized hubs for aviation weather briefing services, operating continuously to support flight operations nationwide. The FSS system has evolved to provide standardized, comprehensive weather briefings regardless of geographic location.

The **1-800-WX BRIEF** telephone service provides 24-hour access to certified weather briefing specialists from anywhere in the United States and Puerto Rico. This toll-free service connects pilots directly to FSS specialists who can provide tailored weather briefings based on specific flight requirements. The system automatically routes calls to the appropriate FSS facility based on the caller's location and flight plans.

When contacting 1-800-WX BRIEF, pilots should be prepared to provide essential flight information including aircraft identification and type, departure point, estimated time of departure (ETD), flight altitude, route of flight, destination, and estimated time en route (ETE). This information enables specialists to provide targeted weather information relevant to the specific flight profile. The briefing type requested—standard, abbreviated, or outlook—determines the scope and detail of information provided.

> **Regulatory Requirement**: All information provided during FSS briefings is recorded in the flight plan system with notation of the briefing type provided. This record can be referenced later for flight plan filing or amendments and is used during search and rescue operations for overdue or missing aircraft.

FSS facilities also provide comprehensive in-flight weather briefing services and weather advisories to flights within their area of responsibility. Pilots can contact FSS during flight for updated weather information, route changes, or emergency assistance. The specialists have access to real-time weather data and can provide current conditions, forecasts, and hazardous weather advisories as conditions change.

Contact information for individual FSS facilities can be found in the Chart Supplement U.S. (formerly the Airport/Facility Directory) or in the United States Government section of local telephone directories. However, the 1-800-WX BRIEF number provides universal access and is the preferred method for obtaining weather briefings.

### Telephone Information Briefing Service (TIBS)

The **Telephone Information Briefing Service (TIBS)** provides automated telephone recordings of meteorological and aeronautical information through the FSS system. This service utilizes recorded messages that are automatically updated as weather conditions change, providing pilots with preliminary weather information without requiring specialist interaction.

TIBS recordings include area and route briefings, airspace procedures, and special announcements relevant to specific geographic regions. The system covers standard weather elements including current conditions, forecasts, and significant weather phenomena. Special emphasis is placed on hazardous weather conditions, temporary flight restrictions, and airspace changes that could affect flight operations.

The service operates exclusively through touchtone telephone systems, requiring pilots to navigate through menu options to access specific information categories. Phone numbers for TIBS services are published in the Chart Supplement U.S., organized by geographic region and specific airport areas. Multiple TIBS numbers may serve different regions or provide specialized information for major metropolitan areas.

> **Important Limitation**: TIBS is designed as a preliminary briefing tool and does not replace standard briefings from FSS specialists. The automated nature of TIBS recordings means they cannot provide the customized analysis and recommendations that certified weather briefers offer during interactive briefings.

Pilots should use TIBS for initial weather assessment and route planning, particularly when conducting preliminary flight planning well in advance of departure. The service is most effective for routine weather checks and can help pilots determine whether more detailed briefings are necessary based on current conditions.

The automated update system ensures information currency, but pilots must recognize that recorded information may not capture rapidly changing weather conditions or provide the interpretive analysis available through specialist briefings. TIBS serves as an efficient preliminary tool but should be supplemented with comprehensive briefings for critical weather decisions.

### Hazardous Inflight Weather Advisory Service (HIWAS)

**Hazardous Inflight Weather Advisory Service (HIWAS)** provides continuous automated broadcasts of hazardous weather information over selected VOR navigational aids throughout the 48 contiguous states. This system ensures pilots have access to critical weather advisories during flight without requiring radio contact with ground facilities.

HIWAS broadcasts include **AIRMETs**, **SIGMETs**, **Convective SIGMETs**, and urgent **Pilot Weather Reports (PIREPs)**. These advisories warn of weather phenomena that could significantly impact flight safety, including severe turbulence, icing conditions, low-level wind shear, and instrument meteorological conditions. The broadcasts are automatically updated as new advisories are issued or existing ones are modified or cancelled.

The service utilizes existing VOR navigation infrastructure, making hazardous weather information available wherever VOR signals can be received. This integration provides comprehensive coverage throughout the continental United States without requiring separate communication equipment or frequencies. Pilots can monitor HIWAS broadcasts while navigating using the same VOR facilities for position fixing and course guidance.

> **Chart Symbol**: VOR facilities equipped with HIWAS capability are identified on aeronautical charts by an "H" symbol in the upper right corner of the VOR identification box. This marking indicates that hazardous weather advisories are continuously broadcast on that facility's voice frequency.

[Figure 13-4: HIWAS availability is shown on sectional chart - PHAK Ch 13, Fig 13-4]

To access HIWAS information, pilots tune the VOR frequency and listen to the continuous voice broadcast. The automated system cycles through current hazardous weather advisories, providing location information, valid times, and specific weather phenomena details. Pilots should note that HIWAS provides summary information and should contact FSS or Flight Watch for detailed weather discussions and additional information.

HIWAS broadcasts operate continuously, but pilots should recognize that the automated format provides standardized information without the interpretive analysis available through direct specialist contact. For complex weather situations or specific routing questions, pilots should use HIWAS information as an alerting service and follow up with interactive weather services for detailed planning assistance.

### Transcribed Weather Broadcast (TWEB) Alaska Operations

**Transcribed Weather Broadcast (TWEB)** provides continuous automated broadcast of meteorological and aeronautical data over selected low frequency (LF), medium frequency (MF), and VHF omnidirectional range (VOR) NAVAID facilities exclusively in Alaska. This service addresses the unique geographical and operational challenges of Alaska aviation operations.

TWEB broadcasts contain adverse weather conditions, surface weather observations, pilot reports, and density altitude statements when applicable. Alaska's extreme weather variations and challenging terrain make density altitude information particularly critical for aircraft performance calculations. The broadcasts automatically update as weather conditions change, ensuring pilots have access to current information throughout extended Alaska flights.

The service includes synopsis information, winds aloft forecasts, en route and terminal forecast data, and radar reports when available. This comprehensive weather package addresses the extended flight distances and limited alternate airports common in Alaska operations. Pilots can receive detailed weather information for multiple locations during a single broadcast cycle.

Selected TWEB locations provide **TEL-TWEB** service, offering telephone access to the same recorded weather information available over radio frequencies. Telephone numbers for TEL-TWEB services are published in the Alaska Chart Supplement U.S. This dual-access system ensures pilots can obtain weather information both during preflight planning and in-flight operations.

> **Primary Purpose**: TWEB broadcasts are intended primarily for preflight and in-flight planning purposes and should not be considered substitutes for specialist-provided preflight briefings. The automated nature of TWEB recordings cannot provide the analysis and recommendations available through interactive FSS briefings.

Alaska TWEB operations recognize the unique challenges of Alaska aviation, including extended overwater flights, mountain operations, and limited communication facilities. The service provides essential weather information where other communication methods may be unavailable or impractical. However, pilots should supplement TWEB information with comprehensive briefings when accessible.

The broadcast format includes specific Alaska weather phenomena such as ice fog, blowing snow, and extreme temperature variations. Pilots operating in Alaska should be familiar with TWEB broadcast formats and understand how to interpret the specialized weather information relevant to Alaska flying conditions.

TWEB frequency information and coverage areas are published in Alaska aeronautical publications. Pilots should identify available TWEB facilities along planned routes and understand the coverage limitations of specific broadcast locations. The service provides valuable weather information but requires pilots to actively monitor broadcasts and interpret information relevant to their specific operations.

Weather service outlets represent the critical interface between weather data collection systems and flight operations. Through FSS services, TIBS recordings, HIWAS broadcasts, and Alaska TWEB operations, pilots have continuous access to weather information essential for safe flight operations. Understanding how to effectively utilize these services ensures pilots can make informed weather-related decisions throughout all phases of flight operations. The coordination between these various outlets provides comprehensive weather support for the diverse needs of general aviation, commercial operations, and specialized flight activities across the United States.

## WEATHER BRIEFING TYPES AND PROCEDURES

Prior to every flight, pilots must gather all information vital to the nature of the flight, with an appropriate weather briefing from a Flight Service Station specialist serving as the cornerstone of flight planning. The **briefing process** represents a critical safety procedure that transforms raw meteorological data into actionable flight information tailored to specific routes and aircraft operations.

Weather specialists require specific information to provide an appropriate briefing. Pilots must identify which type of briefing is needed and provide essential flight details including aircraft identification and type, departure point, estimated time of departure, flight altitude, route of flight, destination, and estimated time en route. This information is recorded in the flight plan system with a notation regarding the briefing type provided, creating a permanent record that can be referenced for flight plan amendments or overdue aircraft procedures.

### Standard Briefing Components and Sequence

A **standard briefing** provides the most complete weather information and comprehensive weather picture available. This briefing type should be obtained prior to departure of any flight and serves as the foundation of thorough flight planning. The standard briefing follows a specific sequence designed to present the most critical information first.

The briefing begins with **adverse conditions**, which includes information about significant weather that may influence a decision to cancel or alter the route of flight. Adverse conditions encompass thunderstorms, aircraft icing, turbulence, low ceilings, poor visibility, or other important factors such as airport closures. This information receives priority presentation because it directly impacts flight safety and go/no-go decisions.

When weather conditions warrant, specialists may state **"VFR flight not recommended."** This advisory is issued when the weather for the proposed route is below VFR minimums or when conditions make VFR flight doubtful due to forecast weather. While the final decision remains with the pilot-in-command, this advisory carries significant weight and should be carefully considered in the decision-making process.

> **Critical Safety Note**: The "VFR flight not recommended" advisory does not prohibit VFR flight but serves as a professional meteorological assessment of marginal conditions that could challenge pilot capabilities or aircraft limitations.

The **synopsis** provides an overview of the larger weather picture, describing fronts and major weather systems affecting the general area. This broad perspective helps pilots understand the meteorological environment and anticipate weather changes during flight.

**Current conditions** include present ceilings, visibility, winds, and temperatures at departure, en route, and destination airports. Current conditions are omitted from the briefing if the departure time exceeds 2 hours, as the information loses relevance for flight planning purposes.

The **en route forecast** summarizes expected weather conditions along the proposed route of flight, including ceiling heights, visibility restrictions, precipitation, and significant weather phenomena. This information enables pilots to anticipate conditions they will encounter during the flight.

**Destination forecast** provides expected weather conditions at the destination airport for the estimated time of arrival. This forecast proves critical for determining alternate airport requirements and approach planning considerations.

**Forecast winds and temperatures aloft** include wind direction and velocity at specific altitudes along the route of flight. Temperature information aloft is provided only upon specific request. These forecasts enable accurate flight planning calculations for groundspeed, fuel consumption, and flight time estimates.

### NOTAM Information and ATC Delay Advisories

**Notices to Airmen (NOTAMs)** contain information pertinent to the route of flight that has not been published in regular NOTAM publications. Published NOTAM information is provided only upon pilot request. NOTAMs may include runway closures, navigation aid outages, airspace restrictions, or airport operational changes affecting flight safety.

**ATC delays** represent any known air traffic control delays that may affect the proposed flight. These delays can result from weather conditions, traffic volume, equipment outages, or airspace restrictions. Knowledge of potential delays enables pilots to adjust departure times, fuel planning, and passenger expectations accordingly.

The briefing concludes with **other information**, including radio frequencies needed to open flight plans and contact En Route Flight Advisory Service (EFAS). Additional requested information is also provided during this segment, ensuring all pilot concerns receive attention.

### Abbreviated Briefing Applications and Requirements

An **abbreviated briefing** provides a shortened version of the standard briefing, requested when specific weather information updates are needed. This briefing type serves pilots who have received previous weather information but require updates due to departure delays or changing conditions.

Pilots requesting abbreviated briefings must inform the weather specialist of the time and source of their previous briefing. This information prevents inadvertent omission of critical weather data and ensures continuity in the briefing process. The specialist then focuses on significant changes or updates to previously provided information.

Abbreviated briefings prove particularly valuable when departure delays occur, allowing pilots to update their weather picture without receiving redundant information. The briefing concentrates on items that have changed since the previous briefing, including updated forecasts, new adverse conditions, or amended NOTAMs.

> **Best Practice**: Always request an abbreviated briefing when your departure is delayed more than 2 hours from your original estimated time of departure, as weather conditions and forecasts may have changed significantly.

The abbreviated briefing follows the same information priority as standard briefings, emphasizing adverse conditions and significant changes first. However, the specialist may omit information that remains unchanged from the previous briefing, provided the pilot acknowledges receiving that information earlier.

### Outlook Briefing Limitations and Follow-up Procedures

An **outlook briefing** should be requested when planned departure is 6 hours or more in the future. This briefing type provides initial forecast information with inherent limitations due to the extended time frame between briefing and departure.

Outlook briefings contain information based on weather trends and existing conditions in geographical areas at or near the departure airport. The briefing scope remains limited because weather forecast accuracy decreases with time, making detailed route-specific information less reliable for extended periods.

These briefings serve as excellent sources of preliminary flight planning information, influencing decisions regarding route selection, altitude planning, and initial go/no-go assessments. However, pilots must recognize that outlook briefings provide trend information rather than specific operational forecasts.

**Follow-up procedures** require pilots to obtain a standard or abbreviated briefing closer to departure time. Outlook briefings should never serve as the sole source of weather information for flight operations. The follow-up briefing ensures pilots have the most current and accurate weather information available prior to departure.

Weather conditions can change dramatically in 6-hour periods, making outlook briefing information potentially obsolete by flight time. Forecast confidence decreases significantly beyond 12-hour time frames, emphasizing the critical need for updated briefings.

> **Regulatory Consideration**: While outlook briefings satisfy initial planning requirements, they do not meet the regulatory obligation for current weather information prior to flight operations under 14 CFR Part 91.

### Required Pilot Information for Effective Briefings

Effective weather briefings require pilots to provide complete and accurate flight information to weather specialists. **Aircraft identification** enables specialists to correlate briefings with flight plans and provides reference for follow-up communications or overdue aircraft procedures.

**Aircraft type** information allows specialists to tailor briefings to specific aircraft capabilities and limitations. Different aircraft types have varying weather minimums, performance characteristics, and equipment capabilities that influence weather-related decisions.

**Departure point** identification enables specialists to provide current conditions and departure airport-specific information. This includes field conditions, density altitude considerations, and local weather phenomena affecting takeoff operations.

**Estimated time of departure** allows specialists to focus on weather information relevant to the intended flight period. Forecast timing becomes critical for flight planning, as weather systems move and evolve continuously.

**Intended route of flight** enables specialists to provide route-specific weather information rather than general area conditions. This focus improves briefing efficiency and provides more relevant information for decision-making.

**Flight altitude** information allows specialists to provide winds aloft forecasts and altitude-specific weather phenomena such as icing levels, turbulence, or cloud tops. Different altitudes may experience significantly different weather conditions along the same route.

**Destination airport** identification enables specialists to provide arrival weather forecasts and any destination-specific information affecting the flight. This includes alternate airport requirements and approach considerations.

**Estimated time en route** helps specialists focus on the appropriate time periods for en route and destination forecasts. Weather timing becomes critical for determining when conditions are expected to change along the route.

### Flight Plan Integration and Overdue Aircraft Procedures

Weather briefing information integrates directly with flight plan systems, creating permanent records of pilot weather awareness and briefing content. This integration serves multiple safety functions beyond basic flight planning requirements.

When pilots file flight plans following weather briefings, the system notes the briefing type and content provided. This information becomes available for reference when pilots request flight plan amendments or route changes due to weather conditions encountered during flight.

**Overdue aircraft procedures** rely heavily on weather briefing records to assist search and rescue operations. When aircraft become overdue, flight service specialists can review briefing records to understand the weather information available to pilots and the conditions they expected to encounter.

The briefing record provides search and rescue coordinators with critical information about pilot weather awareness, intended routes, and conditions that may have influenced flight path deviations. This information proves invaluable for determining probable aircraft locations and focusing search efforts.

Weather briefing documentation also supports accident investigation efforts by establishing pilot weather awareness and the information available for flight planning decisions. The National Transportation Safety Board frequently reviews briefing records to understand the meteorological context of aviation accidents.

Flight Service Stations maintain briefing records for specified periods, ensuring availability for operational and investigative purposes. These records form part of the comprehensive aviation safety system designed to support both normal operations and emergency response procedures.

The integration of weather briefings with flight planning systems exemplifies the systematic approach to aviation weather services, where individual components work together to enhance flight safety through improved pilot awareness and decision-making capabilities.

## AVIATION WEATHER REPORTS AND DECODING

Aviation weather reports provide pilots with accurate depictions of current weather conditions at specific locations and times. These reports serve as the foundation for flight planning decisions and represent real-time observations that complement forecasted weather information. Understanding how to decode and interpret these reports is essential for safe flight operations.

Each weather report follows standardized international formats that ensure consistency and accuracy across different reporting stations. The two primary types of aviation weather reports are **METARs** (routine surface observations) and **PIREPs** (pilot-reported conditions). Both provide critical information that cannot be obtained from any other source and are updated at regular intervals throughout the day.

### METAR Format, Elements, and Interpretation Procedures

The **Aviation Routine Weather Report (METAR)** represents the international standard for reporting surface weather observations. METARs provide comprehensive current weather information in a standardized format that pilots worldwide can decode and interpret consistently.

A typical METAR contains twelve sequential elements that describe current conditions at the reporting station. Each element appears in a specific order and uses internationally recognized abbreviations and codes. The format ensures that critical weather information is presented systematically, allowing pilots to quickly extract the information they need.

**ICAO station identifiers** form the foundation of the METAR system. In the contiguous United States, airports use a four-letter identifier that begins with "K" followed by the three-letter airport code. For example, Dallas/Fort Worth International Airport uses "KDFW." In Alaska, identifiers begin with "PA" (such as "PANC" for Anchorage), while Hawaiian airports use "PH" (such as "PHNL" for Honolulu).

**Time reporting in UTC** ensures global standardization. All METAR observations use **Coordinated Universal Time (UTC)**, also known as **Zulu time**. The time appears as a six-digit group where the first two digits represent the day of the month and the last four digits show the time in hours and minutes. A "Z" suffix confirms UTC usage. For example, "161753Z" indicates the 16th day of the month at 1753 UTC.

The complete METAR format follows this sequence: Type of report, Station identifier, Date/time, Modifier (if applicable), Wind, Visibility, Weather phenomena, Sky condition, Temperature/dew point, Altimeter setting, and Remarks.

**Wind reporting** uses a five-digit group (six digits if wind speed exceeds 99 knots) that shows direction and speed. The first three digits indicate the true direction from which wind is blowing in tens of degrees. The final two digits show wind speed in knots, followed by "KT." Variable wind direction appears as "VRB." Gusting winds include a "G" followed by the peak gust speed. For example, "14021G26KT" means wind from 140 degrees at 21 knots gusting to 26 knots.

**Visibility reporting** uses statute miles with the "SM" suffix. Fractions appear as needed, such as "3/4SM" for three-quarters of a mile visibility. When **Runway Visual Range (RVR)** is reported, it follows the visibility element and shows the distance a pilot can see down a specific runway from a moving aircraft.

**Weather phenomena coding** breaks down into qualifiers and phenomena. Intensity qualifiers include "-" for light, no symbol for moderate, and "+" for heavy. The proximity qualifier "VC" indicates phenomena within five to ten miles of the airport. Descriptors like "TS" (thunderstorms), "SH" (showers), and "FZ" (freezing) precede the actual weather phenomena.

**Sky condition reporting** follows a specific sequence of amount, height, and cloud type. Cloud base heights appear in hundreds of feet AGL using three-digit numbers. Coverage descriptors include "SKC" or "CLR" for clear, "FEW" for 1/8 to 2/8 coverage, "SCT" for scattered (3/8 to 4/8), "BKN" for broken (5/8 to 7/8), and "OVC" for overcast (8/8 coverage). Significant cloud types like cumulonimbus (CB) and towering cumulus (TCU) are specifically identified.

**Temperature and dew point formats** always use Celsius with temperatures below zero preceded by "M" for minus. The format shows temperature first, followed by a slash and the dew point. For example, "18/17" indicates 18°C temperature and 17°C dew point.

**Altimeter setting formats** appear as a four-digit group preceded by "A" and represent inches of mercury. The example "A2970" equals 29.70 inches of mercury. This information is critical for proper altimeter settings and pressure altitude calculations.

### SPECI Reports and Automated Station Indicators

**Special Aviation Weather Reports (SPECI)** supplement routine METARs when significant weather changes occur between scheduled observation times. SPECIs use the same format as METARs but are issued whenever rapidly changing conditions warrant immediate pilot notification.

SPECI reports are triggered by specific criteria including visibility changes of one-half mile or more when visibility is less than three miles, ceiling changes of 200 feet when the ceiling is below 1,000 feet, wind shifts of 45 degrees or more when winds are 10 knots or greater, and the beginning or ending of precipitation or thunderstorms.

**Automated station indicators** appear in the modifier section of METARs and SPECIs. The "AUTO" modifier indicates the report originates from an automated station without human oversight. Automated stations also include "AO1" or "AO2" in the remarks section to specify the type of precipitation sensors installed. "AO1" indicates stations without precipitation discriminators, while "AO2" denotes stations equipped to distinguish between liquid and frozen precipitation.

**Automated Weather Observing Systems (AWOS)** and **Automated Surface Observing Systems (ASOS)** provide continuous weather observations at airports without full-time weather observers. These systems measure and report standard meteorological parameters automatically, though they may have limitations in detecting certain weather phenomena compared to human observers.

The "COR" modifier appears when a corrected report replaces an earlier transmission containing errors. Corrected reports maintain the same time stamp as the original observation but include updated accurate information.

> **Important Note**: Automated stations cannot detect all weather phenomena that human observers can identify. Pilots should be aware of these limitations when interpreting automated weather reports, particularly regarding phenomena like freezing drizzle, ice pellets, or virga.

### PIREP Encoding, Filing Requirements, and Distribution

**Pilot Weather Reports (PIREPs)** provide the only real-time source of information about actual flight conditions, including turbulence, icing, cloud tops and bases, and other phenomena that cannot be observed from the ground. PIREPs bridge the gap between surface observations and actual flight conditions.

PIREPs are encoded using a standardized format with specific elements identified by forward slashes and abbreviations. The basic format includes: UA or UUA (routine or urgent), /OV (location), /TM (time), /FL (altitude), /TP (aircraft type), /SK (sky conditions), /WX (weather), /TA (temperature), /WV (wind), /TB (turbulence), /IC (icing), and /RM (remarks).

**Filing requirements** specify that elements 1 through 5 (type, location, time, altitude, and aircraft type) are mandatory, along with at least one weather element. Location references should use three-letter NAVAID identifiers with radial and distance information when appropriate. Time must be reported in four-digit UTC format.

The encoding system uses standard abbreviations and contractions. Aircraft types appear as four-digit maximum identifiers like "C172" or "B737." Sky conditions describe cloud coverage, bases, and tops using standard terms. Weather phenomena use the same coding as METARs, while temperature appears in Celsius with negative values preceded by "M."

**Turbulence reporting** uses standard intensity descriptors: NEG (none), LGT (light), LGT-MOD (light to moderate), MOD (moderate), MOD-SVR (moderate to severe), SVR (severe), and EXTRM (extreme). The type may be specified as CAT (clear air turbulence) or CHOP when appropriate.

**Icing reports** describe intensity and type using similar conventions: NEG (none), TRC (trace), LGT (light), MOD (moderate), and SVR (severe). Icing types include RIME, CLR (clear), and MXD (mixed). Altitude ranges should be included when different from the flight level element.

**Distribution systems** ensure PIREPs reach other pilots quickly. ATC facilities and Flight Service Stations add filed PIREPs to the distribution network for inclusion in weather briefings and advisories. PIREPs are also transmitted via **Hazardous Inflight Weather Advisory Service (HIWAS)** and included in area forecasts when significant.

### Weather Phenomena Coding and Intensity Qualifiers

Weather phenomena coding in aviation reports uses a systematic approach that combines intensity qualifiers, descriptors, and specific weather types. This standardized system ensures consistent interpretation of current weather conditions across all reporting stations.

**Intensity qualifiers** appear as the first element in weather groups. Light intensity uses the minus sign (-), moderate intensity requires no qualifier, and heavy intensity employs the plus sign (+). The proximity qualifier "VC" indicates weather phenomena occurring within five to ten miles of the reporting station but not at the station itself.

**Descriptors** modify precipitation and obscuration phenomena to provide additional detail. "MI" indicates shallow conditions, "BC" shows patches, "DR" means low drifting, and "BL" represents blowing conditions. "SH" identifies showers, "TS" indicates thunderstorms, "FZ" denotes freezing conditions, "PR" shows partial conditions.

Weather phenomena fall into three categories: precipitation, obscuration, and other phenomena. **Precipitation types** include "DZ" (drizzle), "RA" (rain), "SN" (snow), "SG" (snow grains), "IC" (ice crystals), "PL" (ice pellets), "GR" (hail), "GS" (small hail or snow pellets), and "UP" (unknown precipitation at automated stations).

**Obscuration phenomena** affect visibility and include "BR" (mist), "FG" (fog), "FU" (smoke), "DU" (dust), "SA" (sand), "HZ" (haze), "PY" (spray), and "VA" (volcanic ash). These phenomena significantly impact flight visibility and may require instrument flight conditions.

**Other phenomena** encompass various weather events including "PO" (dust or sand whirls), "SQ" (squalls), "FC" (funnel clouds), "SS" (sandstorms), and "DS" (dust storms). "+FC" specifically indicates tornadoes or waterspouts, representing extreme weather conditions.

The construction of weather groups follows the sequence: intensity/proximity, descriptor, precipitation/obscuration/other phenomena. For example, "+TSRA" indicates heavy thunderstorms with rain, while "-FZDZ" represents light freezing drizzle. "VCFG" shows fog in the vicinity of the airport.

**Mandatory PIREP solicitation by ATC facilities** occurs when ceiling is below 5,000 feet or visibility is at or below five miles. Controllers actively request PIREPs from aircraft in these conditions to provide current information to other pilots. When unexpected weather conditions are encountered, pilots are encouraged to provide PIREPs regardless of whether they are specifically requested.

PIREPs become especially valuable during rapidly changing weather conditions, as they provide real-time information that may not yet appear in routine surface observations. Urgent PIREPs (UUA) are used for reporting hazardous conditions that pose immediate threats to flight safety, including severe turbulence, severe icing, wind shear, or volcanic ash encounters.

The systematic approach to weather phenomena coding ensures that pilots receive precise, standardized information about current conditions. Understanding these codes and qualifiers enables pilots to make informed decisions about flight safety and helps them communicate effectively with ATC and other pilots about weather conditions they encounter.

## AVIATION FORECASTS AND PREDICTION METHODS

While current weather observations provide critical information about existing conditions, aviation forecasts enable pilots to plan flights and make informed decisions about future weather conditions. **Aviation forecasts** are meteorological predictions specifically designed for flight operations, providing essential information about expected weather phenomena that could affect flight safety and efficiency.

Modern forecasting relies heavily on sophisticated computer models, satellite data, and ground-based observations. **Meteorological prediction techniques** have advanced significantly, incorporating numerical weather prediction models that process vast amounts of atmospheric data to generate increasingly accurate forecasts. These computer models analyze current conditions and apply mathematical equations governing atmospheric physics to predict future weather patterns.

### Terminal Aerodrome Forecast (TAF) Format and Validity

The **Terminal Aerodrome Forecast (TAF)** represents the primary source of forecast weather information for specific airports. TAFs provide detailed forecasts for the five statute mile radius around an airport, making them essential for arrival, departure, and approach planning.

**TAF issuance schedules** follow a standardized pattern worldwide. TAFs are issued four times daily at 0000Z, 0600Z, 1200Z, and 1800Z. Most TAFs are valid for **24 hours**, though some major airports issue **30-hour TAFs**. The forecast period begins at the issuance time, providing continuous coverage throughout the day.

When significant weather changes occur that weren't anticipated in the routine forecast, **TAF amendments** (TAF AMD) are issued. These amendments update the forecast to reflect more current meteorological analysis and ensure pilots have the most accurate information available.

The TAF format follows a specific sequence that pilots must understand for proper interpretation:

**Type of Report and Station Identifier**: Each TAF begins with either "TAF" for routine forecasts or "TAF AMD" for amended forecasts, followed by the four-letter ICAO station identifier.

**Date and Time of Origin**: A six-digit group indicates when the forecast was prepared. The first two digits represent the day of the month, followed by four digits for the UTC time of preparation, concluded with "Z" to denote Zulu time.

**Valid Period**: The validity period uses an eight-digit format showing the forecast's effective timeframe. For example, "1212/1312" indicates validity from 1200Z on the 12th through 1200Z on the 13th. When the valid period ends at midnight UTC, it's annotated as "24" rather than "00."

**Forecast Elements**: TAFs include wind direction and speed (in the same format as METARs), visibility in statute miles, significant weather phenomena using standard METAR symbology, sky condition with cloud heights in hundreds of feet AGL, and optional wind shear information for the first 2,000 feet.

> **TAF Validity Periods**: 24-hour TAFs are standard, but 30-hour TAFs are issued for major airports to provide extended planning capability for long-haul flights and complex operations.

### Area Forecast (FA) Coverage and Regional Applications

**Area Forecasts (FA)** provide weather information for large geographical regions, typically covering multiple states. These forecasts are essential for en route flight planning and offer critical information for airports that don't have individual TAF coverage.

The continental United States is divided into **six FA regions**: Boston (BOS), Chicago (CHI), Dallas-Fort Worth (DFW), Kansas City (MKC), Miami (MIA), and Salt Lake City (SLC). Each region encompasses several states and their adjacent coastal waters, providing comprehensive coverage for cross-country flight planning.

**FA issuance and validity** follows a structured schedule. Area forecasts are issued three times daily and are valid for **18 hours**. The synopsis portion of the FA is valid for 12 hours, while the VFR clouds and weather section covers the first 12 hours with a 6-hour outlook period extending the total coverage to 18 hours.

**Regional applications** of area forecasts vary based on geographical and meteorological characteristics. The DFW FA region, for example, covers areas frequently affected by severe thunderstorms and strong low-level wind shear. The SLC region addresses mountain weather phenomena and high-altitude considerations unique to western terrain.

Area forecasts contain four main sections:

**Header Information**: Identifies the issuing office, issuance time, validity periods, and geographical coverage area.

**Precautionary Statements**: Alert users to hazardous conditions requiring additional information sources. The standard statement "TS IMPLY SEV OR GTR TURB SEV ICE LLWS AND IFR CONDS" reminds pilots that thunderstorms involve multiple hazards beyond precipitation.

**Synopsis**: Provides an overview of major weather systems, including the location and movement of pressure systems, fronts, and significant circulation patterns affecting the region.

**VFR Clouds and Weather**: Details expected sky conditions, visibility, and weather phenomena for sub-regions within the FA area, using geographical references and state boundaries for location identification.

> **Regional Coverage**: Each FA region contains detailed sub-regional breakdowns using geographical features like river valleys, mountain ranges, and coastal plains to provide location-specific forecasts.

### Forecast Change Groups and Probability Statements

Aviation forecasts use specific indicators to communicate expected changes in weather conditions and the confidence level of those predictions. **Forecast change groups** provide temporal information about when conditions are expected to change and how long those changes might persist.

**FM (From) Groups** indicate rapid, significant changes expected to occur within approximately one hour. When an FM group appears in a TAF, it means the weather elements following the FM completely replace all previous forecast conditions. For example, "FM1500 25015G25KT P6SM BKN040" indicates that from 1500Z, winds will shift to 250° at 15 knots gusting to 25 knots, with visibility greater than 6 statute miles and a broken ceiling at 4,000 feet.

**TEMPO (Temporary) Groups** describe temporary fluctuations in weather conditions expected to last less than one hour at a time, with total duration not exceeding half the period specified. These conditions may occur repeatedly during the indicated timeframe. TEMPO groups don't replace the prevailing forecast but indicate brief departures from it.

An example might be: "TEMPO 1400/1600 1SM TSRA OVC020CB" indicating that between 1400Z and 1600Z, temporary conditions of one statute mile visibility in thunderstorms and rain with an overcast ceiling at 2,000 feet with cumulonimbus clouds may occur.

**PROB30 and PROB40 Groups** express the probability of specific weather phenomena occurring. **PROB30** indicates a 30 percent probability, while **PROB40** represents a 40 percent chance. These probability groups are used exclusively for precipitation and thunderstorms and are not used for the first six hours of a TAF period.

Probability groups often combine with TEMPO to indicate both the likelihood and temporary nature of conditions: "PROB30 1800/2200 TEMPO 3SM TSRA BKN030CB" suggests a 30 percent chance of temporary thunderstorms between 1800Z and 2200Z.

**Conditional terms** like BECMG (becoming) indicate gradual changes expected to occur over a longer period, typically 2-4 hours. Unlike FM groups, BECMG indicates the change will happen gradually rather than rapidly.

The proper interpretation of these change indicators is crucial for flight planning. Pilots must understand that FM groups represent definitive changes, TEMPO indicates brief fluctuations from prevailing conditions, and probability statements reflect forecaster confidence levels in uncertain situations.

> **Change Group Priority**: When multiple change groups affect the same time period, FM groups take precedence as they represent complete condition changes, while TEMPO and PROB groups modify existing forecasts.

### Computer Modeling and Meteorological Prediction Techniques

Modern aviation weather forecasting relies heavily on sophisticated **numerical weather prediction (NWP) models** that process enormous amounts of atmospheric data to generate forecasts. These computer models form the foundation of contemporary meteorological prediction and directly influence the aviation forecasts pilots receive.

**Global Forecast System (GFS)** models provide worldwide weather predictions with resolution down to approximately 13 kilometers. The GFS runs four times daily (00Z, 06Z, 12Z, and 18Z) and produces forecasts extending up to 16 days, though accuracy decreases significantly beyond 7-10 days.

**North American Mesoscale (NAM) models** focus on higher resolution forecasting for North America, with grid spacing of approximately 12 kilometers. NAM models update four times daily and provide detailed forecasts for the first 84 hours, making them particularly valuable for aviation weather prediction.

**Rapid Refresh (RAP)** and **High-Resolution Rapid Refresh (HRRR)** models provide frequent updates specifically designed for aviation applications. The RAP model updates hourly with 13-kilometer resolution, while HRRR updates every 15 minutes with 3-kilometer resolution, offering exceptional detail for rapidly changing weather situations.

**Ensemble forecasting** techniques run multiple model iterations with slightly different initial conditions to assess forecast uncertainty. When ensemble members agree closely, forecasters have higher confidence in the prediction. Divergent ensemble solutions indicate lower confidence and higher uncertainty.

**Data assimilation** processes combine observations from multiple sources including radiosondes, aircraft reports (AMDAR/MDCRS), satellite imagery, and surface stations. This observational data constrains the mathematical models to reflect actual atmospheric conditions rather than purely theoretical calculations.

**Model post-processing** involves meteorologists interpreting raw model output and applying local knowledge to improve forecast accuracy. This human element remains crucial despite advancing automation, particularly for complex terrain effects and local climatological patterns.

**Statistical post-processing** techniques like Model Output Statistics (MOS) apply historical relationships between model forecasts and observed conditions to calibrate predictions for specific locations. MOS significantly improves temperature and precipitation forecasts at individual airports.

Aviation-specific model applications include **icing forecasts** that predict the likelihood and intensity of aircraft icing conditions, **turbulence algorithms** that identify areas of expected turbulence based on wind shear and atmospheric stability, and **convective parameters** that assess thunderstorm potential.

**Forecast verification** continuously compares predictions with actual observed conditions to identify model strengths and weaknesses. This verification process drives model improvements and helps meteorologists understand when and where specific models perform best.

The **temporal resolution** of different models varies based on their intended application. While global models may update every six hours, rapid-update models provide new forecasts every hour or more frequently, enabling meteorologists to track fast-moving weather systems that could affect aviation operations.

**Spatial resolution** determines how small-scale weather features can be resolved. High-resolution models can identify individual thunderstorm cells and local weather phenomena that larger-scale models might miss, providing critical information for tactical flight planning.

> **Model Limitations**: Despite sophisticated technology, all weather models contain inherent limitations and uncertainties. Pilots must understand that forecasts represent the most probable scenario based on current information, not absolute certainties about future conditions.

The integration of multiple model systems creates a comprehensive forecasting framework that supports aviation weather services. **VFR clouds and weather outlook procedures** specifically utilize these models to predict conditions affecting visual flight operations, considering factors like ceiling heights, visibility restrictions, and the development of instrument meteorological conditions.

Understanding the foundation of weather prediction helps pilots appreciate both the capabilities and limitations of aviation forecasts, enabling more informed decision-making throughout all phases of flight planning and execution.

## Summary

Review the key points from this unit:

- Aviation weather services operate through coordinated efforts of the NWS, FAA, DOD, and international organizations to provide comprehensive weather information for flight operations.
- The National Weather Service produces official weather forecasts while the FAA handles weather information dissemination and operates specialized aviation weather detection systems.
- International cooperation through WMO and ICAO ensures consistent global weather standards and enables worldwide data sharing critical for international aviation.
- Weather forecast accuracy varies significantly with time period and phenomena, with short-term forecasts (0-6 hours) achieving higher reliability than longer-term predictions.
- Surface weather observations are collected through manual stations, AWOS, and ASOS systems, each providing different levels of automated weather measurement capabilities.
- Manual weather observers provide detailed assessments that automated systems cannot capture, particularly for cloud types and complex weather phenomena.
- The surface observation network provides extensive but variable geographic coverage, with denser station spacing in populated areas and larger gaps in remote regions.
- Radiosonde balloon operations provide critical three-dimensional atmospheric data by ascending to 115,000 feet and transmitting real-time weather measurements.
- Pilot Weather Reports (PIREPs) are the only real-time source of actual flight conditions information, particularly for turbulence, icing, and cloud observations.
- Upper air weather observation methods include radiosondes, PIREPs, AMDAR systems, and MDCRS, each contributing unique capabilities to the comprehensive weather picture.
