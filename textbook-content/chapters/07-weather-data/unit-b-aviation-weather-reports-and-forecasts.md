# Unit B: Aviation Weather Reports and Forecasts

A pilot's ability to interpret weather data can mean the difference between a smooth flight and a potentially dangerous encounter with hazardous conditions. In aviation, weather information isn't just helpful—it's absolutely critical for making informed go/no-go decisions and planning safe routes. This unit will equip you with the essential skills to decode, interpret, and apply the various weather reports and forecasts that form the backbone of aviation weather services.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this unit, you will be able to:

- Identify the primary sources of aviation weather information and understand the structure of weather briefing services
- Execute proper weather briefing procedures and understand regulatory requirements for obtaining weather information
- Decode and interpret METAR reports to assess current weather conditions at airports
- Analyze Pilot Weather Reports (PIREPs) to gain real-time insights into flight conditions
- Interpret Terminal Aerodrome Forecasts (TAFs) to predict airport weather conditions for flight planning
- Evaluate area forecasts and regional weather products to understand broader meteorological patterns affecting your route
- Apply winds and temperatures aloft forecasts to optimize flight planning and fuel calculations
- Recognize and respond appropriately to AIRMETs and SIGMETs for hazardous weather conditions
- Utilize automated weather data systems effectively to gather current meteorological information

## FUNDAMENTALS OF AVIATION WEATHER SERVICES

Aviation weather service represents one of the most sophisticated information networks in modern aviation. The system combines efforts from multiple government agencies, private organizations, and individual observers worldwide to provide pilots with critical weather information for safe flight operations.

The foundation of this network rests on the collaboration between the **National Weather Service (NWS)**, **Federal Aviation Administration (FAA)**, **Department of Defense (DOD)**, and various aviation organizations. While weather forecasts cannot achieve 100 percent accuracy, meteorologists employ scientific study and computer modeling to predict weather patterns with increasing precision. This vast knowledge base translates into up-to-date reports and forecasts that enable pilots to make informed decisions regarding weather and flight safety.

> **Critical Point**: Weather service is a combined effort requiring multiple agencies and systems working together to provide comprehensive coverage across all flight levels and geographical areas.

### Weather Observation Network and Data Collection Systems

The aviation weather observation network spans the entire United States through a complex system of surface and upper-air observation stations. This network operates continuously, providing real-time data that forms the basis for all weather forecasts, advisories, and briefings.

**Surface observation stations** include both government-operated facilities and privately contracted stations that maintain continuous weather monitoring. These stations provide local weather conditions including wind, visibility, cloud cover, temperature, dewpoint, and barometric pressure readings. The network ensures comprehensive coverage across all major airports and many smaller facilities throughout the country.

**Automated Weather Observing Systems (AWOS)** and **Automated Surface Observing Systems (ASOS)** represent the backbone of surface weather observation. AWOS stations provide automated weather reports at airports without full-time weather observers, while ASOS stations offer more sophisticated measurements and are typically located at larger airports with higher traffic volumes.

> **AWOS vs ASOS**: AWOS provides basic automated weather observations, while ASOS includes precipitation discriminators and more detailed measurements. Both systems update continuously and transmit standardized weather reports.

**Upper-air observations** present greater challenges than surface measurements. The primary method involves **radiosonde balloon observations**, where small cubic instrumentation packages suspended below six-foot hydrogen or helium-filled balloons ascend at approximately 1,000 feet per minute. These balloons gather temperature, moisture, pressure, wind speed, and wind direction data up to altitudes of 115,000 feet.

Each year, over 75,000 radiosondes are launched nationwide. The balloons expand during ascent until reaching their elastic limits at diameters exceeding 20 feet, then burst and descend via parachute. Twenty percent of these instruments are recovered and reconditioned for reuse, with return instructions printed on each unit.

### Government and Private Weather Service Providers

The **National Weather Service (NWS)** serves as the primary government weather service provider, working in coordination with the FAA to ensure aviation weather needs are met. This partnership ensures that weather information meets both meteorological standards and aviation operational requirements.

Private weather service providers supplement government services by offering specialized aviation weather products, enhanced forecasting capabilities, and customized briefing services. These organizations often provide value-added services such as detailed route-specific forecasts, real-time weather updates, and specialized analysis for commercial aviation operations.

**Flight Service Stations (FSS)** represent the primary interface between pilots and weather services. FSS facilities provide preflight weather briefings 24 hours daily through the 1-800-WX BRIEF telephone service. These stations also offer inflight weather advisory services and accept pilot weather reports from aircraft within their areas of responsibility.

The **Hazardous Inflight Weather Advisory Service (HIWAS)** provides automated continuous broadcasts of hazardous weather information over selected VOR navigational aids. These broadcasts include AIRMETs, SIGMETs, convective SIGMETs, and urgent pilot reports. VOR stations equipped with HIWAS capability display an "H" in the upper right corner of their identification boxes on aeronautical charts [Figure 7-1: HIWAS Symbol - PHAK Ch 7, Fig 13-4].

### Types of Weather Observations: Surface, Upper Air, Radar, and Satellite

Aviation weather observations fall into four distinct categories, each providing unique and essential information for flight planning and safety.

**Surface observations** compile current weather elements at individual ground stations across the United States. These **METAR** reports include station identification, observation time, wind conditions, visibility, runway visual range when applicable, weather phenomena, sky conditions, temperature and dewpoint, altimeter settings, and relevant remarks. Surface observations provide detailed local conditions but cover only small geographical areas.

**Upper air observations** utilize multiple collection methods beyond radiosondes. **Pilot reports (PIREPs)** provide the only real-time source of information regarding turbulence, icing, and actual cloud heights as experienced by aircraft in flight. The **Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay (AMDAR)** program utilizes commercial aircraft equipped with automated sensors to provide approximately 220,000-230,000 weather observations daily worldwide.

The **Meteorological Data Collection and Reporting System (MDCRS)** represents the U.S. automated airborne weather observation program. More than 1,500 participating aircraft report wind and temperature data, with many also providing turbulence and humidity information. Data transmission occurs via the Aircraft Communications Addressing and Reporting System (ACARS) or through satellite links when aircraft operate beyond VHF range.

**Radar observations** employ four distinct radar types for weather detection:

1. **WSR-88D NEXRAD Doppler radar** operates in two modes: clear air mode with 10-minute update intervals for maximum sensitivity, and precipitation mode with 4-6 minute updates for active weather detection. Intensity measurements use dBZ (decibels of Z) scales with corresponding intensity terminology for ATC communication [Figure 7-2: NEXRAD Intensity Scale - PHAK Ch 7, Fig 13-2].

2. **Terminal Doppler Weather Radar (TDWR)** installations at major airports provide severe weather alerts and warnings to air traffic control, specifically detecting wind shear, gust fronts, and heavy precipitation affecting terminal operations.

3. **Airport Surveillance Radar** primarily detects aircraft but also identifies precipitation location and intensity for routing traffic around severe weather in airport environments.

4. **Airborne radar** carried by aircraft operates in C-band (6 GHz) or X-band (10 GHz) frequencies, allowing both penetration of heavy precipitation and reflection from lighter precipitation for thunderstorm detection.

**Satellite observations** have evolved to include commercial weather uplink services providing near real-time weather information for North American operations. Satellite-based systems offer comprehensive coverage including areas beyond ground-based radar range.

### Weather Service Outlets and Access Methods

Weather information reaches pilots through various outlets designed to meet different operational needs and flight phases.

**Flight Service Stations** remain the primary source for preflight weather briefings, accessible 24 hours daily through 1-800-WX BRIEF. FSS specialists provide three types of briefings: standard briefings for comprehensive weather information, abbreviated briefings for updates to previous briefings, and outlook briefings for flights departing six or more hours in the future.

**Telephone Information Briefing Service (TIBS)** provides automated recordings of meteorological and aeronautical information accessible only through touchtone phones. TIBS serves as a preliminary briefing tool but cannot replace specialist-provided briefings from FSS personnel.

**Transcribed Weather Broadcast (TWEB)**, available only in Alaska, provides continuous automated broadcasts over selected low/medium frequency and VHF omnidirectional range facilities. These broadcasts include adverse conditions, surface observations, PIREPs, density altitude statements, synopsis, winds aloft forecasts, and radar reports.

Modern aircraft increasingly feature **Electronic Flight Displays (EFD)** and **Multi-Function Displays (MFD)** with integrated weather services. These systems provide access to graphical NEXRAD data, METAR information, TAF reports, wind data, echo tops, lightning information, SIGMETs/AIRMETs, surface analysis, and temporary flight restrictions.

**Flight Information Service-Broadcast (FIS-B)** operates through the Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast network over 978 MHz UAT data links. FIS-B provides properly equipped aircraft with flightdeck displays of aviation weather and aeronautical information including METARs, TAFs, NEXRAD precipitation maps, NOTAMs, AIRMETs/SIGMETs, winds aloft, PIREPs, and temporary flight restrictions.

> **Important Limitation**: All data link weather products are for information only and do not meet safety and regulatory requirements for official weather products. Pilots must understand system capabilities, limitations, product latency, update cycles, and coverage areas for safe utilization.

The integration of these various weather service components creates a comprehensive network providing pilots with multiple sources of current and forecast weather information essential for safe flight operations.

## WEATHER BRIEFING PROCEDURES AND REQUIREMENTS

Obtaining an appropriate weather briefing is one of the most critical aspects of flight planning. The **Flight Service Station (FSS)** serves as the primary source for preflight weather information, available 24 hours a day by calling **1-800-WX-BRIEF** from anywhere in the United States and Puerto Rico. This standardized system ensures pilots receive comprehensive, accurate, and timely weather information essential for safe flight operations.

Weather briefings follow established procedures designed to provide pilots with all necessary information in a logical sequence. The effectiveness of a briefing depends on the pilot providing complete flight information and the specialist understanding the specific type of briefing required.

### Standard Briefing Components and Sequence

The **standard briefing** provides the most complete weather information available and should be obtained prior to any flight departure. This comprehensive briefing follows a specific sequence to ensure no critical information is omitted. Flight Service specialists are trained to provide information in the following order when applicable to the proposed route of flight.

**Adverse conditions** are presented first and include any weather phenomena that might influence the decision to cancel or alter the flight. This encompasses significant weather such as thunderstorms, aircraft icing, turbulence, or operational factors like airport closures. The briefer will highlight conditions that pose the greatest threat to flight safety.

When weather conditions are marginal, the specialist may issue a **"VFR flight not recommended"** advisory. This occurs when the route weather is below VFR minimums or when conditions are doubtful for VFR flight. While this advisory does not prohibit VFR flight, it serves as a strong cautionary statement that pilots must carefully consider before proceeding.

> **Critical Decision Point**: A "VFR flight not recommended" advisory should trigger serious reconsideration of the flight. This is not a regulatory prohibition but represents professional meteorological judgment that conditions may be unsuitable for safe VFR operations.

The **synopsis** provides an overview of the larger weather picture, including major fronts, pressure systems, and significant weather patterns affecting the general area. This "big picture" view helps pilots understand the overall meteorological environment and anticipate weather trends.

**Current conditions** include present ceilings, visibility, winds, and temperatures at departure and key points along the route. However, if departure time is more than two hours away, current conditions are typically omitted from the briefing since they will likely change significantly before departure.

The **en route forecast** summarizes expected weather conditions along the proposed route of flight, including anticipated changes in ceiling, visibility, precipitation, and winds. This information is crucial for determining if the flight can be completed under VFR conditions.

**Destination forecast** provides expected weather conditions at the destination airport for the estimated time of arrival (ETA). This includes forecast ceiling, visibility, winds, and any significant weather phenomena expected during the arrival timeframe.

### Abbreviated and Outlook Briefing Procedures

An **abbreviated briefing** represents a shortened version of the standard briefing and should be requested in specific circumstances. This briefing type is appropriate when departure has been delayed, when updating a previous briefing, or when only specific weather information is needed.

When requesting an abbreviated briefing, pilots must inform the specialist of the time and source of any previous briefing received. This ensures the briefer can focus on new or changed information without inadvertently omitting critical updates. The abbreviated briefing typically includes only those elements that have changed or are specifically requested by the pilot.

The **outlook briefing** should be requested when the planned departure is six hours or more in the future. This briefing provides initial forecast information that is necessarily limited due to the extended time frame. Outlook briefings contain information based on weather trends and existing conditions in geographical areas at or near the departure airport.

While outlook briefings are valuable for preliminary flight planning and go/no-go decision making, they should never replace a standard briefing obtained closer to departure time. Weather conditions can change significantly over a six-hour period, making updated information essential for safe flight operations.

### Required Information for Effective Briefings

For weather specialists to provide appropriate and comprehensive briefings, pilots must supply specific flight information. This includes the **type of briefing** needed (standard, abbreviated, or outlook), whether the flight will be conducted under **VFR or IFR**, and the **aircraft identification and type**.

Essential flight details include the **departure point**, **estimated time of departure (ETD)**, planned **flight altitude**, **route of flight**, **destination**, and **estimated time en route (ETE)**. This information is recorded in the flight plan system with notation of the briefing type provided, creating a permanent record that can be referenced if the aircraft becomes overdue or is reported missing.

**Forecast winds and temperatures aloft** are provided for the route of flight at specific altitudes. Temperature information aloft is typically provided only upon request. These wind forecasts are essential for calculating ground speed, fuel requirements, and flight time estimates.

**NOTAMs (Notices to Airmen)** include information pertinent to the route of flight that has not been published in standard NOTAM publications. Published NOTAM information is provided only when specifically requested by the pilot.

**ATC delays** that may affect the flight are included in the briefing when known. This information helps pilots plan departure times and fuel requirements more accurately.

### Telephone Information Briefing Service (TIBS) and HIWAS

The **Telephone Information Briefing Service (TIBS)** provides automated telephone recordings of meteorological and aeronautical information through the FSS system. TIBS offers area and route briefings, airspace procedures, and special announcements through automatically updated recordings as conditions change.

TIBS is designed as a preliminary briefing tool and cannot replace a standard briefing from an FSS specialist. The service requires a touch-tone phone for access, with phone numbers listed in the Chart Supplement U.S. This system allows pilots to obtain basic weather information quickly, particularly useful for initial flight planning or weather updates.

**Hazardous Inflight Weather Advisory Service (HIWAS)** provides continuous automated broadcasts of hazardous weather information over selected VOR navigational aids in the 48 contiguous states. These broadcasts include **AIRMETs**, **SIGMETs**, **Convective SIGMETs**, and urgent **PIREPs**.

HIWAS broadcasts are automatically updated as conditions change, providing real-time hazardous weather information to aircraft in flight. VORs equipped with HIWAS capability are identified on aeronautical charts with an "H" symbol in the upper right corner of the identification box [Figure 13-4: HIWAS symbol on sectional charts - PHAK Ch 7, Fig 13-4].

> **Navigation Note**: While HIWAS provides valuable hazardous weather information, pilots should contact FSS or En Route Flight Advisory Service (EFAS) for additional details or clarification of broadcast information.

### Briefing Documentation and Flight Plan Integration

All weather briefings are documented in the flight plan system, creating a permanent record that includes the type of briefing provided, flight details, and relevant weather information discussed. This documentation serves multiple purposes, including flight plan filing assistance, amendments to existing flight plans, and search and rescue coordination if needed.

The briefing record can be referenced later to file or amend a flight plan, ensuring consistency between the weather information received and the flight plan filed. When aircraft are overdue or reported missing, this documented information assists search and rescue coordinators in understanding the pilot's weather knowledge and planned route decisions.

At the conclusion of a standard briefing, FSS specialists provide **radio frequencies** needed to open the flight plan and contact **En Route Flight Advisory Service (EFAS)** for inflight weather updates. Any additional information requested by the pilot is also provided at this time.

**Alaska TWEB services** deserve special mention as **Transcribed Weather Broadcast (TWEB)** continues operation in Alaska only. This continuous automated broadcast transmits meteorological and aeronautical data over selected low/medium frequency (L/MF) and VHF omnidirectional range (VOR) facilities. Alaska TWEB broadcasts include adverse conditions, surface weather observations, PIREPs, and density altitude statements when applicable.

TWEB recordings may also include synopsis information, winds aloft forecasts, en route and terminal forecast data, and radar reports. At selected locations, telephone access to TWEB (TEL-TWEB) is available, with numbers listed in the Alaska Chart Supplement U.S. These broadcasts serve primarily for preflight and inflight planning but should not substitute for specialist-provided preflight briefings.

The integration of briefing information with flight planning requires pilots to analyze all received weather data in context of their aircraft capabilities, pilot experience, and regulatory requirements. Weather briefings provide the foundation for critical aeronautical decision-making, but the final determination of flight feasibility remains the pilot-in-command's responsibility.

Weather briefing procedures ensure standardized, comprehensive information transfer from meteorological specialists to pilots. Understanding these procedures and requirements enables pilots to obtain maximum benefit from available weather services, supporting safe and efficient flight operations under all weather conditions.

## METAR DECODING AND INTERPRETATION

**METAR (Meteorological Aerodrome Report)** represents the cornerstone of aviation weather observation, providing pilots with standardized, regularly updated surface weather conditions at airports worldwide. These reports follow an internationally recognized format established by the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO), ensuring consistent interpretation regardless of location. Understanding METAR decoding is essential for safe flight operations, as these reports provide critical information about current conditions that directly impact takeoff, landing, and flight planning decisions.

A **METAR** is issued on a regularly scheduled basis, typically every hour, unless significant weather changes warrant a **special METAR (SPECI)**. These observations are compiled from both automated weather systems and human observers, providing comprehensive surface weather data that forms the foundation for aviation weather services.

### METAR Format and Sequential Elements

METAR reports follow a strict sequential format that presents weather information in a standardized order. Each element occupies a specific position within the report, allowing pilots to quickly locate and interpret critical weather data. The standard METAR format includes twelve primary elements, each providing essential information about current surface conditions.

The sequential presentation ensures consistency across all reporting stations and eliminates confusion that could arise from varying formats. This standardization becomes particularly important when pilots operate in unfamiliar locations or under time-critical conditions where rapid weather assessment is essential.

Understanding the METAR sequence enables pilots to efficiently extract pertinent information without having to decode the entire report. For example, a pilot primarily concerned with visibility and ceiling conditions can immediately locate these elements in their designated positions within the report structure.

> **METAR Memory Aid**: Remember "Type-Station-Time-Modifier-Wind-Visibility-Weather-Sky-Temp-Altimeter-Remarks" to recall the standard sequence of METAR elements.

### Station Identifiers and Time Reporting (UTC/Zulu)

**ICAO station identifiers** consist of four-letter codes that uniquely identify each reporting location worldwide. In the contiguous United States, these identifiers begin with the letter "K" followed by the familiar three-letter airport code. For example, Dallas/Fort Worth International Airport uses the identifier "KDFW," where "K" designates the United States and "DFW" represents the specific airport.

Alaska station identifiers always begin with "PA," such as "PANC" for Ted Stevens Anchorage International Airport. Hawaii identifiers start with "PH," like "PHNL" for Daniel K. Inouye International Airport in Honolulu. Other regions worldwide use different two-letter prefixes to indicate country or regional designations.

The date and time element appears as a six-digit group immediately following the station identifier. The first two digits represent the day of the month, while the last four digits indicate the time in **Coordinated Universal Time (UTC)**. The time is always followed by the letter "Z" (Zulu), confirming UTC usage rather than local time.

For example, "161753Z" indicates the report was issued on the 16th day of the month at 1753 UTC. This standardized time reference eliminates confusion that would arise from multiple time zones, particularly important for flight planning across large geographical areas.

**Zulu time** (UTC) serves as aviation's universal time standard, placing all weather observations on a common reference regardless of local time zones. Pilots must become proficient in UTC conversion to properly correlate weather reports with flight planning times and regulatory requirements.

### Wind, Visibility, and Weather Phenomena Coding

Wind information appears as a five-digit group (or six digits when wind speeds exceed 99 knots) following any modifier elements. The first three digits indicate **true wind direction** in tens of degrees, while the last two digits represent **wind speed in knots**. When winds are variable in direction, the group shows "VRB" instead of a specific direction.

**Gust reporting** uses the letter "G" followed by the peak gust speed. For example, "14021G26KT" indicates winds from 140 degrees true at 21 knots, gusting to 26 knots. If wind direction varies more than 60 degrees with speeds greater than six knots, a separate group shows the directional extremes separated by "V" (e.g., "280V350").

**Visibility** is reported in statute miles using the designation "SM." Visibility appears as whole numbers or fractions, such as "3/4SM" for three-quarters statute mile visibility. When visibility is variable, the lesser value is reported in the body of the METAR, with remarks providing additional details about variations.

**Runway Visual Range (RVR)** may be reported following prevailing visibility when conditions warrant. RVR represents the horizontal distance a pilot can see down a specific runway from a moving aircraft. The format begins with "R" followed by the runway number, a slash, and the visual range in feet. For example, "R17L/1400FT" indicates 1,400 feet RVR on runway 17 Left.

Weather phenomena coding uses a systematic approach combining **intensity qualifiers**, **descriptors**, and **precipitation types**. Intensity is indicated by symbols: minus (-) for light, no symbol for moderate, and plus (+) for heavy. The descriptor "VC" indicates weather phenomena within five to ten miles of the airport.

**Descriptors** provide additional information about precipitation characteristics:
- **SH** (showers) indicates convective precipitation
- **TS** (thunderstorm) denotes thunderstorm activity
- **FZ** (freezing) indicates freezing precipitation
- **BL** (blowing) describes wind-raised particles

[Figure 7-5: Weather phenomena descriptors and precipitation types - PHAK Ch 13, Fig 13-5]

Precipitation types include **DZ** (drizzle), **RA** (rain), **SN** (snow), **GR** (hail), and **PL** (ice pellets). Obscuration phenomena use codes like **BR** (mist), **FG** (fog), **HZ** (haze), and **DU** (dust).

### Sky Condition and Cloud Reporting Procedures

Sky condition reporting describes cloud coverage in **eighths of the sky** from horizon to horizon, with specific contractions indicating coverage amounts. **SKC** or **CLR** indicates clear skies (less than 1/8 coverage), **FEW** represents 1/8 to 2/8 coverage, **SCT** (scattered) indicates 3/8 to 4/8 coverage, **BKN** (broken) represents 5/8 to 7/8 coverage, and **OVC** (overcast) indicates complete sky coverage (8/8).

Cloud heights are reported in **three-digit groups representing hundreds of feet above ground level (AGL)**. For example, "BKN008" indicates a broken cloud layer at 800 feet AGL. Multiple cloud layers are reported from lowest to highest, providing a complete vertical picture of sky conditions.

**Cumulonimbus (CB)** and **towering cumulus (TCU)** clouds receive special designation due to their significance for aviation operations. These cloud types are appended to the height group, such as "BKN015CB" indicating broken cumulonimbus clouds at 1,500 feet AGL.

When vertical visibility is restricted due to surface-based obscurations, the sky condition shows **"VV"** followed by the vertical visibility in hundreds of feet. For example, "VV003" indicates vertical visibility of 300 feet, typically associated with fog, snow, or other obscuring phenomena.

**Indefinite ceiling** conditions occur when cloud bases cannot be determined due to obscurations. These conditions significantly impact flight operations and require careful consideration during flight planning and approach operations.

### Temperature, Dew Point, and Altimeter Settings

Temperature and dew point values appear as a group separated by a slash, always reported in **degrees Celsius**. The format shows temperature first, followed by dew point (e.g., "18/17" indicates 18°C temperature and 17°C dew point). Temperatures below zero are preceded by "M" to indicate minus values.

The **temperature-dew point spread** provides valuable information about atmospheric moisture content and potential weather developments. A small spread (less than 3°C) indicates high relative humidity and potential for fog or low cloud formation. Pilots use this information to assess changing weather conditions and anticipate visibility restrictions.

**Altimeter settings** appear as a four-digit group preceded by "A," representing inches of mercury to the nearest hundredth. For example, "A2970" indicates an altimeter setting of 29.70 inches of mercury. This value is crucial for proper altitude reference and must be set in the aircraft's altimeter for accurate altitude readings.

Pressure tendency information may appear in the remarks section using **"PRESRR"** for rapidly rising pressure or **"PRESFR"** for rapidly falling pressure. These indicators help pilots anticipate weather changes, as rapid pressure changes often precede significant weather developments.

### Automated vs. Manual Observation Indicators

The modifier **"AUTO"** indicates the METAR originated from an automated weather observation system without human oversight. Automated stations use various sensors to measure weather parameters, but limitations exist in their ability to detect certain weather phenomena or unusual conditions.

**Automated Weather Observing System (AWOS)** and **Automated Surface Observing System (ASOS)** represent the primary automated observation networks. These systems provide continuous weather monitoring but may miss phenomena that require human observation or interpretation.

The remarks section includes **"AO1"** to indicate automated stations without precipitation discriminators (unable to distinguish between rain and snow) or **"AO2"** for stations equipped with precipitation discriminators. This information helps pilots understand the reliability and limitations of precipitation reporting at specific locations.

**Corrected reports** use the modifier "COR" to identify METARs issued to replace earlier reports containing errors. For example, "METAR KORD 161753Z COR" indicates a corrected observation. Pilots should ensure they reference the most recent corrected version to avoid basing decisions on erroneous data.

Manual observations may include phenomena that automated systems cannot detect, such as virga, distant lightning, or specific cloud types. Human observers can provide context and identify conditions that fall outside automated system parameters.

### Remarks Section Interpretation Including PRESFR/PRESRR

The **remarks section** begins with "RMK" and contains additional information that doesn't fit into standard METAR categories or provides amplification of reported conditions. This section may include wind data variations, visibility changes, weather phenomena timing, and equipment status information.

**Pressure tendency remarks** use standardized codes:
- **PRESFR** indicates pressure falling rapidly (0.06 inches of mercury or more in the past hour)
- **PRESRR** indicates pressure rising rapidly (0.06 inches of mercury or more in the past hour)

These rapid pressure changes often precede significant weather developments and provide valuable forecasting information for flight planning decisions.

**Automated station remarks** include maintenance indicators when equipment requires attention. Codes like "TSNO" indicate thunderstorm information is not available, while "RVRNO" means runway visual range information is unavailable. Understanding these limitations helps pilots assess the completeness of available weather data.

**Weather phenomena timing** appears in remarks using format "RAB15E32" (rain began at 15 minutes past the hour, ended at 32 minutes past the hour). This temporal information helps pilots understand weather trend developments and anticipate future conditions.

**Lightning information** uses codes like "LTG DSNT NE" (lightning distant northeast) or "OCNL LTGICCG" (occasional lightning in clouds and cloud-to-ground). This information proves valuable for thunderstorm avoidance and flight planning around convective activity.

**Variable visibility** appears in remarks when conditions fluctuate significantly. The format "VIS 1V3" indicates visibility varying between one and three statute miles, providing pilots with information about changing conditions that could impact approach and landing operations.

**Peak wind information** may be included showing maximum wind speeds recorded since the previous observation. The format "PK WND 28045/15" indicates peak wind from 280 degrees at 45 knots occurring at 15 minutes past the hour.

**Example METAR Interpretation:**

METAR KGGG 161753Z AUTO 14021G26KT 3/4SM +TSRA BR BKN008 OVC012CB 18/17 A2970 RMK PRESFR

**Translation:** Automated routine METAR for Gregg County Airport on the 16th day at 1753 UTC. Winds from 140 degrees at 21 knots gusting to 26 knots. Visibility three-quarters statute mile in heavy thunderstorms with rain and mist. Ceiling broken at 800 feet, overcast at 1,200 feet with cumulonimbus clouds present. Temperature 18°C, dew point 17°C. Altimeter setting 29.70 inches of mercury. Remarks indicate pressure falling rapidly.

This comprehensive METAR indicates severe weather conditions with thunderstorms, low visibility, low ceiling, and rapidly falling pressure. Such conditions would require careful consideration for any flight operations and likely indicate deteriorating weather patterns approaching the area.

## PILOT WEATHER REPORTS (PIREPS)

**Pilot weather reports (PIREPs)** represent the only real-time source of information available to aviation weather services regarding actual flight conditions aloft. Unlike ground-based observations and computer-generated forecasts, PIREPs provide first-hand accounts of turbulence, icing, cloud bases and tops, visibility, precipitation, and wind conditions as experienced by pilots in flight. These reports fill critical gaps in the weather observation network, particularly for conditions between the surface and flight levels that cannot be adequately measured by other means.

### PIREP Importance and Real-Time Weather Information

PIREPs serve as an essential complement to automated weather observations and forecasts. While METARs provide surface conditions and TAFs offer forecasts, only PIREPs can confirm actual flight conditions between reporting stations and at various altitudes. This real-time intelligence becomes particularly valuable when weather conditions are rapidly changing or when automated systems cannot detect certain phenomena.

The strategic importance of PIREPs extends beyond individual flight safety. These reports are immediately entered into the national weather distribution system, where they become available to Flight Service Stations, Air Traffic Control facilities, the National Weather Service, and other pilots planning flights in the same area. A single PIREP can influence route planning, altitude selection, and go/no-go decisions for multiple flights.

Weather forecasters rely heavily on PIREPs to verify and refine their predictions. When PIREPs consistently report conditions different from forecasted values, meteorologists can adjust subsequent forecasts and issue amended products. This feedback loop improves the overall accuracy of the aviation weather system.

> **Critical Safety Note**: PIREPs are the only source of real-time turbulence and icing reports. No ground-based system can adequately detect or forecast these hazardous conditions with the precision that pilot reports provide.

### Required Elements and Optional Reporting Items

All PIREPs must contain specific required elements to be useful for weather dissemination. The **required elements** include location, time, altitude, aircraft type, and at least one weather phenomenon. These elements ensure that other pilots can assess the relevance and reliability of the reported conditions.

**Required PIREP Elements:**
- Location (using three-letter NAVAID identifiers)
- Time (in UTC/Zulu format)
- Altitude or flight level
- Aircraft type
- At least one weather phenomenon (sky conditions, visibility, precipitation, turbulence, icing, temperature, or wind)

**Optional reporting items** enhance the value of PIREPs by providing additional context. These may include specific temperature readings, detailed wind information, flight visibility measurements, and descriptive remarks about weather intensity or duration. Pilots should include as much relevant detail as possible while maintaining concise, standardized terminology.

The location element requires particular attention to accuracy. Pilots must reference their position using established three-letter **NAVAID identifiers** rather than airport codes or geographic landmarks. This standardization ensures consistent interpretation and plotting of PIREP locations on weather charts and displays.

### PIREP Encoding and Transmission Procedures

PIREPs follow a standardized encoding format that facilitates rapid transmission and interpretation. The encoding system uses specific identifiers for each type of information, creating a structured format that weather personnel and pilots can quickly decode [Figure X-X: PIREP Encoding Format - PHAK Ch 13, Fig 13-7].

**Standard PIREP Format Elements:**
- **UA** (routine PIREP) or **UUA** (urgent PIREP)
- **/OV** - Location relative to NAVAID
- **/TM** - Time in UTC (four digits)
- **/FL** - Flight level or altitude in hundreds of feet
- **/TP** - Aircraft type (up to four characters)
- **/SK** - Sky cover and cloud information
- **/WX** - Weather phenomena
- **/TA** - Air temperature in Celsius
- **/WV** - Wind direction and velocity
- **/TB** - Turbulence intensity and type
- **/IC** - Icing intensity and type
- **/RM** - Additional remarks

Location reporting uses the format "/OV ABC" for positions at a NAVAID, or "/OV ABC 090025" for positions on a specific radial and distance from the NAVAID. Multiple waypoints can be referenced using "/OV ABC-DEF-GHI" to describe a route segment or area.

Pilots can transmit PIREPs through any Flight Service Station frequency, Air Traffic Control facility, or company radio frequency. The receiving facility is responsible for encoding the report into the standard format and entering it into the distribution system. Most PIREPs are disseminated within minutes of receipt, making them available to other pilots and weather facilities throughout the region.

### Turbulence, Icing, and Cloud Height Reporting

Accurate reporting of turbulence, icing, and cloud conditions requires pilots to use standardized terminology and intensity descriptors. This standardization ensures consistent interpretation of potentially hazardous conditions.

**Turbulence Reporting** uses four intensity levels:
- **Light** - Slight, erratic changes in altitude and attitude
- **Moderate** - Changes in altitude and attitude, but aircraft remains in positive control
- **Severe** - Large, abrupt changes in altitude and attitude; momentary loss of control
- **Extreme** - Aircraft violently tossed about, impossible to control

Turbulence reports should specify the type when known, such as **clear air turbulence (CAT)**, **mechanical turbulence**, or **convective turbulence**. The altitude range affected should be included if different from the flight level element.

**Icing Reports** describe both intensity and type:
- Intensity: **Trace**, **Light**, **Moderate**, or **Severe**
- Type: **Rime**, **Clear**, **Mixed**, or **Unknown**

Icing reports must include the altitude range where icing was encountered, even if different from the reported flight level. The rate of accumulation provides critical information for other pilots - trace icing barely affects flight characteristics, while severe icing can quickly overwhelm aircraft de-icing systems.

**Cloud Height Reporting** includes bases and tops in hundreds of feet above sea level. Pilots should report cloud coverage using standard terms: **Few** (1/8 to 2/8 coverage), **Scattered** (3/8 to 4/8), **Broken** (5/8 to 7/8), or **Overcast** (8/8 coverage). Cloud type should be specified when significant, particularly **Cumulonimbus** or **Towering Cumulus** clouds.

### ATC Solicitation Requirements and Pilot Responsibilities

Federal Aviation Regulations establish specific conditions under which Air Traffic Control facilities **must** solicit PIREPs from aircraft in their area of responsibility. These **mandatory solicitation conditions** occur when:
- Ceiling is below **5,000 feet AGL**
- Visibility is **5 statute miles or less**
- Thunderstorms or other hazardous weather conditions exist in the area

When ATC requests a PIREP, pilots operating under Instrument Flight Rules have a responsibility to comply unless the request would compromise flight safety. This regulatory requirement ensures systematic collection of weather information during marginal conditions when accurate reports become most critical for flight safety.

**Urgent PIREPs (UUA)** require special handling and immediate dissemination. Conditions warranting urgent classification include:
- Any degree of turbulence in clear air at or above FL 240
- Severe turbulence at any altitude
- Severe icing at any altitude
- Hail of any intensity
- Tornadoes, waterspouts, or funnel clouds
- Volcanic ash clouds

Beyond regulatory requirements, pilots have a professional responsibility to voluntarily report hazardous conditions encountered during flight. The aviation community's safety depends on pilots sharing information about unexpected weather phenomena, particularly when conditions differ significantly from forecasts.

Pilots should provide PIREPs when encountering conditions that would affect the safety or efficiency of other flights. This includes reporting both hazardous conditions and unexpectedly good conditions - a PIREP reporting smooth air and light winds can be as valuable as one reporting severe turbulence, depending on what other pilots expect based on forecasts.

> **Best Practice**: Experienced pilots often provide PIREPs routinely during flights, contributing to the overall weather picture even when conditions are normal. This practice helps establish baseline conditions and provides confidence to other pilots when forecasts appear questionable.

The timeliness of PIREP transmission directly affects their value to the aviation community. Weather conditions can change rapidly, particularly around convective activity or frontal passages. Pilots should transmit significant PIREPs as soon as practical after encountering the reported conditions, while the information remains current and relevant for following traffic.

## TERMINAL AERODROME FORECASTS (TAF)

**Terminal Aerodrome Forecasts (TAF)** represent one of the most critical weather products available to pilots for flight planning and operational decision-making. Unlike METARs that provide current conditions, TAFs offer forecast weather information for specific airports within a **five statute mile radius** of the airport reference point. These forecasts enable pilots to evaluate expected conditions at departure, destination, and alternate airports throughout their planned flight period.

TAFs are issued for larger airports that typically support commercial operations, military installations, and high-traffic general aviation facilities. The forecast coverage extends from the surface up to the airport's maximum operational altitude, providing comprehensive information about conditions that directly affect takeoff, landing, and airport operations.

### TAF Format, Validity Periods, and Issuance Times

The TAF follows a standardized international format similar to METAR reports but includes time-specific forecast elements and change indicators. Each TAF contains a systematic sequence of coded information that pilots must understand to extract meaningful operational data.

**Issuance Schedule**: TAFs are issued **four times daily** at **0000Z, 0600Z, 1200Z, and 1800Z**. This regular schedule ensures pilots have access to updated forecast information every six hours, allowing for comprehensive flight planning regardless of departure time. The issuance times align with the synoptic observation schedule used by meteorologists worldwide.

**Validity Periods**: Standard TAFs are valid for either **24 hours or 30 hours** depending on the specific airport and operational requirements. The 24-hour TAFs are most common for domestic operations, while 30-hour forecasts provide extended planning capability for longer flights and international operations.

The validity period coding follows a four-digit format representing the start and end times. For example, **0812/0912** indicates the TAF is valid from the 8th day of the month at 1200Z until the 9th day at 1200Z. When a forecast period begins at midnight UTC, it is coded as **00**, and when it ends at midnight UTC, it appears as **24**.

> **Critical Note**: TAF times are always expressed in UTC (Zulu time) and represent the forecast period, not the preparation time. Pilots must account for time zone differences when correlating TAF validity with local departure times.

### Forecast Elements: Wind, Visibility, Weather, and Sky Conditions

TAF forecast elements mirror METAR coding conventions but represent expected rather than observed conditions. Understanding these elements enables pilots to anticipate operational challenges and make informed go/no-go decisions.

**Wind Forecasts**: Wind information appears as a five-digit group (e.g., **15012KT**) where the first three digits indicate true wind direction in tens of degrees, and the last two digits represent wind speed in knots. Winds greater than 99 knots use six digits. Variable winds are coded as **VRB**, and gusting winds include the letter **G** followed by peak gust speed (e.g., **15012G18KT**).

Wind direction variations exceeding 60 degrees with speeds greater than six knots are shown with direction extremes separated by **V** (e.g., **180V240**). Calm winds or speeds less than five knots appear as **00000KT**.

**Visibility Forecasts**: Prevailing visibility is forecast in statute miles using whole numbers and fractions. The **P6SM** coding indicates visibility greater than six statute miles, which is significant for VFR operations since it represents unlimited visibility for practical flight purposes. Visibility less than six miles is coded with specific values such as **3SM**, **1 1/2SM**, or fractions like **1/2SM**.

**Weather Phenomena**: Forecast weather uses the same descriptors and abbreviations as METAR reports. **Qualifiers** indicate intensity (-, +, or no symbol for moderate), proximity (**VC** for vicinity), and descriptors (**TS** for thunderstorm, **SH** for showers, **FZ** for freezing). **Precipitation types** include **RA** (rain), **SN** (snow), **DZ** (drizzle), and **GR** (hail). **Obscurations** such as **BR** (mist), **FG** (fog), and **HZ** (haze) are forecast when expected to impact visibility.

**Sky Condition Forecasts**: Cloud coverage follows METAR conventions using **SKC/CLR** (clear), **FEW** (1/8 to 2/8 coverage), **SCT** (3/8 to 4/8 coverage), **BKN** (5/8 to 7/8 coverage), and **OVC** (overcast). Cloud heights are given in hundreds of feet AGL with three digits (e.g., **BKN025** indicates broken clouds at 2,500 feet AGL).

Only **cumulonimbus (CB)** clouds are specifically forecast in TAFs, identified by the **CB** suffix after the height (e.g., **SCT040CB**). This designation is critical since CB clouds indicate thunderstorm activity with associated turbulence, wind shear, and severe weather hazards.

### Change Group Indicators: FM, TEMPO, and PROB30

TAFs incorporate change indicators to specify when and how forecast conditions will differ from the prevailing forecast. These indicators help pilots understand timing and probability of weather changes affecting their operations.

**FM (From) Groups**: The **FM** indicator forecasts rapid, significant changes expected to occur within approximately one hour and persist. FM groups represent a complete replacement of all forecast elements after the specified time. The format includes **FM** followed by a four-digit time group (e.g., **FM1200** means from 1200Z onward).

When an FM group appears, all subsequent forecast elements apply until the next change group or end of the TAF validity period. For example: **FM0300 09008KT P6SM SKC** indicates that from 0300Z, winds will be from 090 degrees at 8 knots, visibility greater than 6 miles, and skies clear.

**TEMPO (Temporary) Groups**: **TEMPO** indicates temporary fluctuations in conditions expected to last less than one hour at a time and occur during less than half of the specified time period. The format shows **TEMPO** followed by a four-digit time period (e.g., **TEMPO 1214** indicates temporary conditions between 1200Z and 1400Z).

TEMPO conditions represent brief departures from the prevailing forecast that will return to the main forecast without additional change indicators. Example: **TEMPO 0204 1/2SM FG** forecasts temporary visibility reductions to 1/2 mile in fog between 0200Z and 0400Z.

**PROB30 Groups**: **PROB30** indicates a 30 percent probability of specific conditions occurring during the forecast period. This indicator is **not used for the first 6 hours** of the TAF validity period and typically forecasts thunderstorms and associated precipitation.

The format combines **PROB30** with a time period and the conditional forecast elements (e.g., **PROB30 1416 TSRA**). This coding means there is a 30% probability of thunderstorms with rain between 1400Z and 1600Z. PROB30 groups help pilots assess risk levels for marginal conditions that may or may not develop.

### TAF vs METAR Coding Differences

While TAFs and METARs share similar coding structures, several important differences distinguish forecast from observational data. Understanding these differences prevents misinterpretation and ensures proper use of each product.

**Time References**: METARs include observation time as a six-digit group (day and time), while TAFs show validity periods as four-digit groups (start time to end time). METAR times represent when conditions were observed; TAF times indicate when conditions are forecast to occur.

**Cloud Type Reporting**: METARs report both **towering cumulus (TCU)** and **cumulonimbus (CB)** clouds, but TAFs forecast only **CB** clouds. This difference reflects the greater significance of CB clouds for flight planning purposes and the difficulty of accurately forecasting TCU development timing.

**Detail Level**: METARs provide more detailed current information including remarks sections with supplementary data, while TAFs focus on operationally significant forecast elements. METARs may include runway visual range, present weather details, and equipment status information not found in TAFs.

**Change Documentation**: METARs can be corrected (**COR**) or issued as special reports (**SPECI**), while TAFs use amendment (**AMD**) procedures when significant changes to the forecast are required between scheduled issuances.

**Visibility Coding**: Both use **P6SM** for visibility greater than six miles, but TAFs more frequently employ this coding since exact visibility values are less critical for planning than for current operations.

### 24-Hour and 30-Hour Forecast Interpretation

Proper TAF interpretation requires understanding how forecast accuracy and detail vary with time periods. Meteorological science provides greater accuracy for shorter forecast periods, with decreasing reliability as the forecast extends further into the future.

**24-Hour TAF Structure**: Most domestic TAFs provide 24-hour forecasts with the highest detail and accuracy in the first 12 hours. The initial forecast period typically contains the most specific information about wind, visibility, weather, and sky conditions. Change groups become less precise for later periods as meteorological uncertainty increases.

**30-Hour Extended Forecasts**: Selected airports issue 30-hour TAFs to support long-range flight planning and international operations. These extended forecasts provide general trends and significant weather patterns but may lack the detailed change group information found in shorter forecasts.

**Forecast Confidence Levels**: Pilots should understand that forecast accuracy decreases with time. Conditions forecast for the first 6 hours generally show high accuracy, while forecasts beyond 18 hours provide trend information rather than precise timing of weather changes. Critical flight decisions should incorporate this accuracy gradient.

**Midnight UTC Notation**: Special attention is required when interpreting TAF periods that cross midnight UTC. The **24** notation indicates midnight of the following day, while **00** represents midnight of the current day. For example, a TAF issued at 1800Z on the 15th with validity period **1518/1618** is valid from 1800Z on the 15th to 1800Z on the 16th.

> **Operational Consideration**: When flight operations extend beyond 12 hours from TAF issuance, pilots should obtain updated forecasts or be prepared for conditions to differ from original forecasts. Longer flights require continuous weather monitoring and alternate planning.

**Interpretation Example**: 
TAF KORD 121720Z 121818/131818 25015G25KT P6SM SCT250 FM130200 28012KT P6SM BKN020 OVC040 TEMPO 1302/1306 4SM BR BKN008 FM131000 31008KT P6SM SCT040 BKN250

This Chicago O'Hare TAF issued on the 12th at 1720Z forecasts: from 1800Z on the 12th to 1800Z on the 13th, winds 250° at 15 knots gusting to 25, visibility greater than 6 miles, scattered clouds at 25,000 feet. From 0200Z on the 13th, winds shift to 280° at 12 knots with broken clouds at 2,000 feet and overcast at 4,000 feet. Temporarily between 0200Z and 0600Z on the 13th, visibility drops to 4 miles in mist with broken clouds at 800 feet. From 1000Z on the 13th, winds 310° at 8 knots, visibility greater than 6 miles, scattered at 4,000 feet and broken at 25,000 feet.

This comprehensive forecast enables pilots to plan fuel requirements for potential holding or alternate airports during the low visibility period, assess crosswind components during the wind shift, and anticipate ceiling restrictions for instrument approaches during the middle period of the forecast.

## AREA FORECASTS AND REGIONAL WEATHER

The **Area Forecast (FA)** provides a comprehensive picture of weather conditions and forecasts over large geographical regions encompassing multiple states. Unlike terminal forecasts that focus on individual airports, area forecasts give pilots essential en route weather information and coverage for smaller airports without dedicated terminal forecasts. Understanding FA format, coverage areas, and interpretation requirements is crucial for effective cross-country flight planning.

### Area Forecast (FA) Coverage and Validity Periods

The contiguous 48 states are divided into **six distinct area forecast regions** for weather forecasting purposes. These regions include Boston (BOS), Miami (MIA), Chicago (CHI), Dallas/Fort Worth (DFW), Salt Lake City (SLC), and San Francisco (SFO). Each region covers multiple states and their adjacent coastal waters, providing comprehensive weather coverage across the entire continental United States.

[Figure 7-6: Area Forecast Regional Coverage Map - PHAK Ch 7, Fig 7-6]

Area forecasts are issued **three times daily** at 0245Z, 0845Z, and 1445Z. Each FA has an **18-hour validity period** with forecasts extending from the time of issuance. Additionally, each area forecast includes an **outlook period** covering an additional 6 hours beyond the initial 18-hour forecast, providing a total weather picture spanning 24 hours from issuance.

> **Critical Timing**: The 18-hour validity period covers detailed weather forecasts, while the 6-hour outlook provides general weather trends and conditions expected beyond the primary forecast period.

The FA serves as a vital planning tool for flights covering extensive geographical areas. Pilots conducting long cross-country flights often traverse multiple weather systems and terrain features that significantly influence local weather patterns. The area forecast addresses these concerns by providing synopsis information about major weather systems and their expected movement across the forecast region.

### Header Information and Regional Boundaries

Area forecast headers contain essential identification and validity information that pilots must understand for proper interpretation. The header format follows a standardized sequence beginning with the issuing office identifier, date and time of issuance, and the geographical coverage area.

A typical header appears as: **DFWC FA 120945 SYNOPSIS AND VFR CLDS/WX SYNOPSIS VALID UNTIL 130400 CLDS/WX VALID UNTIL 122200...OTLK VALID 122200-130400 OK TX AR LA MS AL AND CSTL WTRS**

This header indicates the Dallas/Fort Worth Center issued the area forecast on the 12th day of the month at 0945Z. The synopsis section remains valid until 0400Z on the 13th, while VFR clouds and weather forecasts are valid until 2200Z on the 12th. The outlook period extends from 2200Z on the 12th through 0400Z on the 13th, covering Oklahoma, Texas, Arkansas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, and coastal waters.

Regional boundaries within each area forecast region are defined by **state abbreviations** and **geographical features**. Common geographical references include mountain ranges, river valleys, coastal areas, and major metropolitan regions. The FA uses standardized abbreviations such as "CNTRL" for central, "NRN" for northern, "SRN" for southern, and "EXTRM" for extreme when describing specific portions of states or regions.

Understanding these regional designations is essential because weather conditions can vary significantly across large states. For example, northern Texas may experience different weather patterns than the Gulf coastal regions of the same state. The FA accounts for these variations by providing specific forecasts for different geographical subdivisions within each state.

### Synopsis and Pressure System Movement

The **synopsis section** provides a comprehensive overview of major weather systems affecting the forecast region. This section identifies the location, intensity, and expected movement of pressure systems, fronts, and significant weather patterns that influence regional weather conditions.

Synopsis information typically describes **low pressure systems**, **high pressure areas**, **cold fronts**, **warm fronts**, **occluded fronts**, and **troughs**. The synopsis uses directional indicators and movement speeds to describe how these systems will progress during the forecast period. Understanding pressure system movement is crucial for anticipating weather changes along planned routes of flight.

A sample synopsis might read: **"LOW PRES TROF 10Z OK/TX PNHDL AREA FCST MOV EWD INTO CNTRL-SWRN OK BY 04Z. WRMFNT 10Z CNTRL OK-SRN AR-NRN MS FCST LIFT NWD INTO NERN OK-NRN AR EXTRM NRN MS BY 04Z."**

This synopsis indicates that at 1000Z, a low pressure trough over the Oklahoma and Texas panhandle area is forecast to move eastward into central to southwestern Oklahoma by 0400Z. A warm front located over central Oklahoma, southern Arkansas, and northern Mississippi at 1000Z is forecast to lift northwestward into northeastern Oklahoma, northern Arkansas, and extreme northern Mississippi by 0400Z.

Pilots use synopsis information to understand the **"big picture"** weather scenario. Knowing that a cold front is approaching from the west helps pilots anticipate deteriorating conditions, potential thunderstorm development, and changing wind patterns. Similarly, understanding high pressure system locations helps identify areas of generally stable weather conditions.

> **Weather System Movement**: Most weather systems in the continental United States move from west to east at speeds typically ranging from 15 to 35 knots, though speeds can vary significantly based on upper-level wind patterns.

### VFR Clouds and Weather Forecasting

The VFR clouds and weather section forms the primary operational portion of the area forecast. This section provides detailed information about **sky conditions**, **visibility**, **weather phenomena**, and **cloud heights** expected during the forecast period. The information is organized geographically by states or regional subdivisions within the forecast area.

Cloud information includes coverage descriptions using standard contractions: **SCT (scattered)**, **BKN (broken)**, **OVC (overcast)**, and **FEW (few)**. Cloud heights in the FA are referenced to **Above Ground Level (AGL)** unless specifically noted otherwise. This AGL referencing is critical for flight planning because pilots need to know cloud heights relative to terrain and airport elevations.

Visibility forecasts use **statute mile** measurements and include the causes of reduced visibility such as **fog (FG)**, **mist (BR)**, **haze (HZ)**, or **precipitation**. Weather phenomena are coded using the same abbreviations found in METAR reports, including **rain (RA)**, **snow (SN)**, **thunderstorms (TS)**, and various intensities indicated by **light (-)**, **moderate (no symbol)**, or **heavy (+)** indicators.

A typical VFR clouds and weather entry might read: **"S CNTRL AND SERN TX AGL SCT-BKN010. TOPS 030. VIS 3-5SM BR. 14-16Z BECMG AGL SCT030. 19Z AGL SCT050."**

This entry indicates that in south central and southeastern Texas, scattered to broken clouds are forecast from 1,000 feet AGL with tops at 3,000 feet. Visibility is expected to be 3 to 5 statute miles in mist. Between 1400Z and 1600Z, conditions are forecast to improve with scattered clouds at 3,000 feet AGL. After 1900Z, cloud bases are expected to lift to 5,000 feet AGL.

**Time-specific changes** within the forecast period are indicated by specific time groups such as "14-16Z BECMG" (becoming between 1400Z and 1600Z) or "AFT 19Z" (after 1900Z). These time indicators help pilots understand when conditions are expected to change and plan accordingly.

### Precautionary Statements and Height References

Area forecasts contain important **precautionary statements** that alert pilots to significant weather hazards and clarify height referencing systems used throughout the forecast. These statements appear near the beginning of the FA and provide essential interpretive guidance for safe use of the forecast information.

The standard precautionary statement reads: **"SEE AIRMET SIERRA FOR IFR CONDS AND MTN OBSCN. TS IMPLY SEV OR GTR TURB SEV ICE LLWS AND IFR CONDS. NON MSL HGTS DENOTED BY AGL OR CIG."**

This statement provides three critical pieces of information. First, pilots must reference **AIRMET Sierra** for IFR conditions and mountain obscuration details, as the FA focuses primarily on VFR conditions. Second, any thunderstorm reference implies the possibility of **severe or greater turbulence**, **severe icing**, **low-level wind shear**, and **IFR conditions**. Third, the statement clarifies the height referencing system used throughout the forecast.

**Height references** in area forecasts use **Mean Sea Level (MSL)** as the standard reference unless specifically noted otherwise. When heights are referenced to **Above Ground Level (AGL)** or represent **ceilings (CIG)**, these designators are explicitly stated. This distinction is crucial because terrain elevation varies significantly across forecast regions, and pilots must understand whether forecast heights account for terrain elevation.

For example, a cloud forecast of "BKN050" indicates broken clouds at 5,000 feet MSL. In an area where terrain elevation is 2,000 feet MSL, these clouds would appear at approximately 3,000 feet AGL. Conversely, "AGL BKN050" indicates broken clouds at 5,000 feet above the local terrain elevation.

> **Height Reference Critical Point**: Always verify whether cloud heights are MSL or AGL before making go/no-go decisions, especially when flying in mountainous terrain where the distinction significantly affects flight safety.

The **outlook period** extends the forecast an additional 6 hours beyond the primary 18-hour forecast period. Outlook information provides general weather trend information but with less detail than the primary forecast period. Outlook entries typically indicate whether conditions are expected to be **VFR**, **MVFR**, or **IFR** without detailed cloud height or visibility specifics.

A typical outlook statement appears as **"OTLK...VFR"** indicating that VFR conditions are expected to prevail during the outlook period. More detailed outlook information might specify expected changes such as **"OTLK...VFR XCPT MVFR CIGS/VIS FG BKMT"** (VFR except marginal VFR ceilings and visibility in fog before morning).

Understanding area forecast interpretation enables pilots to effectively plan cross-country flights by providing essential weather information for route planning, alternate airport selection, and fuel planning considerations. The FA serves as a foundational weather planning document that, when combined with terminal forecasts, current observations, and pilot reports, provides the comprehensive weather picture necessary for safe flight operations.

## WINDS AND TEMPERATURES ALOFT FORECASTS

**Winds and temperatures aloft forecasts (FB)** provide essential upper-air weather information for flight planning and en route operations. These forecasts deliver wind direction, wind speed, and temperature data at specific altitude levels throughout the United States, including network locations in Hawaii and Alaska. The FB reports are critical for calculating true airspeed, ground speed, drift correction, and fuel planning for flights above the surface level.

### Winds Aloft Forecast Format and Altitude Levels

The winds and temperatures aloft forecast follows a standardized format that presents data in columns by altitude and rows by location. **Standard altitude levels** are forecast for 3,000, 6,000, 9,000, 12,000, 18,000, 24,000, 30,000, 34,000, and 39,000 feet. Altitudes through 12,000 feet are referenced as **true altitudes** above mean sea level (MSL), while altitudes of 18,000 feet and above are classified as **flight levels**.

The forecast data is arranged in a tabular format with station identifiers listed vertically on the left side and altitude levels displayed horizontally across the top. Each data group within the table represents the wind and temperature conditions forecast for that specific location and altitude combination.

> **Important**: Wind direction in FB forecasts is always referenced to **true north**, not magnetic north. This differs from surface wind reports and requires pilots to apply magnetic variation corrections when using the data for navigation purposes.

**Data groups** are presented in either four-digit or six-digit formats. A four-digit group (such as "2714") indicates wind direction and speed only, while a six-digit group (such as "2725+00") includes temperature information. The first two digits represent wind direction in tens of degrees from true north, the next two digits show wind speed in knots, and when present, the final two digits indicate temperature in degrees Celsius.

### Temperature Reporting Procedures and Omissions

Temperature information follows specific reporting procedures and omission rules within the FB format. **Temperature omission** occurs when a forecast location is within 2,500 feet of the station elevation, as temperature variations at these levels are considered minimal and unreliable for forecast purposes.

When temperatures are reported, they appear as the final two digits in a six-digit data group. **Positive temperatures** are preceded by a plus sign (+) or no sign, while **negative temperatures** are preceded by a minus sign (-). For altitudes above 24,000 feet (FL240), all temperatures are negative, so the minus sign is omitted from the forecast to simplify the format. The forecast header will indicate "TEMPS NEGATIVE ABV 24000" to clarify this convention.

**Temperature accuracy** decreases significantly at lower altitudes due to terrain effects, solar heating, and local weather influences. This explains why temperatures are not forecast for stations within 2,500 feet AGL of the reporting location. Pilots should use current METAR reports and observed conditions for temperature information at these lower levels.

Surface heating and cooling effects can cause substantial temperature variations that cannot be accurately predicted in upper-air forecasts. The 2,500-foot AGL threshold ensures that temperature forecasts represent free-atmosphere conditions rather than surface-influenced measurements.

### Forecast Validity Periods and Update Schedules

Winds and temperatures aloft forecasts are issued **twice daily** based on radiosonde upper-air observations taken at 0000Z and 1200Z. The 0000Z observation generates forecasts valid for use from approximately 0600Z to 1500Z, while the 1200Z observation produces forecasts valid from approximately 1800Z to 0300Z of the following day.

**Forecast validity periods** are clearly indicated in the FB header information. A typical header might read "DATA BASED ON 151200Z VALID 151800Z FOR USE 1400-2100Z," indicating the observation time, valid time, and recommended use period. The forecast remains most accurate during the first 6-12 hours after issuance, with accuracy decreasing over time.

**Update schedules** follow a consistent pattern, with new forecasts transmitted approximately 4-6 hours after the upper-air observation time. This allows sufficient time for data collection, quality control, and forecast model processing. Pilots should always obtain the most recent FB forecast available for flight planning purposes.

The **12-hour forecast validity** means that winds and temperatures can change significantly between forecast periods, especially during periods of active weather systems or strong pressure gradient changes. Pilots conducting flights that span multiple forecast periods should monitor updated forecasts and be prepared for changing conditions.

### Light and Variable Wind Coding Conventions

**Light and variable wind conditions** are encoded using specific conventions in the FB format. When forecast wind speeds are less than 5 knots, the data group appears as "9900," indicating **light and variable** conditions. This coding tells pilots that wind direction will be inconsistent and wind speeds will be minimal at that altitude and location.

**Variable wind direction** situations occur when upper-air flow patterns are weak or when locations are positioned between pressure systems. The 9900 code warns pilots that calculated ground speeds and drift corrections may be unreliable, and that actual conditions may differ significantly from forecast assumptions.

**High wind speed encoding** requires special attention when winds exceed 99 knots. For winds between 100-199 knots, 50 is added to the direction and 100 is subtracted from the speed. For example, a wind from 270° at 119 knots would be encoded as "771960" (27+50=77, 119-100=19). To decode, subtract 50 from the direction (77-50=27) and add 100 to the speed (19+100=119).

When forecast winds reach 200 knots or greater, the maximum encoding of "7799" is used, representing winds of 199 knots or greater from the indicated direction. Such extreme wind conditions are typically associated with jet stream cores and should prompt careful flight planning considerations.

> **Decoding Example**: A data group of "731945" decodes as: 73-50=23 (230°), 19+100=119 knots, -45°C. This represents winds from 230° true at 119 knots with a temperature of minus 45 degrees Celsius.

**No wind reporting** occurs when a forecast altitude is within 1,500 feet of the station elevation. This prevents inaccurate wind forecasts that would be heavily influenced by terrain effects rather than representing true upper-air flow patterns. The data group will be omitted entirely for these altitude/location combinations.

Pilots must understand that winds aloft forecasts represent **broad-scale atmospheric flow** and may not accurately reflect local wind conditions influenced by terrain, thermal activity, or microscale weather phenomena. The forecasts provide excellent guidance for flight planning but should be supplemented with pilot reports, current observations, and real-time assessments during flight operations.

**Forecast limitations** include decreased accuracy during rapidly changing weather conditions, reduced reliability in mountainous terrain, and potential errors during periods of strong convective activity. Pilots should always compare FB forecasts with current pilot reports and surface observations to verify forecast accuracy and identify significant deviations from predicted conditions.

## AIRMETS AND SIGMETS - HAZARDOUS WEATHER ADVISORIES

Aviation weather hazards pose significant risks to flight safety, making it essential for pilots to understand and interpret hazardous weather advisories. **AIRMETs** (Airman's Meteorological Information) and **SIGMETs** (Significant Meteorological Information) provide critical information about weather conditions that can adversely affect aircraft operations. These advisories serve as early warning systems, allowing pilots to make informed decisions about route planning, altitude selection, and flight continuation.

The Federal Aviation Administration and National Weather Service issue these advisories to alert pilots of potentially dangerous weather phenomena. Understanding the criteria, format, and validity periods of each advisory type is crucial for safe flight operations.

### AIRMET Categories: Sierra, Tango, and Zulu

AIRMETs are issued for weather conditions that may be hazardous to small aircraft and aircraft with limited operational capabilities. These advisories are categorized into three distinct types, each identified by a specific phonetic alphabet designator.

**AIRMET Sierra** addresses **IFR conditions** and **mountain obscuration**. This advisory is issued when widespread areas experience ceilings below 1,000 feet AGL and/or surface visibility less than 3 statute miles. Mountain obscuration occurs when mountains become hidden by clouds, precipitation, fog, or other weather phenomena, creating hazardous conditions for VFR flight in mountainous terrain.

**AIRMET Tango** covers **turbulence**, **strong surface winds**, and **low-level wind shear**. This advisory is issued for moderate turbulence, sustained surface winds of 30 knots or greater, and non-convective low-level wind shear conditions. Wind shear represents a particular hazard during takeoff and landing phases of flight.

**AIRMET Zulu** provides information about **icing conditions** and **freezing level** data. This advisory addresses moderate icing conditions and includes freezing level information critical for flight planning. Icing conditions pose significant threats to aircraft not equipped with anti-icing or de-icing systems.

> **Important Note**: AIRMETs use fixed alphanumeric designators that are numbered sequentially for easy identification, beginning with the first issuance of each day.

### SIGMET Criteria and Issuance Procedures

SIGMETs address weather phenomena that are potentially hazardous to all aircraft, regardless of size or operational capability. The **criteria for SIGMET issuance** include severe or extreme turbulence, severe icing not associated with thunderstorms, widespread dust storms or sandstorms reducing visibility below 3 statute miles, and volcanic ash.

**Severe icing** conditions warrant SIGMET issuance when aircraft encounter icing rates that exceed the capability of most anti-icing and de-icing systems. **Extreme turbulence** represents conditions where aircraft are violently tossed about and practically impossible to control, while **severe turbulence** causes large, abrupt changes in altitude and attitude.

**Dust storms** and **sandstorms** that reduce surface or in-flight visibility below 3 statute miles across areas of 3,000 square miles or greater trigger SIGMET issuance. **Volcanic ash** presents unique hazards to aircraft engines and flight surfaces, requiring immediate pilot awareness and avoidance.

SIGMETs are issued under alphabetic identifiers from November through Yankee. The first issuance of a SIGMET for a particular weather phenomenon is designated as an **Urgent Weather SIGMET (UWS)**. Subsequent reissues for the same weather phenomenon are numbered sequentially until the hazardous conditions end.

### Convective SIGMET Requirements and Coverage

**Convective SIGMETs** address hazardous convective weather affecting flight safety. These advisories are issued for **severe thunderstorms** with surface winds exceeding 50 knots, **hail** at the surface greater than or equal to ¾ inch in diameter, or **tornadoes**.

Additional criteria include **embedded thunderstorms**, **lines of thunderstorms**, and thunderstorms producing **heavy or greater precipitation** affecting 40 percent or more of a 3,000 square mile area. Embedded thunderstorms are particularly dangerous because they are obscured by other clouds and may not be visible to pilots.

The contiguous United States is divided into three **Convective SIGMET regions**: **Eastern (E)**, **Central (C)**, and **Western (W)**. This regional approach ensures comprehensive coverage and allows for more localized and specific convective weather warnings.

> **Critical Timing**: Convective SIGMETs are issued at 55 minutes past each hour, with special issuances available as needed between regular times.

### Advisory Validity Periods and Update Schedules

Understanding validity periods is crucial for proper use of weather advisories in flight planning and operations. **AIRMETs** are issued every **6 hours** with intermediate updates as conditions warrant. These regular issuances ensure pilots have current information about evolving weather conditions.

**SIGMETs** have a **4-hour validity period** for most weather phenomena. However, **hurricane-related SIGMETs** are valid for **6 hours** due to the extended nature and predictable movement of tropical systems. This extended validity period allows for more comprehensive flight planning around major storm systems.

**Convective SIGMETs** have the shortest validity period at **2 hours**, reflecting the rapid development and movement of convective weather. The short validity period ensures pilots receive the most current information about fast-changing thunderstorm activity.

Each advisory type follows specific update schedules. AIRMETs follow the Area Forecast regions and are updated as weather conditions change. SIGMETs are issued as needed when hazardous conditions develop or are forecast to develop. Convective SIGMETs follow a regular hourly schedule with special issuances between regular times when rapidly developing convective situations warrant immediate pilot notification.

### Geographic Coverage and Alphanumeric Designators

The geographic coverage system for weather advisories ensures systematic organization and easy identification. **Area Forecast regions** determine AIRMET coverage areas, with each region covering multiple states and geographic features. These regions include the Northeast, Southeast, North Central, South Central, Rocky Mountain, and Pacific Coast areas.

**AIRMET designators** follow a specific naming convention. Each bulletin uses fixed alphanumeric designators numbered sequentially from the first issuance of the day. For example, "AIRMET SIERRA UPDT 2" indicates the second updated issuance of Sierra-type conditions for that day.

**SIGMET geographic coverage** spans the entire contiguous United States, with specific boundaries defined for each advisory. The alphabetic identifier system (November through Yankee) provides clear identification, avoiding confusion with other weather products.

**Convective SIGMET regions** divide the continental United States into three distinct areas. The **Eastern region** generally covers states from the Mississippi River eastward. The **Central region** encompasses the Great Plains states, while the **Western region** includes the Rocky Mountains and western states.

Each Convective SIGMET is numbered sequentially within its region, starting from 1 each day at 00Z. The numbering system can reach 99 if sufficient convective activity occurs. When no hazardous convective weather exists, the advisory states "CONVECTIVE SIGMET...NONE."

> **Pilot Responsibility**: Pilots should check for the most current advisories before flight and monitor updates during flight operations, as weather conditions can change rapidly and new advisories may be issued.

The systematic organization of weather advisories through geographic coverage and alphanumeric designators enables pilots to quickly identify relevant weather information for their specific route of flight. This organizational structure, combined with understanding of validity periods and update schedules, provides the foundation for effective use of hazardous weather advisories in aviation decision-making.

Weather advisories represent one component of the comprehensive aviation weather system. Pilots should use these advisories in conjunction with other weather products, including METARs, TAFs, pilot reports, and radar information, to develop a complete weather picture for safe flight operations. The integration of all available weather information sources provides the most accurate assessment of current and forecast conditions along the intended route of flight.

## AUTOMATED WEATHER DATA SYSTEMS

Modern aviation weather services rely increasingly on automated systems to collect, process, and disseminate meteorological data. These systems provide continuous, real-time weather observations from commercial aircraft, ground-based sensors, and satellite networks. Understanding these automated weather data systems is essential for pilots to effectively utilize the comprehensive weather information available for flight planning and decision-making.

### Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay (AMDAR) Systems

The **Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay (AMDAR)** is an international program that utilizes commercial aircraft to provide automated weather observations worldwide. This system transforms commercial flights into flying weather stations, collecting vital meteorological data during routine operations.

AMDAR provides approximately 220,000 to 230,000 aircraft observations per day globally. Aircraft equipped with onboard sensors and probes continuously measure wind speed and direction, temperature, humidity and water vapor content, turbulence intensity, and icing conditions. These measurements are automatically transmitted to ground stations without pilot intervention.

The system collects data during all phases of flight, including climb, cruise, and descent profiles. **Vertical profiles** obtained during takeoff and landing phases are particularly valuable for weather forecasting, as they provide detailed atmospheric conditions at various altitudes near airports. En route observations fill critical data gaps over oceanic areas and sparsely populated regions where traditional weather stations are unavailable.

> **AMDAR Benefits**: The program significantly enhances aircraft safety and operational efficiency through improved weather analysis and forecasting capabilities, contributing to better short and medium-term numerical weather predictions.

### Meteorological Data Collection and Reporting System (MDCRS)

The **Meteorological Data Collection and Reporting System (MDCRS)** is the United States' automated airborne weather observation program. This system collects and disseminates real-time upper-air weather observations from participating domestic airlines.

More than 1,500 aircraft currently participate in the MDCRS program, with all aircraft reporting wind and temperature data. A subset of these aircraft also provides turbulence intensity measurements and humidity/water vapor information. Each participating airline works with avionics manufacturers to program their equipment to provide specific levels of meteorological data collection.

The system monitors and collects data during climb, en route cruise, and descent phases through the aircraft's **Flight Data Acquisition and Monitoring System (FDAMS)**. This automated collection ensures consistent and comprehensive weather data coverage across the National Airspace System.

Weather elements collected by MDCRS are automatically downlinked via **ACARS (Aircraft Communications Addressing and Reporting System)** and managed by **Aeronautical Radio, Inc. (ARINC)**. ARINC then forwards the data in **Binary Universal Form for the Representation of Meteorological Data (BUFR)** format to the National Weather Service and in raw data form to the Earth Science Research Laboratory and participating airlines.

### ACARS and Satellite Data Transmission Methods

**Aircraft Communications Addressing and Reporting System (ACARS)** serves as the primary data transmission method for automated weather observations. ACARS uses VHF radio communications to transmit meteorological data from aircraft to ground stations when aircraft operate within VHF communication range.

When aircraft operate beyond ACARS range, particularly over oceanic areas, weather data transmission occurs through **Aircraft to Satellite Data Acquisition and Relay (ASDAR)** systems. ASDAR utilizes satellite communication links to ensure continuous weather data flow regardless of aircraft location.

In most cases when aircraft temporarily move beyond ACARS range, weather reports are buffered in the aircraft's systems. These buffered reports are automatically downloaded when the aircraft returns to ACARS communication range, ensuring no weather data is lost during oceanic or remote area operations.

The **BUFR format** standardizes meteorological data representation, allowing efficient processing and integration into weather forecasting models. This binary format compresses large amounts of weather data while maintaining precision and enabling rapid transmission and processing.

> **Data Link Coverage**: ACARS provides reliable coverage over continental areas, while satellite links ensure global weather data collection coverage, particularly important for international operations and oceanic flights.

### Automated vs. Augmented Weather Observations

Weather observation systems operate under two primary configurations: **automated observations** and **augmented observations**. Understanding the differences between these systems helps pilots properly interpret weather report reliability and limitations.

**Automated Weather Observing Systems (AWOS)** and **Automated Surface Observing Systems (ASOS)** provide fully automated weather observations without human intervention. These systems use various sensors to measure wind, visibility, precipitation, cloud height, temperature, and barometric pressure. Automated stations are identified in weather reports by the "AUTO" modifier in METAR reports.

Automated stations include precipitation discriminator capabilities, indicated by "AO1" (no precipitation discriminator) or "AO2" (with precipitation discriminator) notations in the remarks section. AO2 stations can distinguish between different precipitation types, while AO1 stations provide basic precipitation detection only.

**Augmented observations** combine automated sensor data with human weather observer input. Certified weather observers can enhance automated reports by adding information about weather phenomena that sensors cannot detect or accurately measure, such as virga, distant lightning, or specific cloud types like towering cumulus.

The **Flight Data Acquisition and Monitoring System (FDAMS)** represents advanced automated weather data collection aboard commercial aircraft. FDAMS continuously monitors multiple aircraft systems and environmental parameters, automatically extracting relevant meteorological data for transmission to ground-based weather services.

Automated systems provide consistent, continuous observations but may miss subtle weather phenomena or require maintenance that affects data quality. Human-augmented systems offer enhanced accuracy and detail but depend on observer availability and training. Modern weather services integrate both approaches to provide comprehensive weather observation coverage.

> **System Reliability**: Automated systems provide 24/7 weather observations with consistent measurement standards, while augmented systems add critical human interpretation for complex weather phenomena that sensors cannot adequately detect.

These automated weather data systems form the backbone of modern aviation meteorology, providing the continuous, accurate, and comprehensive weather information essential for safe flight operations. Pilots benefit from this vast network of automated observations through improved weather forecasts, real-time condition updates, and enhanced situational awareness throughout all phases of flight.

## Summary

Review the key points from this unit:

- Aviation weather services combine efforts from the NWS, FAA, DOD, and private organizations to provide comprehensive weather information for flight safety decisions.
- Surface observations use AWOS and ASOS systems for automated weather reporting, while upper-air observations primarily rely on radiosonde balloon launches reaching 115,000 feet.
- Flight Service Stations provide 24-hour weather briefings through 1-800-WX-BRIEF, offering standard, abbreviated, and outlook briefing types based on pilot needs.
- HIWAS broadcasts hazardous weather information continuously over selected VOR facilities, identified by an "H" symbol on aeronautical charts.
- Weather observations include surface reports (METARs), upper-air data, radar information from WSR-88D NEXRAD systems, and satellite imagery for comprehensive coverage.
- Standard weather briefings follow a specific sequence: adverse conditions, synopsis, current conditions, en route forecast, destination forecast, winds aloft, and NOTAMs.
- METAR reports use standardized ICAO format with four-letter station identifiers and UTC time references for consistent worldwide interpretation.
- Wind information in METARs shows true direction in degrees, speed in knots, with gust reporting using "G" followed by peak gust speed.
- Sky conditions use specific coverage terms: SKC/CLR (clear), FEW (1/8-2/8), SCT (3/8-4/8), BKN (5/8-7/8), and OVC (8/8 coverage).
- Temperature and dew point appear in Celsius separated by a slash, while altimeter settings use four-digit groups preceded by "A" in inches of mercury.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **METAR** | Meteorological Aerodrome Report providing standardized hourly surface weather observations at airports |
| **AWOS** | Automated Weather Observing System providing basic automated weather reports at airports without full-time observers |
| **ASOS** | Automated Surface Observing System offering more sophisticated measurements than AWOS, typically at larger airports |
| **Radiosonde** | Small instrument package carried by weather balloons to collect upper-air temperature, humidity, pressure, and wind data |
| **HIWAS** | Hazardous Inflight Weather Advisory Service providing continuous broadcasts of hazardous weather over selected VORs |
| **NEXRAD** | Next Generation Radar (WSR-88D) Doppler weather radar system with precipitation and clear air detection modes |
| **PIREP** | Pilot Report providing real-time turbulence, icing, and cloud information from aircraft in flight |
| **FSS** | Flight Service Station providing weather briefings, flight plan services, and inflight advisory services to pilots |
| **UTC/Zulu Time** | Coordinated Universal Time used as aviation's standard time reference, eliminating confusion from multiple time zones |
| **RVR** | Runway Visual Range indicating horizontal distance a pilot can see down a specific runway, reported in feet |
| **SPECI** | Special METAR issued when significant weather changes occur between regular hourly observations |
| **Temperature-Dew Point Spread** | Difference between temperature and dew point values indicating atmospheric moisture and fog potential |
| **ICAO Station Identifier** | Four-letter code uniquely identifying weather reporting locations worldwide (e.g., KDFW for Dallas/Fort Worth) |
