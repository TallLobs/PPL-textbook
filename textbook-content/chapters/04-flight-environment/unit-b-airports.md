# Unit B: Airports

Every time you approach an airport, you're entering one of the most carefully designed and regulated environments in aviation—a complex system where precise markings, lights, and procedures work together to ensure safe operations for aircraft of all sizes. From the moment you first spot the runway to your final parking position, understanding this environment is crucial for making sound decisions and maintaining safety. Mastering airport operations is fundamental to becoming a confident and competent pilot.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this unit, you will be able to:

- Identify different types of airports and their classification systems used throughout the aviation system
- Locate and interpret essential airport information using charts, directories, and electronic resources
- Recognize and explain the meaning of runway markings, signs, and their significance for safe operations
- Interpret taxiway markings and signs to navigate ground operations efficiently and safely
- Describe various airport lighting systems and their use during different visibility conditions
- Execute safe procedures and communication practices at nontowered airports
- Demonstrate proper airport traffic pattern entry, flow, and departure procedures
- Apply wake turbulence avoidance techniques and collision avoidance strategies in the airport environment

## AIRPORT CLASSIFICATION AND TYPES

Understanding airport classifications is essential for safe flight operations, as different airport types present unique operational requirements, facilities, and procedures. The Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) categorizes airports based on various criteria including passenger activity, cargo operations, ownership structure, and air traffic control services. This classification system helps pilots understand what services and facilities to expect at different airports and determines applicable regulations and procedures.

### Airport Categories and Definitions

The FAA defines an **airport** as any area of land or water used or intended for landing or takeoff of aircraft. This broad definition encompasses various facility types including traditional airports, seaplane bases, heliports, and facilities designed to accommodate tilt rotor aircraft. The airport definition also includes areas used for airport buildings, facilities, and associated rights of way.

Airport categories are legally defined based on specific operational criteria and passenger activity levels. These classifications determine federal funding eligibility, regulatory requirements, and the types of services provided at each facility. Understanding these categories helps pilots anticipate the level of services available and the operational complexity they may encounter.

The classification system also serves administrative purposes for airport planning, funding allocation, and regulatory oversight. Each category has specific requirements for safety standards, facility maintenance, and operational procedures that directly impact flight operations.

### Commercial Service, Cargo Service, Reliever, and General Aviation Airports

**Commercial Service Airports** are publicly owned facilities that accommodate at least **2,500 passenger boardings** each calendar year and receive scheduled passenger service. **Passenger boardings** refer to revenue passenger boardings on aircraft in service in air commerce, whether in scheduled or non-scheduled service. This definition includes passengers continuing on international flights that stop at U.S. airports for non-traffic purposes such as refueling or maintenance rather than passenger activity. These boardings are also referred to as **enplanements**.

Commercial service airports are further subdivided into categories based on passenger volume. **Primary airports** handle more than 10,000 passenger enplanements annually, while smaller commercial service airports handle between 2,500 and 10,000 enplanements. These facilities typically offer the most comprehensive services including multiple runways, sophisticated navigation aids, and extensive terminal facilities.

**Cargo Service Airports** serve aircraft providing air transportation of cargo only, with a total annual **landed weight of more than 100 million pounds**. **Landed weight** specifically means the weight of aircraft transporting only cargo in intrastate, interstate, and foreign air transportation. An airport may simultaneously qualify as both a commercial service and cargo service airport if it meets the criteria for both categories.

**Reliever Airports** are designated by the FAA specifically to relieve congestion at commercial service airports and provide improved general aviation access to surrounding communities. These facilities may be either publicly or privately owned. Reliever airports serve a critical role in the national airport system by handling general aviation traffic that might otherwise compete for space at busy commercial airports.

**General Aviation Airports** represent the largest single category in the U.S. airport system, encompassing all remaining airports not classified in other categories. This diverse group includes privately owned, public-use airports that enplane 2,500 or more passengers annually and receive scheduled airline service, but don't meet the public ownership requirement for commercial service designation.

> **Important Note**: The distinction between these categories affects more than just statistical classification—it determines federal funding eligibility, regulatory oversight requirements, and the types of services airports are required to provide.

### Towered vs. Nontowered Airports

The presence or absence of an operating control tower creates the fundamental operational distinction between airport types. This classification directly impacts communication procedures, traffic pattern operations, and regulatory requirements for aircraft operations.

**Towered airports** have an operating air traffic control (ATC) facility that provides "the safe, orderly, and expeditious flow of air traffic." At towered airports, pilots are required to maintain **two-way radio communication** with ATC and must acknowledge and comply with all air traffic control instructions. Pilots must immediately advise ATC if they cannot comply with issued instructions and request amended instructions.

When operating at towered airports, pilots may deviate from ATC instructions only in emergency situations, but must advise ATC of the deviation as soon as possible. This requirement ensures that controllers maintain awareness of all aircraft movements within their area of responsibility. Tower-controlled airports typically have more complex airspace requirements and may operate within Class B, C, or D airspace.

**Nontowered airports** do not have an operating control tower, making two-way radio communications optional rather than required. However, radio communication remains a strongly recommended safety practice for announcing intentions to other traffic in the area. The key to safe operations at nontowered airports is selecting the correct **Common Traffic Advisory Frequency (CTAF)**.

The **CTAF** is specifically designated for carrying out airport advisory practices while operating to or from airports without operating control towers. The CTAF may be a **UNICOM** frequency, **MULTICOM** frequency, Flight Service Station (FSS) frequency, or tower frequency when the tower is closed. This frequency is identified in appropriate aeronautical publications including sectional charts and the Chart Supplement U.S.

**UNICOM** is a nongovernment air-to-ground radio communication station that may provide airport information at public-use airports without towers or FSS. UNICOM stations can provide weather information, wind direction, recommended runway information, and other necessary operational data upon pilot request.

At nontowered airports, traffic patterns are always entered at pattern altitude, typically 1,000 feet AGL unless otherwise established. The preferred entry method from the downwind side is approaching the pattern on a 45-degree course to the downwind leg, joining at midfield. Entry from the upwind side requires crossing midfield at least 500 feet above pattern altitude (normally 1,500 feet AGL), though 2,000 feet AGL is recommended where large or turbine aircraft operate.

### Civil, Military, and Private Airports

Airport ownership and access restrictions create another important classification system that affects operational procedures and pilot access rights.

**Civil airports** are open to the general public and constitute the majority of airports in the United States. These facilities welcome transient aircraft and typically provide various services including fuel, maintenance, and passenger amenities. Civil airports may be publicly or privately owned, but must allow public access to maintain their civil airport designation.

Public-use civil airports are required to meet specific safety and operational standards, maintain published operational data, and provide non-discriminatory access to all qualified aircraft operators. These airports are depicted on sectional charts and included in the Chart Supplement U.S. with comprehensive operational information.

**Military/Federal Government airports** are operated by the military, National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA), or other federal agencies. These facilities are generally restricted to official government operations, though some military airports may allow civilian aircraft access under specific circumstances or during joint-use agreements.

Military airports often have unique operational procedures, communication requirements, and security restrictions. Civilian pilots should not attempt to use military facilities without prior authorization and appropriate clearances. Some military bases operate under joint-use agreements allowing limited civilian access during specified hours or conditions.

**Private airports** are designated for private or restricted use only and are not open to the general public. These facilities are privately owned and operated, with access limited to authorized users such as aircraft owners, club members, or invited guests. Private airports may range from simple grass strips serving individual aircraft owners to sophisticated corporate facilities.

Private airports are not required to meet the same regulatory standards as public-use facilities and may have unique operational characteristics, non-standard procedures, or limited services. While some private airports may be depicted on sectional charts, many are not included in standard aeronautical publications.

> **Regulatory Reminder**: Operating at private airports requires permission from the airport owner or operator. Unauthorized use of private airports may result in trespassing charges and regulatory violations.

The distinction between public-use and private airports also affects emergency operations. In emergency situations, pilots may use any available airport including private facilities, but should notify airport operators and authorities as soon as possible after landing.

Understanding these airport classifications enables pilots to select appropriate facilities for their missions, anticipate available services, and prepare for the operational procedures required at different airport types. This knowledge is essential for flight planning and contributes significantly to operational safety and efficiency.

## AIRPORT INFORMATION SOURCES

Pilots require comprehensive and current information about airports to conduct safe flight operations. Multiple information sources provide critical data about runway conditions, services, frequencies, construction activities, and temporary changes that could affect flight planning and airport operations. Understanding how to access and interpret these information sources is essential for every pilot.

### Aeronautical Charts and Airport Symbols

**Aeronautical charts** serve as the primary visual reference for airport information during flight planning and navigation. These charts display airports using standardized symbols that convey essential information at a glance. The **sectional chart** is the most commonly used VFR chart and provides detailed airport information for visual navigation.

Airport symbols on sectional charts vary based on the type and services available at each facility. **Public-use airports** are depicted with blue symbols, while **private airports** appear in magenta. The symbol design indicates whether the airport has **hard-surface runways** (solid symbols) or **soft-surface runways** (open symbols with tick marks).

> **Chart Symbol Categories**: Blue symbols indicate public-use airports, magenta indicates private-use airports. Solid symbols show hard-surface runways, while open symbols with tick marks indicate soft surfaces.

Critical information displayed adjacent to airport symbols includes the **airport identifier**, **field elevation** in feet above mean sea level, and the **longest runway length** in hundreds of feet. For example, an airport shown as "3R7" indicates the longest runway is 3,700 feet. **Control tower** airports display the letters "CT" in a blue box, along with the primary **air traffic control frequency**.

Additional services are indicated by specific notations. **Fuel availability** is shown by the letter "F" following runway length data. **Lighting capability** for night operations appears as "L" in the airport data block. Airports with **instrument approaches** display a star symbol adjacent to the airport identifier.

### Chart Supplement U.S. (formerly Airport/Facility Directory)

The **Chart Supplement U.S.** provides the most comprehensive source of airport information available to pilots. Published in seven regional volumes and revised every 56 days, this publication contains detailed data on all public-use airports, heliports, and seaplane bases.

The Chart Supplement entry for each airport follows a standardized format that begins with the **airport name** and **identifier**. The first line includes the **airport location**, **time zone**, **latitude and longitude coordinates**, and **sectional chart reference**. This is followed by elevation data showing both **field elevation** and **traffic pattern altitude**.

**Runway information** occupies a central portion of each entry, detailing dimensions, surface composition, condition, and lighting systems for each runway. Weight-bearing capacity, approach lighting systems, and **visual glideslope indicators** such as **VASI** or **PAPI** systems are specified when available.

**Communication frequencies** are listed in order of typical use, beginning with **ATIS** (Automated Terminal Information Service) when available, followed by **ground control**, **tower**, **approach control**, and **departure control** frequencies. The **Common Traffic Advisory Frequency (CTAF)** is clearly identified for non-towered airports.

**Services and facilities** information includes fuel types available, maintenance services, ground transportation options, and **Fixed Base Operator (FBO)** contact information. **NOTAM identifier codes**, **weather reporting systems**, and special procedures or restrictions are detailed in this section.

The Chart Supplement contains additional valuable information including **special notices**, **preferred IFR routing**, **VOR receiver checkpoints**, and **airport diagrams** for complex towered airports. **Land and Hold Short Operations (LAHSO)** information appears for participating airports, along with specific runway combinations and available landing distances.

### Notices to Airmen (NOTAMs)

**Notices to Airmen (NOTAMs)** provide time-critical information about temporary changes or hazards that affect flight operations. This information is too recent or temporary to be included in published charts or the Chart Supplement, making NOTAMs essential for current situational awareness.

**NOTAM-D** (distant NOTAMs) contain information that could affect flight planning decisions, including runway closures, construction activities, changes in navigational aid status, and airspace restrictions. These NOTAMs are distributed beyond the local area and remain in the system until the condition is corrected or becomes permanent enough for chart publication.

**FDC NOTAMs** are issued by the **Federal Data Center** and contain regulatory information such as temporary flight restrictions, changes to instrument approach procedures, and modifications to published routes. These NOTAMs often reference specific chart amendments or procedure changes that pilots must observe.

**Military NOTAMs** pertain to U.S. Air Force, Army, Marine, and Navy facilities, while **International NOTAMs** affect operations outside the United States. **Search and Rescue NOTAMs** contain information about search and rescue operations that may impact normal flight operations.

NOTAM information typically includes the **effective date and time**, **estimated duration**, **location identifier**, and detailed description of the condition or restriction. Time references in NOTAMs use **Coordinated Universal Time (UTC)** to eliminate confusion across time zones.

> **NOTAM Currency**: NOTAMs must be checked immediately prior to flight as conditions can change rapidly. Information more than a few hours old may no longer be accurate for flight planning purposes.

Common NOTAM subjects include **runway and taxiway closures**, **lighting system outages**, **construction equipment on or near runways**, **navigational aid malfunctions**, and **airspace restrictions**. Pilots should pay particular attention to NOTAMs affecting their departure airport, destination, and any intermediate stops.

### Automated Terminal Information Service (ATIS)

**Automated Terminal Information Service (ATIS)** broadcasts recorded information containing current weather conditions and essential operational information at towered airports. This continuous broadcast typically updates hourly or more frequently when conditions change significantly.

ATIS broadcasts begin with the **phonetic alphabet identifier** (Alpha, Bravo, Charlie, etc.) that changes with each update. This identifier system ensures pilots receive current information and allows controllers to verify pilots have the latest data. The broadcast concludes with instructions such as "Advise on initial contact, you have information Charlie."

**Weather information** in ATIS broadcasts includes **wind direction and velocity**, **visibility**, **present weather conditions**, **sky condition**, **temperature and dewpoint**, and **altimeter setting**. This meteorological data enables pilots to assess whether conditions meet their personal minimums and aircraft capabilities.

**Operational information** encompasses **runways in use** for departures and arrivals, **instrument approach procedures** in use, **taxiway restrictions**, and **construction activity** that might affect ground movement. **Special procedures** such as **noise abatement** requirements or **wake turbulence** advisories may also be included.

ATIS broadcasts significantly reduce radio congestion by eliminating the need for controllers to repeatedly transmit routine information to each aircraft. Pilots should monitor ATIS before contacting **ground control** for taxi clearance or **approach control** when arriving.

> **ATIS Procedure**: Listen to the complete ATIS broadcast and note the identifier letter. When contacting ATC, state "with information [identifier]" to confirm receipt of current data.

At airports without ATIS, similar information may be available through **Automatic Surface Observing System (ASOS)** or **Automated Weather Observing System (AWOS)** broadcasts. These systems provide automated weather observations but typically do not include operational information found in ATIS broadcasts.

The **ATIS frequency** is listed in the Chart Supplement and appears on sectional charts for participating airports. Most ATIS broadcasts operate on frequencies between 118.0 and 136.975 MHz, with many airports using discrete frequencies to avoid interference with air traffic control communications.

Modern **Digital ATIS (D-ATIS)** systems allow pilots with appropriate equipment to receive ATIS information via data link rather than voice broadcast. This technology reduces frequency congestion while providing the same essential information found in traditional ATIS broadcasts.

## RUNWAY MARKINGS AND SIGNS

Understanding runway markings and signs is fundamental to safe airport operations. Every pilot must comprehend these visual cues to navigate airport surfaces correctly and comply with air traffic control instructions. **Runway markings** are painted directly on the pavement surface in white, while **runway signs** are vertical displays with specific color schemes that convey critical operational information. The standardization of these markings and signs across all airports ensures consistent interpretation regardless of airport size or complexity.

The complexity of runway markings varies significantly based on the type of operations conducted at each airport. A basic VFR runway may display only centerline markings and runway numbers, while precision instrument runways feature comprehensive marking systems including touchdown zone markings, centerline lights, and distance remaining markers. Pilots must familiarize themselves with these variations to operate safely at different airport types.

### Basic Runway Markings and Designation System

**Runway numbering** follows a systematic approach based on magnetic direction. Runway numbers represent the whole number nearest one-tenth the magnetic azimuth of the runway centerline, measured clockwise from magnetic north. For example, a runway aligned at 095° magnetic would be designated as Runway 10, while a runway at 275° magnetic becomes Runway 28. This system provides pilots with immediate directional reference for wind considerations and approach planning.

The numbering system accommodates magnetic variation changes over time. When magnetic declination shifts sufficiently, airports may renumber their runways to maintain accuracy. This process typically occurs when the magnetic bearing changes by more than 5° from the designated number.

**Parallel runway designations** become necessary when airports have two or more runways laid out in the same direction. These runways receive letter suffixes to distinguish them: **L** for left, **R** for right, and **C** for center when three parallel runways exist. Standing at the approach end of the runway and facing the departure end determines the designation. For instance, at an airport with two parallel runways aligned at 090°, the southern runway becomes 09L and the northern runway becomes 09R.

> **Critical Note**: Runway designations are based on magnetic bearings, not true bearings. Pilots must account for local magnetic variation when correlating runway numbers with compass headings.

**Runway centerline markings** consist of uniformly spaced white stripes and gaps extending the full length of the runway. These markings provide visual guidance for maintaining directional control during takeoff and landing operations. The standard centerline marking pattern uses 120-foot-long stripes separated by 80-foot gaps, though dimensions may vary on shorter runways.

**Runway threshold markings** identify the beginning of the runway available for landing. These consist of eight longitudinal stripes for runways 150 feet wide, with the number of stripes decreasing proportionally for narrower runways. The stripes are 150 feet long and 5.75 feet wide, spaced 5.75 feet apart. Some precision instrument runways include **touchdown zone markings** beyond the threshold, consisting of groups of one, two, or three rectangular bars positioned 500 feet apart for the first 3,000 feet of runway.

### Displaced and Relocated Thresholds

Understanding the distinction between **displaced thresholds** and **relocated thresholds** is crucial for safe runway operations, as each type affects aircraft operations differently.

A **displaced threshold** occurs when the threshold is located at a point other than the designated beginning of the runway pavement. This displacement reduces the landing distance available but permits aircraft operations on the displaced portion under specific conditions. The area behind a displaced threshold remains available for takeoffs in either direction and landings from the opposite direction. Aircraft may taxi on this surface but cannot use it for landing in the direction of displacement.

Displaced threshold markings include a ten-foot-wide white threshold bar positioned across the runway width at the displaced threshold location. White arrows appear along the runway centerline in the area between the beginning of the runway pavement and the displaced threshold, pointing toward the usable landing surface. Additionally, **white arrowheads** span the runway width just prior to the threshold bar, providing clear visual indication of the threshold location.

A **relocated threshold** represents a temporary condition where a portion of the runway is closed due to construction, maintenance, or safety concerns. Unlike displaced thresholds, the closed portion behind a relocated threshold is not available for any aircraft operations except taxiing. When a threshold is relocated, it effectively shortens both the landing distance for that runway direction and the takeoff distance for the opposite direction.

Relocated threshold markings feature a ten-foot-wide white bar across the runway width, similar to displaced thresholds. However, **yellow arrowheads** rather than white ones are positioned across the runway width just prior to the threshold bar. The closed portion typically displays yellow crosses or other closure markings to emphasize the unusable area.

> **Operational Impact**: Pilots must verify current runway lengths when displaced or relocated thresholds are present, as these conditions significantly affect takeoff and landing performance calculations.

The distinction between these threshold types directly impacts flight planning. Aircraft performance calculations must account for reduced runway lengths, and pilots should consult current NOTAMs and airport information to identify any threshold displacements or relocations before flight operations.

### Runway Holding Position Signs and Markings

**Runway holding position markings** and signs represent critical safety elements that prevent runway incursions. These markings consist of four yellow lines painted across the taxiway width where taxiways intersect runways. The pattern includes two solid yellow lines followed by two dashed yellow lines when approaching the runway. Aircraft must stop before the solid lines when approaching the runway and may not cross these lines without specific air traffic control clearance at towered airports.

The **runway holding position sign** serves as the airport equivalent of a stop sign. This mandatory instruction sign displays white characters outlined in black on a red background. The sign is always collocated with surface-painted holding position markings at taxiway-runway intersections. Compliance with runway holding position signs is mandatory, and violations may result in FAA enforcement action for pilot deviation.

When positioned at the threshold of a takeoff runway, the holding position sign displays only the designation of that runway (e.g., "14"). However, when located at taxiway intersections with runways at points other than thresholds, the sign shows the designations of both runway directions. The runway numbers are arranged to correspond with their relative positions: if approaching Runway 18-36 from the side, "18" appears on the left and "36" on the right, indicating the respective threshold directions.

At complex intersections where a taxiway meets two intersecting runways, the holding position sign displays both runway designations with arrows indicating the approximate runway alignments. These arrows point toward the respective runway thresholds, providing directional guidance for pilot situational awareness. Such intersections often receive special attention as potential "hot spots" on airport diagrams due to their complexity.

**Surface-painted runway holding position signs** supplement the vertical signs in some locations. These consist of white characters on a red background painted on the pavement to the left of the taxiway centerline. While serving the same function as vertical signs, surface-painted signs provide additional visual reference, particularly valuable in low-visibility conditions.

At towered airports, aircraft may not cross runway holding position markings without explicit ATC clearance. The controller will issue specific instructions such as "cleared to cross Runway 22" or "taxi onto Runway 22 position and hold." At non-towered airports, pilots may proceed past holding position markings only after ensuring the runway is clear of aircraft and no aircraft are on final approach, exercising extreme caution throughout the process.

> **Regulatory Requirement**: 14 CFR 91.129 requires compliance with runway holding position markings at towered airports. Violations can result in certificate action, civil penalties, and remedial training requirements.

**Enhanced taxiway centerline markings** provide additional warning of approaching runway holding positions. These markings consist of yellow dashed lines on either side of the normal solid taxiway centerline, extending up to 150 feet prior to runway holding position markings. The enhanced markings serve as an advance warning system, prompting pilots to prepare for the mandatory stop at the holding position.

### Runway Safety Areas and Boundary Markings

The **Runway Safety Area (RSA)** encompasses a defined surface surrounding the runway, prepared or suitable for reducing aircraft damage risk in case of undershoot, overshoot, or runway excursion events. RSA dimensions vary based on runway classification and aircraft operations, with specific criteria outlined in FAA Advisory Circular 150/5300-13, Airport Design. The RSA extends beyond runway ends and laterally from runway edges, providing a buffer zone for aircraft operations.

RSA lateral boundaries are typically identified by runway holding position signs and markings on adjoining taxiway stubs. The area is usually graded, mowed, and maintained free of objects that could damage aircraft or interfere with emergency response vehicles. At uncontrolled airports, pilots must ensure adequate separation from other aircraft before entering the RSA, as no air traffic control service monitors these areas.

**Runway safety area boundary signs** appear at some airports, particularly towered facilities where controllers routinely request pilots to report "clear of the runway." These signs feature yellow backgrounds with black inscriptions and face away from the runway, visible only to aircraft exiting the runway. The signs provide visual confirmation that the aircraft has cleared the runway safety area boundary when the entire aircraft passes beyond the sign and its accompanying pavement markings.

The boundary sign system assists both pilots and controllers in determining aircraft position relative to the runway. When a controller requests "report clear of Runway 24," the pilot should continue taxiing until the entire aircraft passes the boundary sign before making the required report. This ensures the aircraft has completely cleared the runway safety area and does not conflict with arriving or departing traffic.

**ILS critical area boundaries** represent another type of specialized marking system designed to protect instrument approach signal quality. When weather conditions require protection of ILS critical areas, air traffic controllers issue hold-short instructions to prevent aircraft or vehicles from entering these designated areas. The **ILS critical area holding position marking** appears as a yellow ladder pattern extending across the taxiway width.

ILS critical area holding position signs display white characters outlined in black on a red background, similar to runway holding position signs but with specific "ILS" designations. These signs read "ILS HOLD" or include specific runway designations such as "ILS 09 HOLD." Aircraft must comply with ATC instructions to hold short of ILS critical areas during instrument approaches in low visibility conditions.

> **Weather Consideration**: ILS critical area protection is typically required when ceiling is below 800 feet or visibility is less than 2 miles, though specific requirements may vary by airport and approach type.

### Land and Hold Short Operations (LAHSO)

**Land and Hold Short Operations (LAHSO)** represent an air traffic control procedure designed to maximize runway utilization during simultaneous operations on intersecting runways. LAHSO may be implemented when an aircraft can land and stop before reaching an intersecting runway where another aircraft is operating. This procedure requires active pilot participation and compliance with specific operational requirements.

LAHSO operations require minimum weather conditions of 1,000-foot ceiling and 3 statute miles visibility. Pilots must receive specific clearance language from ATC, such as "Cleared to land Runway 09, hold short of Runway 18." The pilot-in-command has final authority to accept or decline any LAHSO clearance based on aircraft performance, runway conditions, and operational factors.

**LAHSO holding position markings** consist of white markings on the runway surface at the designated hold-short point, typically positioned before runway intersections. The associated **LAHSO holding position sign** displays a white inscription with black border on a red background, indicating the specific runway designation where the aircraft must stop. These signs are positioned adjacent to the holding position markings to provide clear visual reference.

Pilots accepting LAHSO clearances must ensure their aircraft can stop before the designated hold-short point under existing conditions. Factors affecting this decision include aircraft weight, runway surface conditions, wind, and approach speed. If unable to comply with the hold-short requirement, pilots must immediately notify ATC and request alternative instructions.

LAHSO operations include several important restrictions and considerations:

Student pilots performing solo flights are prohibited from participating in LAHSO operations. This restriction recognizes the complexity of these operations and the need for experienced decision-making regarding aircraft performance and stopping distances.

Air carrier aircraft operations may be restricted from LAHSO when the other aircraft is a general aviation aircraft, depending on airport-specific procedures and aircraft size differentials. These restrictions address wake turbulence concerns and stopping distance requirements for larger aircraft.

LAHSO operations are generally not authorized during nighttime hours due to reduced visibility and depth perception challenges. Similarly, wet runway conditions typically preclude LAHSO operations due to extended stopping distances and reduced braking effectiveness.

> **Performance Critical**: Pilots must know their aircraft's actual landing distance under current conditions before accepting LAHSO clearances. Published handbook distances may not account for operational factors like approach speed variations or runway surface conditions.

When LAHSO is in effect, pilots receiving standard "cleared to land" instructions without hold-short requirements are authorized to use the entire landing length of the runway. These pilots should disregard any LAHSO holding position markings and continue normal landing roll and turnoff procedures.

ATC provides essential information to pilots during LAHSO operations, including the reason for the procedure and traffic information that necessitates the hold-short requirement. This information enables pilots to make informed decisions about accepting or declining the LAHSO clearance based on their assessment of the operational situation.

The LAHSO system includes enhanced lighting at equipped airports, featuring **land and hold short lights** that consist of pulsing white lights installed across the runway at the hold-short point. These lights operate automatically when LAHSO is in effect and turn off when the procedure is no longer required, providing additional visual reference for pilots during critical stopping operations.

LAHSO procedures require pilots to understand airport signage, know aircraft performance capabilities, maintain situational awareness of intersecting runway traffic, and communicate effectively with air traffic control. Successful LAHSO operations depend on precise pilot technique and conservative decision-making regarding aircraft stopping capabilities under all existing conditions.

## TAXIWAY MARKINGS AND SIGNS

Understanding taxiway markings and signs is critical for safe ground operations at any airport. These visual aids provide essential navigation information and help prevent runway incursions, which represent one of the most serious safety hazards in aviation. **Taxiway markings** are painted directly on the pavement surface, while **taxiway signs** may be either painted on the surface or mounted vertically adjacent to taxiways.

The airport sign system consists of six distinct categories, each identified by specific color combinations that convey different types of information to pilots. These standardized markings and signs create a universal language that allows pilots to navigate safely at unfamiliar airports while maintaining situational awareness during ground operations.

### Taxiway Centerline and Edge Markings

**Taxiway centerline markings** consist of a single continuous yellow line painted down the center of the taxiway. This marking provides directional guidance and helps pilots maintain proper positioning while taxiing. The centerline should be followed precisely to ensure adequate wingtip clearance from obstacles, other aircraft, and adjacent taxiways.

**Taxiway edge markings** are continuous yellow lines that define the usable edges of the taxiway pavement. These markings are particularly important at airports with complex taxiway systems or where taxiways are adjacent to non-movement areas. Edge markings help pilots maintain proper lateral positioning and avoid inadvertently taxiing onto unpaved surfaces or restricted areas.

At intersections, centerline markings may curve to indicate the proper taxi path. Pilots should follow these curved centerlines rather than making abrupt turns, as the markings are designed to provide adequate clearance for the aircraft's turning radius. The standard taxiway centerline width is 6 inches, making it easily distinguishable from runway centerlines, which are significantly wider.

> **Important Note**: Taxiway markings are yellow, while runway markings are white. This color distinction is fundamental to airport surface navigation and helps prevent confusion between taxiways and runways.

### Direction and Destination Signs

**Direction signs** feature black characters and arrows on a yellow background and indicate the designation of intersecting taxiways or the direction to follow to reach a particular taxiway. These signs are normally positioned on the left side of the taxiway prior to an intersection. The arrows on direction signs point in the direction a pilot must turn to follow the indicated taxiway.

Direction signs display taxiway designations using a standardized system. Single letters (A through Z) identify most taxiways, while double letters (AA, BB, etc.) may be used at larger airports with extensive taxiway systems. When multiple taxiways intersect at one location, the direction sign shows all available options with appropriate arrows indicating the turn direction for each.

**Destination signs** also have yellow backgrounds with black characters and arrows, but they indicate directions to specific areas of the airport rather than individual taxiways. Common destinations shown on these signs include runways, terminals, cargo areas, general aviation ramps, and fixed base operators (FBOs). The arrow always points toward the destination, and signs are positioned before intersections where turns are required.

[Figure 14-22: Destination sign to the fixed-base operator (FBO) - PHAK Ch 14, Fig 14-22]

When destination signs show multiple locations sharing the same taxi route, the destinations are separated by dots (•) with a single arrow. However, when destinations require different taxi routes, each destination appears with its own arrow and is separated by vertical black dividers. This system allows pilots to quickly identify the correct path to their intended destination.

**Surface-painted direction signs** may also be encountered, particularly at busy airports. These ground markings use the same color scheme as vertical signs but are painted directly on the taxiway pavement. Surface markings are especially useful when vertical signs might be obscured by weather conditions or other aircraft.

### Location and Information Signs

**Location signs** have black characters on a yellow background with a yellow border and contain no arrows. These signs identify the taxiway or runway on which an aircraft is currently located. Location signs are essential for maintaining situational awareness and provide confirmation of aircraft position during taxi operations.

Location signs appear in several configurations depending on their purpose. **Taxiway location signs** display the designation of the current taxiway and are typically positioned at intersections or at regular intervals along lengthy taxiways. **Runway location signs** show the runway designation and are located along runways to help pilots identify their position during taxi operations or runway crossings.

**Boundary location signs** mark the edge of the runway safety area and are visible only when exiting the runway. These signs have yellow backgrounds with black markings and help pilots determine when they have cleared the runway safety area boundary. The signs face away from the runway and provide a visual reference for the "clear of runway" position that controllers often request.

**Information signs** provide additional data that cannot be conveyed through other sign types. These signs use yellow backgrounds with black characters and may display radio frequencies, noise abatement procedures, or other operational information. The specific content and placement of information signs is determined by individual airport operators based on local requirements.

At complex intersections, multiple location signs may be grouped together to provide comprehensive position information. Pilots should verify their location using these signs, particularly when receiving taxi instructions that reference specific taxiway intersections or when operating at unfamiliar airports.

> **Radio Communication**: When contacting ground control, always include your location as indicated by location signs. For example: "Ground, Cessna 12345 at Taxiway Bravo and Charlie intersection, ready to taxi."

### Enhanced Taxiway Centerline Markings

**Enhanced taxiway centerline markings** provide additional visual warnings to pilots approaching runway holding positions. These markings consist of yellow dashed lines positioned on either side of the normal solid taxiway centerline. The dashed lines extend up to 150 feet prior to runway holding position markings and serve as a "prepare to stop" indication.

[Figure 14-21: Enhanced taxiway centerline marking and runway holding position marking - PHAK Ch 14, Fig 14-21]

The enhanced markings are implemented at most towered airports as part of runway incursion prevention efforts. When pilots observe these dashed lines, they should immediately prepare to stop at the upcoming runway holding position unless they have received specific clearance from ATC to proceed onto or across the runway.

These markings are particularly valuable during periods of reduced visibility or when pilot workload is high. The 150-foot advance warning provides adequate time for pilots to reduce taxi speed and prepare for the stop. The enhanced markings complement but do not replace standard runway holding position markings and signs.

At airports equipped with enhanced taxiway centerline markings, pilots should establish procedures to acknowledge these warnings. Recommended practices include reducing taxi speed when encountering the dashed lines, verifying ATC clearance status, and ensuring situational awareness regarding runway traffic before proceeding.

The enhanced markings use the same yellow paint as standard taxiway markings but create a distinctive visual pattern that draws pilot attention. Studies have shown that these markings significantly reduce runway incursion incidents by improving pilot awareness of approaching runway environments.

### ILS Critical Area Markings and Signs

**ILS critical area markings and signs** protect the sensitive radio signal environment required for precision instrument approaches. The **Instrument Landing System (ILS)** broadcasts precision approach guidance to aircraft, and this system requires protection from interference that can be caused by aircraft or vehicles in designated critical areas.

**ILS critical area holding position markings** appear as a yellow ladder pattern painted across the full width of taxiways that intersect ILS critical areas. This distinctive ladder marking is easily recognizable and clearly different from standard runway holding position markings, which consist of solid and dashed yellow lines.

**ILS critical area signs** have white characters outlined in black on a red background, following the same color scheme as runway holding position signs. These signs display text such as "ILS" followed by the runway designation (e.g., "ILS 4R") and are positioned adjacent to the ladder markings.

[Figure 14-24: Instrument landing system (ILS) holding position sign and marking on Taxiway Golf - PHAK Ch 14, Fig 14-24]

Pilots must hold short of ILS critical area markings only when specifically instructed by ATC. The typical instruction is "hold short of Runway [XX] ILS critical area." During good weather conditions with high ceilings and visibility, ATC may not protect ILS critical areas, and aircraft may taxi past these markings without specific clearance.

ILS critical area protection is most commonly implemented during **low visibility conditions** when arriving aircraft depend heavily on precision approach signals. Weather conditions that typically trigger ILS critical area protection include ceilings below 800 feet or visibility less than 2 miles, though specific minimums may vary by airport and ILS system.

When holding short of an ILS critical area, no part of the aircraft may extend beyond the yellow ladder markings. Pilots should position their aircraft completely behind the markings and remain there until ATC provides clearance to proceed or cancels the hold short instruction.

> **ILS Critical Area Protection**: These areas may extend several hundred feet from the runway centerline and approach path. Understanding the size and sensitivity of ILS critical areas helps pilots appreciate why ATC protection is essential during instrument approaches.

The ILS critical area system operates independently of standard runway holding position procedures. An aircraft may be cleared to "hold short of ILS critical area" while simultaneously being cleared to a runway holding position that is closer to the runway. In such cases, the more restrictive clearance (ILS hold) takes precedence, and the pilot must stop at the ILS critical area boundary.

Understanding the six types of airport signs and their specific color coding is essential for safe ground operations. **Mandatory instruction signs** (red background, white text) require immediate compliance and mark runway entrances and critical areas. **Location signs** (yellow background with black text and yellow border) identify current position. **Direction signs** (yellow background, black text with arrows) show available taxiway routes. **Destination signs** (yellow background, black text with arrows) point toward airport facilities. **Information signs** (yellow background, black text) provide operational data. **Distance remaining signs** (black background, white numbers) indicate remaining runway length in thousands of feet.

**Taxiway/taxiway intersection holding positions** are marked with single dashed yellow lines extending across the taxiway width. These markings indicate where ATC normally holds aircraft during ground traffic management. Unlike runway holding positions, these markings are used for traffic flow control rather than safety separation, but pilots must still comply with all ATC hold short instructions at these locations.

The comprehensive system of taxiway markings and signs creates multiple layers of safety and navigation assistance. Pilots should study airport diagrams before arrival, maintain constant awareness of their position using location signs, and always comply with holding position markings until receiving appropriate clearances. This systematic approach to ground navigation significantly reduces the risk of runway incursions and enhances overall airport safety.

## AIRPORT LIGHTING SYSTEMS

Airport lighting systems are essential components of aviation safety, providing visual guidance for pilots during night operations and periods of reduced visibility. These standardized lighting systems enable safe aircraft operations at airports regardless of weather conditions or time of day. Understanding the various types of airport lighting, their colors, intensities, and operational procedures is crucial for all pilots operating in the National Airspace System.

The Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) has established specific standards for airport lighting to ensure consistency across all airports. These standards cover everything from beacon colors that identify airport types to the precise positioning and intensity levels of runway edge lights. Pilots must be thoroughly familiar with these lighting systems to safely navigate airport surfaces and approach paths during low-visibility operations.

### Airport Beacons and Identification

**Airport beacons** serve as the primary visual identification system for airports during nighttime operations. These rotating or flashing light systems are typically operated from dusk until dawn, though they may also be activated during daylight hours when the ceiling is less than 1,000 feet and visibility is less than 3 statute miles. However, pilots should note that beacon operation during daylight hours does not guarantee VFR weather conditions, as there is no regulatory requirement for this practice.

The beacon's **vertical light distribution** is optimized for maximum effectiveness between 1 and 10 degrees above the horizon, though the beacon remains visible well above and below this range. Modern airport beacons may utilize either omnidirectional capacitor-discharge devices or rotating mechanisms that create the visual effect of regular flashes.

> **Critical Note**: The presence of an operating airport beacon does not relieve pilots of their responsibility to determine current weather conditions and ensure compliance with VFR minimums before flight operations.

Airport beacon color combinations provide immediate identification of airport types. **Civilian land airports** display alternating white and green flashes, with the sequence typically showing white for a longer duration followed by a brief green flash. **Water airports** and seaplane bases are identified by alternating white and yellow flashes, helping pilots distinguish these facilities from standard land-based airports. **Heliports** utilize a three-color sequence of white, yellow, and green flashes to indicate their specialized nature.

**Military airports** employ a distinctive beacon pattern consisting of two quick white flashes alternating with a single green flash. This pattern immediately alerts civilian pilots to the presence of military operations and associated restrictions or procedures that may be in effect.

[Figure 14-28: Airport Beacons - PHAK Ch 14, Fig 14-28]

### Runway Lighting Systems and Intensity Levels

Runway lighting systems provide essential visual guidance for takeoff and landing operations during periods of darkness or reduced visibility. The complexity and sophistication of these systems vary based on the airport's operational requirements and the types of approaches served.

**Runway edge lights** form the foundation of most runway lighting systems, outlining the lateral boundaries of the runway. These lights are classified into three intensity categories based on their candlepower output. **High Intensity Runway Lights (HIRL)** provide maximum illumination for precision instrument approaches and operations in very low visibility conditions. **Medium Intensity Runway Lights (MIRL)** offer intermediate illumination suitable for most general aviation operations and non-precision approaches. **Low Intensity Runway Lights (LIRL)** provide basic edge definition for VFR operations and airports with minimal night traffic.

Both HIRL and MIRL systems feature **variable intensity controls** that allow adjustment based on ambient conditions and operational requirements. Air traffic control at towered airports manages these intensity settings, while pilot-controlled systems at non-towered airports allow pilots to select appropriate intensity levels through radio activation.

The **color coding** of runway edge lights follows strict standards. Standard runway edge lights emit white light along the entire runway length, providing clear lateral guidance. However, on instrument runways, the final 2,000 feet (or half the runway length, whichever is less) displays **amber lights** to warn pilots of the approaching runway end. **Red lights** mark the actual runway end, providing an unmistakable indication that the landing surface is terminating.

**Runway End Identifier Lights (REIL)** consist of synchronized flashing white lights positioned laterally on each side of the runway threshold. These high-intensity strobing lights provide rapid and positive identification of the approach end, particularly valuable when the runway threshold might otherwise be difficult to distinguish from surrounding terrain or airport lighting.

> **Intensity Selection Guidelines**: Pilots should use the lowest intensity setting that provides adequate runway definition to avoid glare and preserve night vision while ensuring sufficient visibility for safe operations.

**In-runway lighting systems** represent the most sophisticated airport lighting technology, installed primarily at airports serving precision instrument approaches. The **Runway Centerline Lighting System (RCLS)** consists of flush-mounted lights spaced at 50-foot intervals along the runway centerline. When viewed from the approach end, these lights display white until the final 3,000 feet of runway, where they begin alternating white and red for the next 2,000 feet, then display solid red for the final 1,000 feet. This color progression provides pilots with precise distance-remaining information during the critical landing phase.

**Touchdown Zone Lights (TDZL)** create two rows of transverse light bars positioned symmetrically about the runway centerline. These steady-burning white lights begin 100 feet beyond the landing threshold and extend 3,000 feet down the runway or to the midpoint, whichever distance is shorter. The touchdown zone lighting helps pilots identify the optimal landing area during low-visibility approaches.

### Visual Glideslope Indicators (VASI, PAPI, Tri-color)

Visual glideslope indicators provide pilots with precise vertical guidance during approach and landing operations, functioning effectively during both day and night conditions. These systems are designed to ensure adequate obstacle clearance while guiding aircraft to the optimal touchdown point on the runway.

**Visual Approach Slope Indicator (VASI)** systems represent the most widely installed glidepath guidance technology. VASI installations provide obstacle clearance within plus or minus 10 degrees of the extended runway centerline and up to 4 nautical miles from the runway threshold, making them suitable for a wide range of aircraft approach speeds and configurations.

The **2-bar VASI** configuration utilizes near and far light bars positioned perpendicular to the runway centerline. Each light unit projects a split beam with white light in the upper portion and red light in the lower portion. The system is calibrated so that an aircraft on the proper glidepath (typically 3 degrees) will see the near bar as white and the far bar as white, indicating **"on glidepath."** When the aircraft is **below the glidepath**, both bars appear red, signaling the need to increase the rate of descent or adjust power settings. An aircraft **above the glidepath** sees both bars as white, indicating excessive altitude that requires correction.

**3-bar VASI** systems provide two distinct glidepaths through the addition of a middle light bar. The lower glidepath, typically set at 3 degrees, serves most general aviation aircraft. The upper glidepath, positioned approximately 1/4 degree above the lower path, accommodates larger aircraft with higher cockpit positions or those requiring a steeper approach angle due to obstacle clearance requirements.

> **VASI Limitations**: Pilots should be aware that VASI indications may be unreliable when the aircraft is significantly displaced laterally from the runway centerline or when atmospheric conditions create unusual light transmission characteristics.

**Precision Approach Path Indicator (PAPI)** systems provide glidepath information through four light units arranged in a single horizontal row, typically positioned on the left side of the runway. The PAPI system offers more precise glidepath information than traditional VASI installations through its four-light configuration.

A PAPI display showing **four white lights** indicates the aircraft is significantly high, requiring an immediate descent or power reduction. **Three white lights and one red light** show the aircraft is slightly high but approaching the correct glidepath. **Two white lights and two red lights** indicate the aircraft is on the proper glidepath, typically 3 degrees. **One white light and three red lights** warn that the aircraft is slightly low and requires correction. **Four red lights** signal a dangerously low approach that requires immediate corrective action.

[Figure 14-29: Two-bar VASI System - PHAK Ch 14, Fig 14-29]
[Figure 14-30: PAPI Glideslope Indicators - PHAK Ch 14, Fig 14-30]

**Tri-color VASI** systems utilize a single light unit that projects different colors based on the aircraft's position relative to the desired glidepath. This simplified system displays **green light** when the aircraft is on the correct glidepath, **amber light** when above the glidepath, and **red light** when below the glidepath. A narrow band of **dark amber** may be visible when descending below the glidepath, but pilots must not confuse this transition zone with the above-glidepath amber indication.

**Pulsating VASI** systems employ a single light unit that varies both color and intensity based on glidepath position. **Steady white light** indicates on-glidepath positioning. **Steady red light** shows slightly below glidepath, while **pulsating red light** warns of significantly low positioning requiring immediate correction. **Pulsating white light** indicates above-glidepath positioning, with the pulse rate increasing as the deviation becomes greater.

### Taxiway and Obstruction Lighting

Taxiway lighting systems guide aircraft safely between runways, ramps, and parking areas while maintaining separation from obstacles and other aircraft. These lighting systems become particularly critical during night operations and periods of reduced visibility when visual references are limited.

**Taxiway edge lights** provide lateral guidance along taxiway surfaces through blue omnidirectional lights positioned along the taxiway edges. The blue color immediately distinguishes taxiway lighting from the white runway edge lights, preventing pilot confusion during ground operations. Many airports equipped with taxiway edge lighting also feature intensity controls that allow adjustment based on visibility conditions and operational requirements.

**Taxiway centerline lights** consist of green lights installed flush with the pavement surface along the taxiway centerline. These lights provide precise lateral guidance and are particularly valuable at large airports with complex taxiway systems. The green color coding ensures immediate identification as taxiway guidance lighting.

**Clearance bar lights** enhance the visibility of holding positions on taxiways through three in-pavement steady-burning yellow lights. These lights increase conspicuity during low visibility conditions and clearly mark locations where aircraft must hold short of runways or other critical areas. The yellow color immediately alerts pilots to stop-and-hold locations.

**Runway guard lights** provide additional warning at taxiway-runway intersections through either elevated flashing yellow lights positioned on both sides of the taxiway or a row of in-pavement yellow lights installed across the entire taxiway width. These systems enhance situational awareness and reduce the risk of runway incursions by clearly identifying runway proximity.

> **Guard Light Operation**: Runway guard lights operate automatically based on airport configuration and may flash at different rates depending on runway operational status and traffic conditions.

**Stop bar lights** represent the most sophisticated taxiway control lighting, consisting of red unidirectional lights installed across the taxiway at runway holding positions. These lights operate in conjunction with **taxiway centerline lead-on lights** that extend from the stop bar toward the runway. When ATC clears an aircraft to proceed, the red stop bar lights extinguish and the green lead-on lights activate, providing positive control and guidance.

**Obstruction lighting** ensures that pilots can identify potential hazards during both day and night operations. **Red obstruction lights** may either flash or emit steady red illumination during nighttime, with associated daytime marking consisting of orange and white paint schemes. **High-intensity white obstruction lights** provide daytime visibility through intense white flashing, with reduced intensity for nighttime operations to prevent pilot vision impairment.

**Dual lighting systems** combine flashing red beacons and steady red lights for nighttime operations with high-intensity white lights for daytime visibility. This comprehensive approach ensures obstacle visibility under all lighting conditions while minimizing the impact on pilot night vision adaptation.

### Pilot-Controlled Lighting Systems

**Pilot-controlled lighting (PCL)** systems enable pilots to activate and control airport lighting at non-towered airports through standard aircraft radio equipment. These systems provide essential lighting services without requiring on-site personnel, significantly enhancing safety during night operations at airports that would otherwise have no lighting control capability.

PCL systems operate on designated **Common Traffic Advisory Frequencies (CTAF)** and respond to specific microphone keying sequences transmitted by aircraft radios. The system typically monitors frequencies including 122.7, 122.8, 122.725, 122.975, or 123.0 MHz, as published in the Chart Supplement U.S. for each specific airport.

**Radio activation procedures** require pilots to key their aircraft microphone a specific number of times within a 5-second period to achieve the desired lighting intensity. **Seven microphone clicks within 5 seconds** activates the highest intensity available for all controlled lighting systems. **Five microphone clicks within 5 seconds** provides medium intensity lighting or lower intensity REIL operation. **Three microphone clicks within 5 seconds** activates the lowest intensity available or turns off REIL systems entirely.

The lighting system remains activated for a **15-minute period** following the last radio activation. Pilots can re-activate or change intensity levels at any time by transmitting the appropriate keying sequence. This automatic timeout feature conserves electrical power while ensuring lighting availability when needed.

> **PCL Coverage**: Pilot-controlled lighting systems typically have an effective range of 5-7 nautical miles from the airport, though terrain, aircraft altitude, and radio power output may affect actual activation distance.

**System components** controlled through PCL may include runway edge lights, taxiway lights, approach lights, and REIL systems, depending on the specific airport installation. Not all lighting systems at an airport may be pilot-controlled; some airports have combinations of pilot-controlled and continuously operating lights.

**Operational considerations** for PCL systems include ensuring the aircraft radio is tuned to the correct frequency as published in current aeronautical publications. Pilots should activate lighting well before beginning the approach to allow time for system response and to verify proper operation. If the lighting system fails to respond, pilots should attempt re-activation and consider alternate airports if lighting is essential for safe operations.

**Frequency verification** is critical for successful PCL operation. The Chart Supplement U.S. provides specific frequency information for each airport equipped with pilot-controlled lighting. Pilots should verify they are transmitting on the published frequency and that their radio is functioning properly if the lighting system does not respond to activation attempts.

[Figure 14-34: Radio-Controlled Runway Lighting - PHAK Ch 14, Fig 14-34]

Advanced lighting technologies continue to enhance airport safety through systems like **Runway Status Lights (RWSL)**, which provide automated warnings when runways are unsafe for entry, crossing, or takeoff. **Runway Entrance Lights (REL)** warn aircraft entering runways when conflicting traffic is present, while **Takeoff Hold Lights (THL)** prevent takeoff attempts when runways are occupied. These systems represent the future of airport lighting technology, providing real-time safety enhancements that reduce the risk of runway incursions and ground conflicts.

## NONTOWERED AIRPORT OPERATIONS

Operating at nontowered airports requires pilots to assume greater responsibility for collision avoidance and traffic coordination. Unlike towered airports where air traffic control provides separation and sequencing, pilots at nontowered facilities must communicate directly with each other and follow established procedures to maintain safety. Understanding proper communication protocols, traffic pattern entry techniques, and right-of-way rules is essential for safe operations in this environment.

### Common Traffic Advisory Frequency (CTAF) Procedures

The **Common Traffic Advisory Frequency (CTAF)** is the designated frequency for pilots to coordinate their activities at nontowered airports. Selection of the correct CTAF is critical for maintaining situational awareness and avoiding conflicts with other aircraft.

CTAF frequencies are published in aeronautical charts, the Chart Supplement U.S., and other official publications. The frequency may be a **UNICOM** station (typically 122.7, 122.8, 122.725, 122.975, or 123.0 MHz), a **MULTICOM** frequency (122.9 MHz), a Flight Service Station frequency, or in some cases, the tower frequency when the tower is closed.

> **CTAF Selection Priority**: When multiple communication services exist at an airport, the CTAF follows this priority: (1) Local control tower frequency when tower is closed, (2) FSS frequency, (3) UNICOM frequency, (4) MULTICOM frequency 122.9.

Pilots must monitor the CTAF when operating within 10 nautical miles of the airport and below 10,000 feet AGL. This monitoring range ensures adequate time to hear other aircraft's position reports and plan accordingly. The CTAF should be monitored continuously during taxi, takeoff, approach, and landing operations.

When the designated CTAF is a UNICOM station, pilots should attempt to contact the station for airport advisories including wind direction, favored runway, and known traffic. However, UNICOM operators are not air traffic controllers and cannot issue clearances or provide separation services. Their role is purely advisory.

### UNICOM and MULTICOM Communications

**UNICOM** (Universal Integrated Community) stations are non-government, privately operated air-to-ground communication facilities. These stations may provide airport information such as weather conditions, wind direction, recommended runway in use, and altimeter setting when available. UNICOM operators may also coordinate ground services like fuel, ground transportation, and aircraft maintenance.

When communicating with UNICOM, pilots should use standard phraseology and be concise. A typical UNICOM contact might be: "Millville UNICOM, Cherokee 1234X, 10 miles southeast, request airport advisory." The UNICOM operator might respond with: "Cherokee 1234X, Millville UNICOM, wind calm, runway 10 in use, no reported traffic, altimeter 30.15."

If no response is received from UNICOM after two attempts, pilots should use **self-announce procedures** on the CTAF frequency. It's important to remember that not all airports with a published UNICOM frequency have an operator on duty at all times.

**MULTICOM** frequency 122.9 MHz is used for self-announce procedures at airports without an operating control tower, FSS, or UNICOM. This frequency serves airports that have no ground-based communication services. All communications on MULTICOM are pilot-to-pilot, with no ground station involvement.

Some airports use **Flight Service Station (FSS)** frequencies as their CTAF. When the FSS is open, specialists can provide airport advisories, weather information, and traffic updates. When the FSS is closed, pilots use the FSS frequency for self-announce procedures.

### Self-Announce Procedures and Phraseology

Self-announce procedures enable pilots to broadcast their positions and intentions for the benefit of other traffic in the area. These broadcasts help create a shared mental picture of traffic flow and reduce the risk of conflicts.

Standard self-announce position reports should be made at specific points in the traffic pattern and during approach and departure phases. The timing of these reports is critical for maximum effectiveness. Reports should be made:

- **10 miles out**: "Millville traffic, Cherokee 1234X, 10 miles southeast, inbound for landing, runway 10, Millville."
- **Entering downwind**: "Millville traffic, Cherokee 1234X, entering left downwind, runway 10, Millville."
- **Base leg**: "Millville traffic, Cherokee 1234X, left base, runway 10, Millville."
- **Final approach**: "Millville traffic, Cherokee 1234X, final approach, runway 10, Millville."
- **Clear of runway**: "Millville traffic, Cherokee 1234X, clear of runway 10, Millville."

Departure announcements should include: "Millville traffic, Cherokee 1234X, taxiing to runway 10, Millville" and "Millville traffic, Cherokee 1234X, taking off runway 10, departing to the southeast, Millville."

> **Standard Phraseology Elements**: Each self-announce transmission should include: (1) Airport name, (2) Aircraft identification, (3) Position or intention, (4) Airport name repeated at the end.

The phraseology should be clear, concise, and standardized. Avoid unnecessary chatter or non-standard terminology that might confuse other pilots. Aircraft identification should use the full call sign on initial contact and may be abbreviated on subsequent calls if no confusion will result.

For practice instrument approaches at nontowered airports, additional reports are required. Pilots should announce: "Departing final approach fix (name) inbound" and "Approach completed/terminated." These calls help other traffic understand the pilot's intentions and avoid conflicts during missed approaches or low passes.

### Traffic Pattern Entry Methods

Proper traffic pattern entry is essential for maintaining the orderly flow of traffic and avoiding conflicts. The **FAA recommends specific entry procedures** based on the direction of arrival relative to the airport and traffic pattern.

The **preferred entry method** for aircraft arriving from the downwind side of the pattern is the **45-degree entry to midfield downwind**. This technique provides the best visibility of other traffic and allows for a smooth integration into the traffic flow [Figure 14-2]. Aircraft should approach at pattern altitude (typically 1,000 feet AGL unless otherwise published) and join the downwind leg at a 45-degree angle to the runway, aiming for the midpoint of the downwind leg.

For aircraft arriving from the upwind side of the pattern, two acceptable methods exist. The **recommended procedure** is to cross over the airport at least 500 feet above pattern altitude, proceed 2 miles beyond the airport, then descend to pattern altitude and enter on the 45-degree leg to midfield downwind. For airports with large or turbine aircraft operations, crossing altitude should be at least 2,000 feet above ground level to avoid conflicts with these aircraft's traffic patterns.

The **alternate midfield crosswind entry** involves crossing the airport at pattern altitude on a course perpendicular to the runway, then turning to join the downwind leg [Figure 14-3]. This method should only be used when traffic is light and pilots must give way to aircraft using the preferred 45-degree entry and to aircraft already established on downwind.

> **Pattern Entry Caution**: Never attempt a straight-in approach from the upwind direction or enter the pattern on base leg from the upwind side, as these maneuvers create conflicts with normal traffic flow and reduce visibility of other aircraft.

Both entry methods require careful scanning for traffic and appropriate speed adjustments to blend with existing traffic flow. Recommended pattern speeds are typically 70-80 knots for fixed-gear single-engine aircraft and 80-90 knots for high-performance retractable-gear aircraft, following manufacturer recommendations.

Pattern altitude should be maintained until abeam the approach end of the landing runway on the downwind leg. This ensures adequate obstacle clearance and maintains consistent traffic flow. Pilots should begin their descent from pattern altitude when abeam the touchdown point or as appropriate for their aircraft type and approach profile.

### Right-of-Way Rules at Nontowered Airports

Understanding and applying right-of-way rules prevents conflicts when multiple aircraft are operating in the same area. These rules, established in 14 CFR Part 91, provide a framework for determining which aircraft has priority in various situations.

**Basic right-of-way priorities** in order of precedence are:
1. Aircraft in distress
2. Balloons  
3. Gliders
4. Airships
5. Airplanes
6. Rotorcraft

When aircraft of the same category are involved, additional rules apply. **Aircraft on final approach** have right-of-way over aircraft in the traffic pattern, on the surface, or approaching to land. However, this does not relieve pilots from the responsibility to see and avoid other aircraft or to use good judgment in maintaining safe separation.

**Surface movement right-of-way** rules specify that aircraft on the ground must give way to aircraft landing or taking off. When two aircraft are approaching an intersection of taxiways, the aircraft on the right has right-of-way. Aircraft taxiing should give way to aircraft being towed or pushed back from the ramp.

In the traffic pattern, **aircraft already established** in the pattern have right-of-way over aircraft entering the pattern. When two aircraft are on approach to land, the lower aircraft generally has right-of-way, but pilots should not take advantage of this rule by cutting in front of another aircraft.

> **See and Avoid Responsibility**: Right-of-way rules do not relieve any pilot of the responsibility to see and avoid other aircraft. When in doubt, yield the right-of-way and communicate your intentions clearly.

**Departing aircraft** should not takeoff if the action would create a conflict with arriving aircraft. Similarly, arriving aircraft should not continue an approach if landing would create an unsafe situation with aircraft on the runway or in the pattern.

Special consideration should be given to **aircraft with limited maneuvering capability**, such as gliders on approach or aircraft experiencing mechanical difficulties. These aircraft may not be able to comply with normal right-of-way rules and should be given priority regardless of their position in the sequence.

The key to safe nontowered airport operations is maintaining situational awareness through proper communication, following established procedures, and exercising good judgment when conflicts arise. Pilots should always be prepared to extend their pattern, execute a go-around, or take other evasive action to maintain safe separation from other aircraft.

## AIRPORT TRAFFIC PATTERNS

Airport traffic patterns provide a standardized flow of aircraft movement around an airport, ensuring safety and efficiency for all aircraft operations. Understanding **traffic pattern components**, entry procedures, and operational techniques is essential for safe airport operations at both towered and nontowered facilities.

### Standard Traffic Pattern Components and Altitudes

The **standard traffic pattern** consists of five distinct legs that form a rectangular flight path around the runway. Each leg serves a specific purpose in the landing sequence and provides predictable aircraft positioning for other traffic.

The **upwind leg** (also called the departure leg) is the flight path directly in line with the landing runway, extending beyond the departure end. Aircraft follow this leg immediately after takeoff or during go-around procedures. The upwind leg continues until the aircraft is well beyond the departure end of the runway.

The **crosswind leg** is flown perpendicular to the landing runway, beginning when the aircraft turns from the upwind leg. This turn should commence beyond the departure end of the runway and within 300 feet of pattern altitude. The crosswind leg provides separation from the runway and positions the aircraft for the downwind leg.

The **downwind leg** runs parallel to the landing runway but in the opposite direction of landing. Aircraft should enter this leg at pattern altitude, maintaining level flight until abeam the approach end of the landing runway. The downwind leg is where pilots typically perform pre-landing checks and configure the aircraft for landing.

The **base leg** is perpendicular to the landing runway and begins when the aircraft turns from the downwind leg. This turn is typically initiated when the aircraft is approximately 45 degrees past the runway threshold. The base leg allows for altitude loss and further landing configuration.

The **final approach leg** (or simply "final") is aligned with the landing runway and begins when the aircraft turns from the base leg. The turn to final should be completed at least one-quarter mile from the runway to provide adequate time for stabilized approach establishment.

> **Pattern Altitude**: Standard traffic pattern altitude is typically 1,000 feet AGL unless otherwise established. This altitude should be maintained until abeam the approach end of the landing runway on the downwind leg.

**Traffic pattern altitudes** for propeller-driven aircraft generally range from 600 to 1,500 feet AGL at most airports. Military turbojet aircraft sometimes operate in patterns extending up to 2,500 feet AGL. The specific pattern altitude for each airport is published in the Chart Supplement U.S. (formerly Airport/Facility Directory) and must be maintained unless cloud clearance requirements necessitate deviation per 14 CFR 91.155.

### Left-Hand vs. Right-Hand Patterns

The **standard traffic pattern** is left-hand, meaning all turns are made to the left. This standardization reduces pilot workload and provides predictable traffic flow. In a standard left-hand pattern, the pilot sits on the left side of most aircraft, providing better visibility of the runway during pattern operations.

**Right-hand traffic patterns** are established at some airports due to obstacles, noise abatement procedures, or conflicting traffic flows. When a right-hand pattern is in effect, all pattern legs remain the same, but all turns are made to the right, creating a mirror image of the standard pattern.

Traffic pattern direction is indicated by several methods. **Segmented circles** with **traffic pattern indicators** show the direction of turns when there is variation from normal left traffic. These indicators consist of visual markers arranged to show whether right turns are required.

> **Pattern Direction Sources**: Check sectional charts, the Chart Supplement U.S., and visual indicators at the airport to determine the correct pattern direction. Sectional charts use "RP" to indicate right-hand patterns for specific runways.

At airports without visual indicators, assume standard left-hand patterns unless published information indicates otherwise. Pilots should verify pattern direction before entering the traffic pattern to avoid conflicts with established traffic flow.

### Pattern Entry Procedures and Techniques

Proper pattern entry is critical for maintaining safe separation and integrating smoothly with existing traffic. **Nontowered airport traffic patterns** are always entered at pattern altitude, with entry method dependent on the direction of arrival.

The **preferred entry method** from the downwind side is to approach the pattern on a **45-degree course** to the downwind leg, joining at midfield. This entry provides excellent visibility of traffic already in the pattern and allows for easy integration into the traffic flow [Figure 14-2: Preferred Entry-Crossing Midfield - PHAK Ch 14, Fig 14-2].

When arriving from the upwind side of the airport, pilots have several options. The **recommended procedure** is to overfly the airport at least 500 feet above pattern altitude (normally 1,500 feet AGL), though 2,000 feet AGL is preferable at airports with large or turbine aircraft operations. After crossing midfield and proceeding approximately 2 miles beyond the airport, scan carefully for traffic, descend to pattern altitude, then turn right to enter at 45 degrees to the downwind leg at midfield.

An **alternate midfield entry** involves crossing at pattern altitude on the crosswind leg, but this technique should only be used when traffic is light [Figure 14-3: Alternate Midfield Entry - PHAK Ch 14, Fig 14-3]. This entry requires careful scanning for traffic and clear announcements of intentions. Aircraft using this entry must give way to aircraft on the preferred 45-degree entry and to aircraft already established on downwind.

**Straight-in approaches** are technically allowed but discouraged at nontowered airports due to the potential for conflicts with standard pattern traffic. If a straight-in approach is necessary, pilots must exercise extreme caution and give way to aircraft in the standard pattern.

> **Entry Communications**: Always announce your entry intentions and position on the appropriate CTAF. Make calls at 10 miles out, when entering the pattern, and at each pattern leg.

### Traffic Pattern Speeds and Aircraft Separation

Appropriate airspeeds in the traffic pattern ensure safe separation and efficient traffic flow. **Pattern speeds** should follow manufacturer recommendations, typically falling between 70-80 knots for fixed-gear single-engine aircraft and 80-90 knots for high-performance retractable gear aircraft.

Speed management begins before entering the pattern. Pilots should adjust power and configuration while approaching the pattern to achieve appropriate entry speed. Once established on downwind, further speed adjustments should be made to blend into traffic flow.

**Aircraft separation** in the pattern relies on proper spacing and consistent speeds. Faster aircraft should extend their patterns to avoid overtaking slower traffic. Slower aircraft should be aware of faster traffic and adjust their patterns accordingly.

Visual separation techniques include maintaining awareness of aircraft positions relative to your own. When following another aircraft in the pattern, maintain sufficient distance to execute a go-around if necessary. The spacing should allow the preceding aircraft to clear the runway before you reach short final.

Pilots should avoid **pattern shortcuts** such as turning base or final too early, as these create conflicts with standard pattern traffic. Similarly, wide patterns can interfere with aircraft on parallel runways or adjacent airports.

> **Speed Coordination**: If unable to maintain pattern speed due to aircraft performance limitations, consider extending the pattern or departing to allow faster traffic to proceed.

### Special Considerations for Different Aircraft Types

Different aircraft categories have varying performance characteristics that affect pattern operations. **Light sport aircraft** and slower aircraft may need extended patterns to avoid conflicts with faster traffic. These aircraft should announce their slower speeds and be prepared to extend patterns as needed.

**High-performance aircraft** with retractable gear require additional time and distance for configuration changes. These aircraft should begin descent and configuration earlier than slower aircraft and may need wider patterns to accommodate their higher approach speeds.

**Turbine aircraft** often operate at higher pattern altitudes (1,500 feet AGL or higher) and require larger patterns due to their performance characteristics. When turbine aircraft are operating at an airport, other aircraft should remain alert for wake turbulence and consider the higher pattern altitude when overflying.

**Helicopter traffic patterns** may vary significantly from fixed-wing patterns. Helicopters can fly tighter patterns and may operate at lower altitudes. Fixed-wing aircraft should be particularly alert for helicopters that may not follow conventional pattern procedures.

**Large aircraft** operations require special consideration for wake turbulence. Smaller aircraft should allow additional time spacing when following large aircraft and consider the wake turbulence avoidance procedures discussed in the airport operations section.

Student pilots and training aircraft may execute **touch-and-go operations**, **stop-and-go landings**, or **multiple pattern operations**. Other traffic should allow additional spacing and be prepared for extended times on final approach or runway occupation.

> **Mixed Traffic Operations**: When different aircraft types share the pattern, communication and flexibility are essential. Pilots should announce their aircraft type and intentions to help others anticipate performance differences.

Weather conditions also affect pattern operations for all aircraft types. Strong winds may require crab angles that affect pattern geometry, while turbulence may necessitate increased spacing between aircraft. Pilots should adjust pattern procedures based on current conditions while maintaining safe separation from other traffic.

## WAKE TURBULENCE AND COLLISION AVOIDANCE

Operating safely in the airport environment requires pilots to understand and apply wake turbulence avoidance procedures and effective collision avoidance techniques. Wake turbulence represents one of the most significant hazards to aircraft operations, while the see-and-avoid principle remains the foundation of collision avoidance in visual flight conditions.

### Wake Turbulence Generation and Characteristics

**Wake turbulence** is an invisible aerodynamic phenomenon that affects every aircraft in flight. Understanding its generation and behavior is crucial for safe airport operations, particularly when operating near larger aircraft.

**Wingtip vortices** form as a direct result of lift generation. When an aircraft produces lift, high pressure air beneath the wing flows around the wingtip toward the lower pressure area above the wing. This creates two counter-rotating cylindrical vortices that trail behind the aircraft like horizontal tornadoes. The vortex from the left wingtip rotates counterclockwise when viewed from behind, while the right wingtip vortex rotates clockwise.

The strength of wake turbulence depends primarily on three factors: aircraft weight, speed, and wing configuration. Vortex strength increases with heavier aircraft weight and decreases with higher airspeeds. The most dangerous wake turbulence occurs when a large aircraft is operating at **heavy weight, slow speed, and clean configuration** (gear and flaps retracted). A "dirty" aircraft configuration with extended flaps and landing gear creates additional turbulence that actually helps break up the wingtip vortices more quickly.

Wake vortices remain spaced approximately one wingspan apart and initially descend at 400-500 feet per minute. Within 100-200 feet of the ground, the vortices tend to move laterally outward at 2-3 knots. Crosswinds affect vortex movement significantly—the upwind vortex moves more slowly while the downwind vortex accelerates its lateral movement.

> **Critical Timing**: Wake vortices are generated from the moment an aircraft lifts off until it touches down. They pose the greatest hazard during takeoff and landing phases when aircraft are at low altitudes and slow speeds.

### Aircraft Weight Categories and Separation Requirements

The FAA has established aircraft **weight categories** to standardize wake turbulence separation procedures. These categories determine minimum separation distances and timing requirements at airports.

**Super** aircraft weigh more than 560,000 pounds maximum takeoff weight. Currently, only the Airbus A380 falls into this category. **Heavy** aircraft have maximum takeoff weights of 300,000 pounds or more, including aircraft like the Boeing 747, 777, and Airbus A340. These aircraft use the call sign suffix "heavy" in radio communications. **Large** aircraft weigh between 41,000 and 300,000 pounds, while **Small** aircraft weigh 41,000 pounds or less.

ATC applies specific **separation standards** based on these categories. When a small aircraft follows a heavy aircraft, controllers require a minimum of 6 miles separation for en route operations and specific timing intervals for airport operations. Small aircraft must wait at least **3 minutes** before takeoff when following a heavy aircraft departure from the same runway. For landing operations, small aircraft should remain **2 minutes** behind heavy aircraft using the same runway.

At towered airports, controllers automatically apply these separation standards. However, pilots may request to waive the interval by stating "request waiver of wake turbulence separation." At nontowered airports, pilots are entirely responsible for ensuring adequate wake turbulence separation.

### Wake Avoidance Procedures and Techniques

Effective wake turbulence avoidance requires pilots to visualize vortex location and adjust their flight path accordingly. The key principle is to avoid flying in the area where vortices are likely to be present.

**Takeoff procedures** behind larger aircraft require specific techniques. Rotate **prior to the point** where the preceding aircraft lifted off, then climb above its departure path until turning clear of the wake. For intersection takeoffs, avoid flight paths that cross below a larger aircraft's climb path. When departing behind an aircraft that has executed a missed approach or touch-and-go, wait at least **2 minutes** before beginning takeoff roll.

**Landing procedures** require staying at or above the larger aircraft's approach path and touching down **beyond its touchdown point**. If landing on a parallel runway closer than 2,500 feet to the runway used by a larger aircraft, consider possible wake drift and maintain altitude above the larger aircraft's glidepath. When landing behind a departing aircraft, land **prior to** the departing aircraft's rotation point.

**Crosswind considerations** are critical for wake avoidance. Light quartering tailwinds create the most hazardous conditions because vortices may linger along the approach path and runway centerline. In crosswind conditions, approach from the upwind side when practical and be prepared to execute a go-around if wake turbulence is encountered.

Pilots should also understand that wake turbulence can be encountered **at cruise altitudes**. Research has documented wake encounters more than 30 nautical miles and 2,000 feet below heavy aircraft at high altitudes. When flying en route behind larger aircraft, maintain lateral separation and avoid flying directly behind and below.

### See-and-Avoid Principles and Limitations

The **see-and-avoid** concept forms the foundation of collision avoidance in visual meteorological conditions. However, this system has inherent limitations that pilots must understand and compensate for through proper scanning techniques and adherence to right-of-way rules.

**Visual scanning techniques** require systematic eye movements to effectively cover the entire visual field. The most effective scan involves **10-degree eye movements**, with each area observed for at least **1 second**. The central visual field provides the highest resolution for detecting objects, while peripheral vision is more sensitive to movement but lacks detail resolution.

Scanning should emphasize areas of highest collision potential: the approach and departure corridors of airports, areas where traffic patterns converge, and locations where aircraft altitudes and flight paths intersect. Pilots should develop a **consistent scanning pattern** that works efficiently with their aircraft's configuration and mission requirements.

**Limitations of visual detection** include physiological and environmental factors. The human eye has a natural blind spot, and aircraft can appear stationary when on a collision course due to the lack of relative motion. Haze, glare, and windscreen distortion can significantly reduce visibility. Dark-colored aircraft are particularly difficult to spot against terrain backgrounds.

**Right-of-way rules** provide a systematic method for determining which aircraft has priority when conflicts arise. Aircraft in distress have the right-of-way over all other aircraft. Among normal operations, **balloons have right-of-way over all other aircraft**, **gliders have right-of-way over airplanes**, and aircraft on final approach have right-of-way over other traffic in the pattern or on the surface.

When aircraft of the same category converge, the aircraft on the right has the right-of-way. When overtaking another aircraft, pass on the right and maintain well clear separation. **Head-on approaches** require both aircraft to alter course to the right. However, right-of-way rules never relieve any pilot of the responsibility to see and avoid other aircraft and to take immediate action to avoid a collision.

> **Technology Limitations**: Modern collision avoidance systems like TCAS provide valuable assistance, but they cannot detect all aircraft (such as those without transponders) and may not provide adequate warning time at low altitudes. The see-and-avoid principle remains the primary method of collision avoidance for VFR operations.

Modern pilots should also understand that increasing air traffic density and aircraft performance capabilities have placed greater demands on the see-and-avoid system. Jet aircraft closure rates can exceed 1,000 knots in head-on situations, providing very limited time for visual detection and collision avoidance. This emphasizes the critical importance of following established traffic patterns, maintaining appropriate altitudes, and using all available technology to enhance situational awareness while never abandoning the fundamental responsibility to see and avoid other aircraft.

## Summary

Review the key points from this unit:

- The FAA categorizes airports based on passenger activity, cargo operations, ownership structure, and air traffic control services to determine regulatory requirements and available services.
- Commercial service airports must accommodate at least 2,500 passenger boardings annually, while cargo service airports handle more than 100 million pounds of landed weight per year.
- Towered airports require two-way radio communication with ATC and compliance with all control instructions, while nontowered airports use CTAF for traffic advisories.
- Civil airports are open to the general public, military airports are restricted to government operations, and private airports require owner permission for access.
- Sectional charts display airports using standardized blue symbols for public-use and magenta symbols for private-use facilities, with additional data showing runway length and services.
- The Chart Supplement U.S. provides comprehensive airport information including detailed runway data, communication frequencies, and available services updated every 56 days.
- NOTAMs contain time-critical information about temporary changes or hazards that could affect flight operations and must be checked before each flight.
- ATIS broadcasts provide current weather and operational information at towered airports, reducing radio congestion and ensuring pilots have essential data.
- Runway numbers represent the magnetic azimuth rounded to the nearest ten degrees, with parallel runways designated using L, R, and C suffixes.
- Displaced thresholds allow operations on the displaced area except landing in that direction, while relocated thresholds prohibit all operations on the closed portion.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Commercial Service Airport** | Publicly owned airport accommodating at least 2,500 passenger boardings annually with scheduled service |
| **Passenger Boardings (Enplanements)** | Revenue passengers boarding aircraft in scheduled or non-scheduled air commerce service |
| **Reliever Airport** | FAA-designated airport that relieves congestion at commercial service airports and improves general aviation access |
| **CTAF** | Common Traffic Advisory Frequency used for airport advisory practices at airports without operating control towers |
| **UNICOM** | Nongovernment air-to-ground radio communication station providing airport information at public-use airports |
| **Chart Supplement U.S.** | Comprehensive publication containing detailed information on all public-use airports, updated every 56 days |
| **NOTAM** | Notice to Airmen providing time-critical information about temporary changes or hazards affecting flight operations |
| **ATIS** | Automated Terminal Information Service broadcasting recorded weather and operational information at towered airports |
| **Runway Safety Area (RSA)** | Defined surface surrounding the runway designed to reduce aircraft damage risk during runway excursion events |
| **Displaced Threshold** | Threshold located away from the runway beginning, reducing landing distance but allowing some operations on displaced area |
| **Relocated Threshold** | Temporary threshold movement due to closure, prohibiting all operations except taxiing on the closed portion |
| **Runway Holding Position Markings** | Four yellow lines across taxiway width where aircraft must stop before crossing runway without ATC clearance |
