# Unit B: Weight and Balance

Picture this: you're preparing for a cross-country flight with three passengers and their luggage, confident that your aircraft can handle the load, only to discover during your weight and balance calculations that your center of gravity is dangerously outside limits. This scenario highlights why weight and balance isn't just paperwork—it's a critical safety skill that can mean the difference between a successful flight and a potential accident. Proper weight and balance management ensures your aircraft performs predictably, maintains controllability, and operates within its designed safety envelope.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this unit, you will be able to:

- Explain the fundamental principles of weight and balance and their impact on aircraft performance and safety
- Define center of gravity, moment, and other essential weight and balance terminology
- Identify the adverse effects of improper weight distribution and loading on aircraft controllability
- Apply regulatory requirements for weight and balance compliance and record-keeping
- Calculate aircraft weight and balance using standard computational methods and formulas
- Perform weight and balance calculations for various loading scenarios, including fuel burns and passenger changes
- Determine the effects of weight shifting, adding, or removing items on aircraft balance and center of gravity limits

## WEIGHT AND BALANCE FUNDAMENTALS

**Weight and balance compliance** represents one of the most critical safety responsibilities for any pilot. Operating an aircraft outside its approved weight and balance limits can result in catastrophic structural failure, unrecoverable flight characteristics, or complete loss of control. Unlike many other flight safety factors that may provide some margin for error, weight and balance violations often lead directly to accidents with little opportunity for pilot intervention or recovery.

The **Federal Aviation Administration (FAA)** establishes weight and balance requirements through Title 14 Code of Federal Regulations (14 CFR). While Part 91 operations do not require formal weight and balance calculations before each flight, 14 CFR 91.9 mandates that pilots comply with all operating limitations specified in the approved Aircraft Flight Manual (AFM) or Pilot's Operating Handbook (POH). These limitations explicitly include weight and balance restrictions.

> **Critical Safety Note**: An aircraft that exceeds its maximum certificated weight compromises structural integrity and may experience complete structural failure during normal flight operations or moderate turbulence.

### Introduction to Weight and Balance Importance

Aircraft manufacturers establish weight and balance limits through extensive flight testing and structural analysis. These limits define the operational envelope within which the aircraft can be safely operated. The **Type Certificate Data Sheet (TCDS)** contains the official weight and balance limitations for each aircraft model, while the AFM provides detailed procedures for calculating and verifying compliance.

**Structural integrity** forms the foundation of weight limitations. Every aircraft component, from wing spars to landing gear assemblies, is designed to withstand specific load factors at maximum certificated weight. Operating above these weight limits subjects the aircraft structure to loads exceeding design specifications. During turbulence encounters, hard landings, or high-G maneuvers, an overweight aircraft may experience structural failure without warning.

**Performance degradation** occurs immediately when aircraft weight exceeds design parameters. Unlike gradual performance reductions seen with altitude or temperature changes, weight-related performance penalties are immediate and predictable. These effects compound with other performance-reducing factors such as high density altitude, contaminated runways, or atmospheric conditions.

**Control authority limitations** present perhaps the most insidious danger of improper weight and balance. An aircraft with its **center of gravity (CG)** outside approved limits may exhibit normal handling characteristics during calm conditions but become uncontrollable during critical flight phases such as approach, landing, or emergency procedures.

### Weight Control Principles and Lift Relationships

**Weight** represents the gravitational force continuously pulling an aircraft toward Earth. As established in aerodynamic principles, weight must be balanced by an equal and opposite **lift force** to maintain level flight. This fundamental relationship governs all aspects of aircraft operation and directly impacts every performance parameter.

The **lift-generating capability** of any airfoil is finite and depends on four primary factors: airfoil design, **angle of attack (AOA)**, airspeed, and air density. Aircraft manufacturers design wings to produce sufficient lift at maximum certificated weight under standard atmospheric conditions. Exceeding this weight may result in insufficient lift production, making sustained flight impossible.

**Critical angle of attack** becomes a limiting factor in overweight aircraft operations. As aircraft weight increases, higher angles of attack are required to generate sufficient lift for flight. This increased AOA requirement reduces the margin between normal flight attitudes and stall conditions. During approach and landing phases, this reduced margin significantly increases stall/spin accident potential.

**Wing loading** calculations help pilots understand lift relationships. Wing loading equals aircraft weight divided by wing area, typically expressed in pounds per square foot. Higher wing loadings require increased lift coefficients, achieved through higher angles of attack or increased airspeed. When wing loading exceeds design limits, the aircraft may be unable to generate sufficient lift for safe flight operations.

The **power-required curve** shifts significantly with weight changes. Heavier aircraft require more power for all flight phases, from takeoff through cruise. This increased power requirement may exceed available engine power, particularly at high density altitudes or during single-engine operations in twin-engine aircraft.

### Effects of Weight on Aircraft Performance

**Takeoff performance** suffers dramatically from excess weight. Overweight aircraft require higher **takeoff speeds** to generate sufficient lift, resulting in longer **takeoff runs**. The relationship is not linear – small weight increases can produce disproportionately large increases in takeoff distance requirements. Ground roll distances may increase by 15-25% for weight increases of only 10% above maximum limits.

**Climb performance** degradation presents serious safety implications, particularly during obstacle clearance scenarios. Overweight aircraft exhibit reduced **rate of climb** and decreased **angle of climb** capabilities. The **service ceiling** – maximum altitude at which the aircraft can maintain 100 feet per minute climb rate – decreases significantly with excess weight. Single-engine climb performance in twin-engine aircraft may become negative, making single-engine flight impossible.

> **Performance Planning**: Always check performance charts during preflight planning to ensure adequate margins exist for actual takeoff weight and atmospheric conditions.

**Cruise performance** penalties include reduced **cruising speed**, decreased **range**, and higher fuel consumption rates. Overweight aircraft must fly at higher angles of attack to maintain altitude, increasing induced drag and reducing fuel efficiency. Maximum range decreases both from increased drag and potential fuel load reductions necessary to meet weight limits.

**Landing performance** creates critical safety concerns during the approach and landing phases. Overweight aircraft have higher **stalling speeds**, requiring faster approach and landing speeds. These increased speeds result in longer **landing rolls** and reduced stopping margins. The faster touchdown speeds also increase tire loads and brake heating, potentially causing tire failure or brake system overheating.

**Stall characteristics** become more dangerous in overweight aircraft. Higher stall speeds reduce the margin between normal flight and stall conditions. Recovery from stalls requires more altitude, and the aircraft may exhibit more aggressive stall characteristics. Spin recovery, if certificated, becomes more difficult and may require techniques outside normal training profiles.

**Maneuverability** decreases significantly with excess weight. Higher wing loadings reduce the aircraft's ability to change direction quickly or respond to control inputs. During emergency procedures requiring rapid maneuvering, such as collision avoidance or severe weather encounters, reduced maneuverability may prevent successful recovery.

### Weight Changes During Flight Operations

**Fuel weight calculations** form a fundamental component of weight and balance planning. **Aviation gasoline (AVGAS) weighs 6 pounds per gallon**, making fuel load a significant factor in total aircraft weight. Thirty gallons of fuel equals 180 pounds – potentially more than one passenger. Pilots must account for fuel weight when loading passengers, baggage, and cargo.

**Jet fuel weights** differ from AVGAS weights. **Jet A and Jet A-1 weigh 6.8 pounds per gallon**, while **Jet B weighs 6.5 pounds per gallon**. Turbine aircraft operators must use appropriate fuel weights for accurate calculations. Temperature variations can affect fuel density, but standard weights provide adequate accuracy for most operations.

**Fuel burn calculations** require careful consideration of CG shifts during flight. Most general aviation aircraft carry fuel in wing tanks located near the CG, minimizing CG movement as fuel is consumed. However, aircraft with fuselage fuel tanks or multiple tank configurations may experience significant CG shifts during fuel consumption. Pilots must verify that the CG remains within limits throughout the planned flight.

**Unusable fuel** must be included in empty weight calculations but cannot be considered available for flight operations. **Usable fuel** represents the fuel available for flight planning. Pilots must understand the difference and ensure adequate usable fuel reserves while maintaining weight and balance compliance.

> **Fuel Management**: Monitor fuel consumption patterns to ensure CG remains within limits throughout flight, especially in aircraft with multiple fuel tank configurations.

**Equipment modifications** significantly impact aircraft weight and balance. Installation of additional radios, instruments, or optional equipment changes both empty weight and CG location. **Supplemental Type Certificates (STCs)** for equipment installations include revised weight and balance information that must be incorporated into aircraft records.

**Maintenance-induced weight changes** occur during repair and modification activities. Replacement parts may have different weights than original components. Paint schemes, interior modifications, and structural repairs can alter both weight and balance characteristics. **Advisory Circular (AC) 43.13-1** establishes criteria for negligible weight changes that do not require formal weight and balance updates.

**Standard weight criteria** for negligible changes include: one pound or less for aircraft weighing less than 5,000 pounds empty weight; two pounds or less for aircraft with empty weights between 5,000 and 50,000 pounds; and five pounds or less for aircraft exceeding 50,000 pounds empty weight. **Negligible CG changes** are defined as less than 0.05% **Mean Aerodynamic Chord (MAC)** for fixed-wing aircraft.

**Ballast requirements** may be necessary when equipment is removed or when operating conditions require CG adjustments. **Permanent ballast** becomes part of the aircraft's empty weight, while **temporary ballast** must be secured and accounted for in each weight and balance calculation. Ballast must be properly secured to prevent shifting during flight operations.

**Loading sequence considerations** become critical in aircraft with wide CG ranges or multiple loading configurations. Pilots must verify that the CG remains within limits during all phases of loading and unloading. Fuel loading sequences, passenger boarding procedures, and cargo loading patterns all affect interim CG locations during ground operations.

## CENTER OF GRAVITY AND BALANCE CONCEPTS

The **center of gravity (CG)** represents the theoretical point where all the weight of an aircraft is concentrated. It is the balance point where, if the aircraft were suspended, it would remain level in all directions. Understanding CG concepts is fundamental to safe flight operations, as the CG location directly affects aircraft stability, control response, and overall performance characteristics.

The CG is a three-dimensional point with longitudinal, lateral, and vertical positioning within the aircraft. While all three dimensions are important, longitudinal CG position receives primary attention in most weight and balance calculations due to its critical impact on pitch stability and elevator effectiveness.

### Center of Gravity Definition and Characteristics

The **center of gravity** functions as the balance point of the aircraft and determines how the aircraft will respond to control inputs and external forces. Unlike a fixed point, the CG location shifts as weight distribution changes within the aircraft. When passengers move, fuel burns off, or cargo is repositioned, the CG moves accordingly.

The CG location is measured in inches from a reference point called the **datum**. This datum is an imaginary vertical plane established by the aircraft manufacturer and serves as the zero station for all weight and balance calculations. All measurements aft of the datum are positive values, while measurements forward of the datum are negative values.

**Moment** calculations determine CG location through the formula: Weight × Arm = Moment. The arm represents the horizontal distance from the datum to the item's center of gravity. Total aircraft moment divided by total weight yields the CG location in inches from the datum.

> **Critical Concept**: The CG is not necessarily located at the aircraft's geometric center. Manufacturers position the CG slightly forward of the center of lift to ensure proper longitudinal stability and control response during flight operations.

Aircraft designers establish a **CG range** within which the balance point must fall for safe flight operations. This range is defined by forward and aft limits, typically specified in inches from the datum. These limits ensure adequate stability while maintaining sufficient control authority throughout the aircraft's operational envelope.

### Longitudinal and Lateral Balance Considerations

**Longitudinal balance** involves the fore-and-aft positioning of the CG along the aircraft's longitudinal axis. This represents the primary concern in weight and balance calculations, as improper longitudinal balance significantly affects pitch stability and elevator control effectiveness.

A **forward CG condition** occurs when the balance point is positioned ahead of the forward limit. This creates a nose-heavy condition requiring increased elevator back-pressure to maintain level flight. Forward CG loading increases longitudinal stability but requires higher control forces and reduces elevator authority for flare during landing.

An **aft CG condition** develops when the balance point moves behind the aft limit. This tail-heavy configuration reduces longitudinal stability and may create extremely light control forces. Aft CG conditions can lead to difficult recovery from stalls and potential loss of control due to reduced stability margins.

**Lateral balance** addresses weight distribution across the aircraft's lateral axis from wingtip to wingtip. While not calculated for most general aviation aircraft, pilots must understand lateral balance effects on flight characteristics and fuel management procedures.

Lateral imbalance typically results from uneven fuel consumption or asymmetric loading. When fuel is consumed unevenly from wing tanks, or when heavy items are positioned on one side of the aircraft, a wing-heavy condition develops. This condition requires constant aileron pressure or trim adjustment to maintain level flight.

The pilot can manage lateral balance through proper fuel management procedures. Most aircraft flight manuals specify fuel consumption procedures to maintain lateral balance, such as alternating fuel tank usage or specific fuel scheduling requirements for extended flights.

> **Fuel Management Alert**: Gasoline weighs 6 pounds per gallon, making fuel distribution a significant factor in both longitudinal and lateral balance. Improper fuel management can create serious balance issues, particularly in aircraft with wingtip tanks or multiple fuel systems.

### CG Limits and Range Specifications

Aircraft manufacturers establish **CG limits** through extensive flight testing to ensure safe handling characteristics throughout the operational envelope. These limits are published in the **Type Certificate Data Sheet (TCDS)** and the approved **Aircraft Flight Manual (AFM)** or **Pilot's Operating Handbook (POH)**.

The **forward CG limit** is typically established based on landing characteristics and elevator control authority. This limit ensures sufficient elevator effectiveness to flare for landing at minimum approach speeds. When structural considerations don't limit forward CG position, the limit is set where full up-elevator deflection is required to obtain the necessary angle of attack for landing.

Forward CG limits may also be established to prevent excessive loads on nose wheel during ground operations. Aircraft loaded beyond the forward limit may experience nosewheel overloading, increased takeoff speeds, and potential difficulty in rotation during takeoff.

The **aft CG limit** represents the most rearward position where adequate stability margins exist for safe flight operations. This limit is established through flight testing to ensure recovery capability from stalls, adequate longitudinal stability, and acceptable control forces throughout the flight envelope.

**CG range specifications** may vary with gross weight on some aircraft. Light aircraft often have constant CG limits regardless of weight, while larger aircraft may have CG limits that change as gross weight increases or decreases. Some aircraft also specify different CG limits for specific operations such as aerobatic flight, retractable landing gear operations, or special equipment installations.

The **Mean Aerodynamic Chord (MAC)** provides an alternative method for expressing CG limits on some aircraft. MAC represents the average distance from leading edge to trailing edge of the wing. CG limits expressed in percent MAC allow easier comparison between different aircraft configurations and aid in understanding stability margins.

> **Regulatory Requirement**: 14 CFR Part 91.9 requires pilots to comply with operating limitations in the approved AFM, including weight and balance limits. While Part 91 doesn't specifically require weight and balance calculations before each flight, compliance with CG limits is mandatory for legal flight operations.

### Stability and Control Relationships

The relationship between CG location and aircraft **stability** is fundamental to safe flight operations. **Longitudinal stability** decreases as the CG moves aft toward the aft limit. An aircraft with forward CG loading exhibits strong longitudinal stability but requires higher control forces and reduced control authority.

**Static stability** refers to the aircraft's initial tendency to return to equilibrium after a disturbance. Forward CG loading increases static stability, making the aircraft more resistant to pitch changes but requiring greater control input for maneuvering. Aft CG loading reduces static stability and may result in neutral or negative stability in extreme cases.

**Dynamic stability** involves the aircraft's response over time following a disturbance. Forward CG conditions generally improve dynamic stability characteristics, while aft CG loading may lead to oscillatory or divergent responses to control inputs or turbulence encounters.

**Control authority** varies significantly with CG position. Forward CG conditions require greater control forces but provide predictable control response. Pilots may experience difficulty raising the nose during takeoff and landing with forward CG loading, potentially requiring trim adjustments or control technique modifications.

Aft CG conditions create very light control forces that may lead to inadvertent overstressing of the aircraft structure. The reduced control forces may feel pleasant initially but can result in dangerous situations during turbulence encounters or emergency maneuvers where precise control is essential.

**Stall characteristics** change dramatically with CG position. Forward CG loading typically results in conventional stall characteristics with adequate warning and straightforward recovery procedures. Aft CG loading may produce violent stall characteristics, delayed stall warnings, and difficult or impossible recovery procedures.

> **Safety Critical**: Aircraft loaded beyond the aft CG limit may be unrecoverable from stalls or spins. The reduced longitudinal stability can result in deep stall conditions where elevator effectiveness is insufficient for recovery, regardless of pilot technique or available altitude.

**Trim requirements** increase with CG positions away from the design center. Forward CG conditions require nose-up trim to relieve control pressure, while aft CG conditions need nose-down trim. Excessive trim requirements reduce available elevator travel for maneuvering and may indicate out-of-limits loading conditions.

The relationship between CG position and **aircraft performance** extends beyond stability considerations. Forward CG loading increases takeoff distances, reduces climb performance, and raises stalling speeds. Aft CG loading may initially appear to improve performance through reduced trim drag but creates unacceptable stability margins that compromise safety.

Understanding these relationships enables pilots to recognize improperly loaded aircraft through flight characteristics observation. An aircraft requiring excessive control forces, unusual trim settings, or exhibiting poor stability characteristics may indicate weight and balance problems requiring immediate attention before continued flight operations.

## ADVERSE BALANCE EFFECTS AND SAFETY

Operating an aircraft with the center of gravity outside approved limits creates dangerous flight conditions that compromise both controllability and structural integrity. Understanding these adverse effects is critical for flight safety, as **improper balance** can transform routine flight operations into life-threatening situations. The consequences of exceeding CG limits are far more severe than simple performance degradation.

The effects of adverse balance conditions are cumulative and interact with other performance factors. What might be manageable in ideal conditions becomes catastrophic when combined with emergency situations, adverse weather, or high density altitude operations.

### Forward CG Limit Effects and Landing Characteristics

When the center of gravity moves forward of approved limits, the aircraft develops a pronounced **nose-heavy condition** that creates multiple safety hazards. The forward CG limit is typically established based on landing characteristics, as this represents one of the most critical phases of flight where control authority is essential.

**Excessive nosewheel loads** represent a primary concern with forward CG operations. The additional downward force on the nosewheel during ground operations can exceed structural design limits, potentially causing wheel assembly failure or nose gear collapse. This condition becomes particularly dangerous during hard landings or when operating on rough surfaces.

Landing characteristics deteriorate significantly with forward CG loading. **Reduced elevator effectiveness** becomes apparent during the landing flare, where pilots may find it difficult or impossible to achieve proper landing attitude. The elevator may lack sufficient authority to raise the nose adequately, resulting in flat landings that impose excessive stress on the landing gear and airframe.

**Flare difficulty** intensifies as the CG moves further forward. Pilots must apply increasing back pressure to achieve the same nose-up attitude, and in extreme cases, full elevator deflection may be insufficient to flare properly. This forces steeper approach angles and higher touchdown speeds, both of which compromise landing safety margins.

> **Critical Landing Consideration**: Aircraft loaded at or near forward CG limits require longer landing distances and may exhibit poor go-around performance due to reduced elevator authority during high angle-of-attack operations.

**Nose-over tendencies** affect tailwheel aircraft particularly severely. During ground operations, especially braking or operating on soft surfaces, forward CG loading increases the likelihood of the aircraft tipping forward onto its nose. This risk is compounded during crosswind landings where directional control inputs may destabilize the aircraft.

Higher control forces throughout all phases of flight characterize forward CG operations. Pilots experience increased workload and fatigue as continuous back pressure is required to maintain level flight. This condition reduces safety margins by making precise control more difficult and increasing pilot workload during critical flight phases.

### Aft CG Limit Effects and Stability Concerns

**Aft CG conditions** create fundamentally different but equally dangerous flight characteristics centered around reduced longitudinal stability. As the center of gravity moves aft, the aircraft becomes increasingly unstable and difficult to control, with effects that can quickly overwhelm pilot capabilities.

**Reduced longitudinal stability** manifests as the aircraft's diminishing tendency to return to trimmed flight conditions after disturbance. Normal aircraft are designed with slight nose-heavy characteristics that provide inherent stability. Aft CG loading reduces or eliminates this stability margin, requiring constant pilot attention to maintain controlled flight.

The aircraft exhibits **light control forces** that initially may seem desirable but actually represent a serious safety hazard. These reduced control forces make it easy for pilots to over-control the aircraft or inadvertently exceed design limitations. Small control inputs produce large aircraft responses, making precise flight path control extremely difficult.

**Decreased stall recovery capability** represents one of the most dangerous aspects of aft CG loading. The reduced down-load on the horizontal stabilizer decreases its effectiveness in breaking the stall. Recovery may require more altitude, more aggressive control inputs, or may be impossible if the CG is sufficiently aft.

> **Stability Warning**: Aircraft loaded beyond aft CG limits may exhibit unstable flight characteristics that cannot be controlled regardless of pilot skill level. Some configurations may be impossible to recover from certain flight attitudes.

Pitch sensitivity increases dramatically with aft CG loading. The aircraft responds more rapidly to elevator inputs, making it prone to pilot-induced oscillations and over-controlling. This sensitivity is particularly dangerous during approach and landing when precise flight path control is essential.

### Control Difficulty and Stall Characteristics

**Stall characteristics** change dramatically when the aircraft is loaded outside approved CG limits, with aft loading creating the most dangerous conditions. The normal progression from pre-stall buffet to full stall may be eliminated, replaced by sudden, uncontrollable departure from controlled flight.

With aft CG loading, **violent stall characteristics** may include sudden wing drops, rapid nose-down pitching moments, or unrecoverable spins. The aircraft may stall without warning, eliminating the pilot's opportunity to recognize and respond to pre-stall indications. Recovery techniques that work with proper CG loading may be ineffective or impossible.

**Spin characteristics** deteriorate significantly with improper balance loading. Aircraft loaded beyond aft CG limits may enter spins more readily and exhibit flat spin characteristics that make recovery extremely difficult or impossible. The reduced effectiveness of flight controls in these conditions limits recovery options.

Forward CG loading creates different but equally problematic control difficulties. **Reduced elevator authority** affects not only landing operations but all phases of flight requiring nose-up attitudes. Slow flight operations become particularly hazardous as the elevator may lack sufficient power to maintain controlled flight at normal approach speeds.

**High angle-of-attack operations** suffer from reduced control effectiveness with forward CG loading. Go-around procedures, obstacle clearance maneuvers, and emergency procedures requiring maximum performance may be compromised or impossible to execute safely.

The interaction between control difficulty and emergency procedures creates compounding safety risks. Procedures that are manageable with proper loading may become impossible when the aircraft is loaded outside approved limits. This factor must be considered during preflight planning, as emergencies cannot wait for weight redistribution.

### Structural and Operational Limitations

**Overstress potential** increases significantly with improper balance loading, particularly with aft CG conditions where light control forces make it easy to exceed design limitations. The reduced control forces can mask the magnitude of control inputs, leading pilots to inadvertently impose excessive G-loads on the airframe.

**Load factor limitations** become more critical with improper CG loading. Aircraft loaded outside approved limits may experience structural failure at lower G-loads than those with proper balance. The manufacturer's design assumes proper CG loading when establishing operational G-limits.

Operational limitations extend beyond normal flight operations to include restrictions on approved maneuvers and flight operations. Aircraft loaded outside CG limits may be prohibited from certain maneuvers, approaches, or operational procedures that would be acceptable with proper loading.

**Flutter and vibration characteristics** may change with extreme CG loading, particularly when combined with high airspeeds. The altered mass distribution can shift natural frequencies and potentially induce destructive oscillations at airspeeds below normal design limits.

> **Emergency Reality**: Emergency situations that would be manageable with proper weight and balance may become unsurvivable when combined with adverse balance conditions. Preflight planning must account for this reduced safety margin.

**Performance degradation** compounds the safety risks of improper balance loading. Reduced climb performance, longer takeoff distances, and degraded go-around capability reduce options available to pilots during emergency situations. These limitations are particularly critical at high density altitude airports or when operating near obstacles.

**Emergency situation considerations** highlight the critical importance of proper balance loading. Engine failures, system malfunctions, and adverse weather encounters all require maximum aircraft performance and controllability. Aircraft loaded outside approved limits may lack the performance margins necessary for successful emergency handling.

The cumulative effect of adverse balance conditions creates safety risks that exceed the sum of individual limitations. When combined with other performance-reducing factors such as high density altitude, contaminated runways, or emergency conditions, improper balance loading can transform manageable situations into accidents. This reality emphasizes why compliance with weight and balance limitations is not optional but represents a fundamental safety requirement that must never be compromised.

## REGULATORY REQUIREMENTS AND MANAGEMENT

Understanding and complying with weight and balance regulations is a fundamental responsibility for all pilots. The Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) has established comprehensive requirements that govern how aircraft must be operated within their certified weight and balance limitations. These regulations, combined with proper documentation and periodic verification procedures, form the foundation of safe aircraft operations.

### 14 CFR Part 91 Requirements and Pilot Responsibilities

**14 CFR Section 91.9** establishes the cornerstone requirement that the pilot in command (PIC) must comply with the operating limits specified in the approved **Aircraft Flight Manual (AFM)**, markings, and placards. While Part 91 does not explicitly require pilots to perform weight and balance calculations before each flight, the regulation mandates compliance with the aircraft's weight and balance limitations as published in the AFM.

The **pilot in command** bears ultimate responsibility for ensuring the aircraft is loaded within approved limits before flight. This responsibility cannot be delegated to ground personnel, passengers, or other parties. The PIC must verify that both the total weight does not exceed maximum allowable limits and that the center of gravity falls within the approved range.

Under Part 91 operations, pilots must have access to current weight and balance data for their aircraft. This includes understanding the aircraft's current **empty weight** and **center of gravity**, which may have changed since manufacture due to equipment modifications, repairs, or alterations. The pilot must be able to calculate the loaded weight and CG based on the actual loading conditions for each flight.

> **Critical Responsibility**: Even if ground personnel provide weight and balance calculations, the PIC remains legally responsible for verifying accuracy and ensuring compliance with limitations.

The regulation extends beyond simple compliance to encompass the pilot's duty to understand how weight and balance affect aircraft performance and handling characteristics. This includes recognizing when conditions such as high density altitude may require operation below maximum certificated weight to maintain adequate performance margins.

### Aircraft Documentation and Data Sources

Accurate weight and balance calculations depend on reliable source documentation. The primary sources of weight and balance information include the **Type Certificate Data Sheet (TCDS)**, AFM or **Pilot's Operating Handbook (POH)**, and current aircraft weight and balance records.

The **Type Certificate Data Sheet** contains the original certification data for the aircraft type, including maximum weights, CG range, and other limitations established during the certification process. The TCDS provides the regulatory foundation for all weight and balance limitations and cannot be exceeded regardless of circumstances.

The **Aircraft Flight Manual** or POH contains detailed weight and balance information specific to the aircraft's configuration. This includes loading charts, computational methods, and sample problems to assist pilots in determining loaded weight and CG position. The AFM limitations section contains legally binding operating limits that must not be exceeded.

Aircraft-specific weight and balance records maintain the current **empty weight** and **empty weight center of gravity** for the individual aircraft. These records must be updated whenever modifications, repairs, or equipment changes affect the aircraft's weight or balance. The records typically include an equipment list detailing all installed items and their contribution to empty weight and moment.

Weight and balance placards installed in the aircraft provide quick reference information for loading calculations. These placards often include simplified loading tables or graphs that allow pilots to determine if their loading configuration is within limits without detailed calculations.

> **Documentation Currency**: Weight and balance documents must reflect the aircraft's current configuration. Using outdated information can lead to incorrect calculations and potentially unsafe loading conditions.

### Weighing Requirements and Inspection Intervals

Aircraft weighing requirements vary significantly based on the type of operation and aircraft category. Understanding these requirements is essential for maintaining accurate weight and balance data over time.

**Part 125 operations** require aircraft with 20 or more seats or maximum payload capacity of 6,000 pounds or more to be weighed every **36 calendar months**. This requirement ensures that the accumulated effects of modifications, repairs, and equipment changes do not significantly alter the aircraft's weight and balance characteristics.

**Part 135 multi-engine aircraft** are also subject to the 36-month weighing requirement unless operated under an approved weight and balance control program specified in the operator's operations specifications. These approved programs may allow alternative methods for tracking weight and balance changes without periodic weighing.

Part 91 operations are not subject to mandatory periodic weighing requirements. However, **Advisory Circular 43.13-1** recommends that aircraft mechanics ensure weight and balance data accuracy during annual or 100-hour inspections. This verification helps identify any discrepancies that may have accumulated since the last formal weighing.

The weighing process must be conducted using certified scales and procedures that account for fuel quantity, oil levels, and equipment configuration. Aircraft are typically weighed in the **empty weight** condition, which includes unusable fuel, undrainable oil, and all permanently installed equipment.

Professional aircraft weighing requires specific environmental conditions and procedures. The aircraft must be in a level attitude, protected from wind, and positioned to allow accurate scale readings. All removable equipment must be accounted for, and fuel tanks should contain only unusable fuel quantities.

> **Weighing Accuracy**: Improper weighing procedures can introduce significant errors in weight and balance calculations. Only qualified personnel using appropriate equipment should conduct aircraft weighing operations.

### Negligible Weight Change Criteria

Not every modification or repair requires a complete weight and balance recalculation. **Advisory Circular 43.13-1** establishes specific criteria for determining when weight changes are considered negligible and do not require formal weight and balance verification.

For **fixed-wing aircraft**, negligible weight change criteria are based on empty weight categories:
- **One pound or less** for aircraft with empty weight less than 5,000 pounds
- **Two pounds or less** for aircraft with empty weight between 5,000 and 50,000 pounds  
- **Five pounds or less** for aircraft with empty weight exceeding 50,000 pounds

**Negligible center of gravity change** is defined as any change less than **0.05 percent of Mean Aerodynamic Chord (MAC)** for fixed-wing aircraft or 0.2 percent for rotary-wing aircraft. The **Mean Aerodynamic Chord** represents the average distance from the wing's leading edge to trailing edge and provides a standardized reference for CG calculations.

Standard parts such as nuts, bolts, washers, rivets, and similar hardware typically fall within negligible weight criteria. However, the cumulative effect of multiple small changes must be considered. If several minor modifications are made over time, their combined weight change may exceed negligible criteria.

Negligible weight standards apply only to individual modifications or repairs. They do not permit exceeding aircraft maximum weight limits or CG range limits. Even if a weight change is negligible, the aircraft must still operate within its certificated limitations.

> **Cumulative Effects**: Multiple negligible weight changes can accumulate to create significant errors in weight and balance data. Periodic verification remains important even when individual changes appear minor.

The negligible weight criteria provide practical guidelines for maintenance operations while ensuring flight safety is not compromised. Mechanics and pilots must understand these standards to make appropriate decisions about when formal weight and balance updates are required.

Aircraft manufacturers may establish more restrictive criteria than those specified in AC 43.13-1. When manufacturer guidance conflicts with advisory circular standards, the more restrictive requirements should be followed. Some aircraft types, particularly those with critical weight and balance characteristics, may require formal calculations for any weight change.

Understanding regulatory requirements and proper documentation procedures forms the foundation for safe weight and balance management. Pilots who comprehend these requirements and maintain current, accurate data are better prepared to make sound loading decisions and operate their aircraft safely within approved limitations.

## WEIGHT AND BALANCE TERMINOLOGY

Understanding proper weight and balance terminology is fundamental to safe aircraft operation. The aviation industry has established standardized definitions through organizations like the **General Aviation Manufacturers Association (GAMA)** to ensure consistency across manufacturers and operators. These terms form the foundation for all weight and balance calculations and regulatory compliance.

### Basic Weight Definitions and GAMA Standards

The aviation industry uses specific weight categories that distinguish between different aircraft loading conditions. **Basic empty weight** represents the standard empty weight plus any optional and special equipment installed on the aircraft. This GAMA-standardized term differs from the older **licensed empty weight**, which some manufacturers used prior to industry standardization efforts.

**Standard empty weight** forms the baseline for all weight calculations. This GAMA-defined term encompasses the airframe, engines, and all permanently installed operating equipment with fixed locations. It includes fixed ballast, hydraulic fluid, unusable fuel, and full engine oil. Standard empty weight specifically excludes usable fuel, oil that can be drained, crew, passengers, and baggage.

The distinction between basic empty weight and standard empty weight is crucial for accurate calculations. While standard empty weight represents the core aircraft with essential fluids, basic empty weight adds the manufacturer-installed optional equipment that varies between individual aircraft of the same model.

**Useful load** represents the total weight available for mission-specific items including pilot, passengers, baggage, and usable fuel. This weight equals the maximum allowable gross weight minus the basic empty weight. Understanding useful load limitations is essential since many aircraft cannot simultaneously carry maximum passengers, full fuel, and baggage while remaining within weight limits.

**Payload**, as defined by GAMA standards, specifically refers to the weight of occupants, cargo, and baggage, excluding fuel weight. This distinction helps pilots separate human and cargo considerations from fuel planning requirements.

### Moment and Arm Calculations

**Moment** represents the fundamental calculation in weight and balance work, expressed as the product of weight multiplied by arm distance. Moments are measured in pound-inches (in-lb) and indicate the rotational force that weight creates about the aircraft's balance point.

The **arm** or moment arm measures the horizontal distance in inches from the reference datum line to the center of gravity of any item. Arms carry algebraic signs: positive (+) when measured aft of the datum and negative (-) when measured forward. This sign convention ensures mathematical accuracy in balance calculations.

**Moment index** simplifies calculations by dividing moments by constants such as 100, 1,000, or 10,000. Aircraft with heavy components and long arm distances generate large, unwieldy numbers that become manageable through moment indexing. For example, a moment of 2,847,000 in-lb becomes 2,847 when using a divisor of 1,000.

The total moment equals aircraft weight multiplied by the distance between the datum and the center of gravity. This relationship allows pilots to determine the aircraft's balance point by dividing total moments by total weight, yielding the center of gravity location in inches from the datum.

> **Critical Calculation**: Weight × Arm = Moment. This fundamental relationship drives all weight and balance computations and must be applied to every item loaded aboard the aircraft.

### Reference Datum and Station Concepts

The **datum** or reference datum establishes an imaginary vertical plane from which all arm measurements originate. Aircraft manufacturers select this arbitrary reference point, which remains fixed throughout the aircraft's service life. Once established, all moment arms and center of gravity ranges reference this datum point, identified as station zero.

**Station** numbers identify specific locations within the aircraft by designating distance in inches from the datum. An item located at station +50 has an arm of 50 inches aft of the datum, while station -20 indicates a position 20 inches forward of the datum. This station system provides precise reference points for weight and balance calculations.

**Mean Aerodynamic Chord (MAC)** represents the average distance from wing leading edge to trailing edge. While primarily used in center of gravity expressions for larger aircraft, MAC becomes important in determining negligible balance changes. According to Advisory Circular 43.13-1, changes less than 0.05 percent MAC for fixed-wing aircraft do not require weight and balance recalculation.

The datum location varies significantly between aircraft manufacturers and models. Some manufacturers place the datum at the firewall, others at the wing leading edge, and some use points forward of the aircraft nose. Regardless of location, all calculations must reference the manufacturer's established datum for accuracy.

**Delta (Δ)** represents change in weight and balance calculations, using the Greek letter symbol to indicate value changes. For example, ΔCG indicates center of gravity movement, while Δweight shows weight change during flight operations.

### Standard Weights and Fuel Specifications

Aviation uses established **standard weights** for common fluids and materials when actual weights are unavailable. These standardized values ensure consistency across the industry but should never replace actual measurements when available.

**AVGAS** (aviation gasoline) weighs 6.0 pounds per US gallon, making it the most common fuel weight for piston-powered aircraft. This weight applies to all aviation gasoline grades including 80/87, 100LL, and 100 octane fuels used in general aviation.

**Jet A and Jet A-1** fuel weighs 6.8 pounds per US gallon, representing the standard for turbine-powered aircraft. These fuels maintain consistent weight characteristics across temperature ranges typically encountered in aviation operations.

**Jet B** fuel, less commonly used, weighs 6.5 pounds per US gallon. Oil weighs 7.5 pounds per US gallon, while water weighs 8.35 pounds per US gallon. These standard weights enable quick calculations during flight planning and weight adjustments.

> **Fuel Weight Reality Check**: Thirty gallons of AVGAS weighs 180 pounds—equivalent to one large passenger. Fuel weight significantly impacts both total aircraft weight and balance calculations.

**Fuel load** specifically refers to usable fuel only, excluding fuel trapped in lines, sumps, or unusable tank areas. This distinction prevents pilots from counting unusable fuel toward payload calculations while ensuring adequate reserves for flight operations.

**Maximum ramp weight** exceeds maximum takeoff weight to account for fuel burned during taxi, run-up, and pre-takeoff operations. Typically, aircraft burn 10-30 pounds of fuel before takeoff, depending on taxi time and engine run-up requirements.

**Maximum takeoff weight** represents the highest allowable weight at brake release for takeoff. This limitation considers structural loads, performance requirements, and certification standards established during aircraft testing.

**Maximum landing weight** may be less than takeoff weight due to structural considerations during landing impact. Aircraft exceeding maximum landing weight must burn fuel or dump fuel (where equipped) before landing to prevent structural damage.

**Maximum zero fuel weight** limits aircraft weight excluding usable fuel. This limitation prevents excessive bending moments on wing structures when fuel tanks are empty. Zero fuel weight becomes critical during cargo loading and passenger seating decisions.

**Floor load limits** specify maximum weight per square inch or foot that aircraft floors can sustain. These limits prevent structural damage from concentrated cargo loads and must be considered when loading dense items like spare parts or machinery.

The relationship between these weight categories creates operational limitations that pilots must understand. An aircraft may reach maximum zero fuel weight before reaching maximum takeoff weight, requiring fuel reduction to accommodate additional passengers or cargo. Alternatively, structural or performance limitations may prevent maximum takeoff weight operations under certain environmental conditions.

Understanding these standardized terms and their precise definitions enables pilots to perform accurate weight and balance calculations, comply with regulatory requirements, and operate aircraft safely within certified limitations. These concepts form the foundation for all subsequent weight and balance procedures and calculations required for safe flight operations.

## WEIGHT AND BALANCE COMPUTATION PRINCIPLES

The successful determination of an aircraft's weight and balance condition relies on the application of fundamental mathematical principles and systematic computational methods. These principles form the foundation for all weight and balance calculations, regardless of the aircraft type or complexity. Understanding these computational methods is essential for pilots to ensure safe flight operations and compliance with certificated limits.

The computational process involves three primary elements: weight, arm, and moment. These elements work together through basic mathematical relationships to determine the aircraft's center of gravity location. The accuracy of these calculations directly impacts flight safety, making mastery of these principles a critical pilot skill.

### Basic Mathematical Principles and Moment Calculations

The foundation of all weight and balance computations rests on a simple but critical formula: **Weight × Arm = Moment**. This relationship expresses the gravitational force that causes a tendency for weight to rotate about a point or axis. In aircraft applications, this point is the center of gravity, and understanding this rotational tendency is essential for proper balance calculations.

**Moments** are the mathematical expression of this rotational force and are measured in **inch-pounds (in-lb)**. To calculate a moment, multiply the weight of any item by its distance from the reference datum. For example, a 50-pound passenger seated 85 inches aft of the datum creates a moment of 4,250 in-lb (50 × 85 = 4,250).

The total moment of an aircraft represents the sum of all individual item moments. This total moment, when divided by the total aircraft weight, yields the center of gravity location. The mathematical expression is: **CG = Total Moments ÷ Total Weight**. This calculation provides the balance point location in inches from the datum reference point.

> **Critical Formula**: The weight and balance relationship can be rearranged to solve for any unknown variable: Weight = Moment ÷ Arm, or Arm = Moment ÷ Weight. These variations prove useful when determining required weight shifts or when working backwards from desired CG locations.

Large aircraft or complex loading scenarios often involve substantial numbers that become unwieldy in calculations. To simplify these computations, manufacturers may use a **moment index** system, dividing moments by constants such as 100, 1,000, or 10,000. This indexing reduces large numbers to manageable figures without affecting the accuracy of CG calculations. When using indexed moments, all calculations follow the same principles, but the final CG determination uses the indexed values.

### Datum Reference System and Station Locations

The **datum** serves as the fundamental reference point for all weight and balance measurements. This imaginary vertical plane is established by the aircraft manufacturer and remains fixed throughout the aircraft's operational life. The datum location varies between aircraft models and is selected for computational convenience rather than any specific physical characteristic.

Once established, the datum becomes **station zero**, and all other locations are measured as distances from this reference point. These measurements create the **station** system, where any location in the aircraft is identified by its distance in inches from the datum. For example, station 85 indicates a point located 85 inches from the datum reference.

The datum may be located at various positions relative to the aircraft structure. Some manufacturers place it at the firewall, while others position it ahead of the aircraft nose or at the wing leading edge. In some cases, the datum is placed well forward of the aircraft to ensure all measurements result in positive numbers, simplifying calculations.

Aircraft stations are typically marked at regular intervals throughout the structure, often every 10 or 20 inches. These markings assist maintenance personnel and pilots in determining the precise location of equipment, cargo, or passengers for weight and balance purposes. The station system provides a standardized method for identifying locations regardless of aircraft configuration changes.

### Balance Point Determination Methods

Determining the aircraft's balance point requires systematic calculation of all weights and their corresponding moments. The process begins with establishing the **empty weight** and **empty weight moment** from the aircraft's weight and balance records. These baseline values include the aircraft structure, engines, required equipment, unusable fuel, and full oil.

The computational method involves listing each item's weight and arm, then calculating individual moments. Consider an aircraft with the following loading:

- Empty weight: 2,100 pounds at arm 78.3 inches = 164,430 in-lb
- Front occupants: 340 pounds at arm 85.0 inches = 28,900 in-lb  
- Rear occupants: 350 pounds at arm 121.0 inches = 42,350 in-lb
- Fuel: 450 pounds at arm 75.0 inches = 33,750 in-lb
- Baggage: 80 pounds at arm 150.0 inches = 12,000 in-lb

Total weight equals 3,320 pounds, and total moments equal 281,430 in-lb. The CG location is 281,430 ÷ 3,320 = 84.8 inches aft of the datum.

> **Balance Board Analogy**: Weight and balance calculations mirror a simple lever and fulcrum system. If you place weights on opposite ends of a board balanced on a fulcrum, the board will balance when the moments on each side are equal. This same principle applies to aircraft balance.

Alternative methods include graphical solutions provided by manufacturers in the Aircraft Flight Manual. These graphs eliminate the need for manual moment calculations by providing visual representations of weight versus moment relationships. The pilot enters each loading condition on the appropriate graph, reads the corresponding moment, and plots the final values on a center of gravity envelope to verify acceptable limits.

### Positive and Negative Arm Calculations

The algebraic sign convention for arm measurements creates either positive or negative values depending on the item's location relative to the datum. Measurements **aft of the datum** receive **positive (+) values**, while measurements **forward of the datum** receive **negative (-) values**. This sign convention directly affects moment calculations and must be carefully applied.

When an item is located forward of the datum, its negative arm creates a negative moment that reduces the total moment sum. For example, if oil at 11 pounds is located at station -31 (31 inches forward of the datum), the moment calculation becomes: 11 × (-31) = -341 in-lb. This negative moment is subtracted from the total moment when determining the CG location.

The mathematical relationship follows standard algebraic rules: positive weight multiplied by positive arm equals positive moment, while positive weight multiplied by negative arm equals negative moment. These sign conventions ensure accurate representation of the item's effect on the aircraft's balance point.

Forward-located items, such as nose baggage compartments or engine accessories, commonly generate negative arms and moments. Oil reservoirs, batteries, and avionics equipment may also fall forward of the datum depending on the manufacturer's datum selection. Pilots must carefully observe the sign conventions when performing calculations to avoid errors that could result in incorrect CG determinations.

> **Calculation Check**: When reviewing weight and balance calculations, verify that items located forward of the aircraft's apparent balance point show negative arms, while items located aft show positive arms. This provides a logical check against calculation errors.

Consider an aircraft where the engine oil is located forward of the datum:
- Oil weight: 15 pounds
- Oil arm: -25 inches (forward of datum)  
- Oil moment: 15 × (-25) = -375 in-lb

This negative moment reduces the total aircraft moment, shifting the CG forward. When performing the final CG calculation, this negative moment is algebraically added to (actually subtracted from) the positive moments of other items. The proper handling of positive and negative arms ensures accurate CG determination and safe aircraft operation.

The station system accommodates both positive and negative measurements through careful notation. Stations forward of the datum may be designated as negative stations or identified with specific notation indicating their forward location. Regardless of the notation method, the arm values used in moment calculations must reflect the proper algebraic sign to ensure computational accuracy.

## WEIGHT AND BALANCE CALCULATION METHODS

Determining the weight and balance of an aircraft requires systematic application of proven calculation methods. Pilots must master these procedures to ensure safe flight operations and compliance with regulatory requirements. The three primary methods for weight and balance calculations are the **computational method**, **graph method**, and **table method**, each utilizing the fundamental principle that the center of gravity location equals total moment divided by total weight.

All calculation methods rely on the basic mathematical relationship between weight, arm, and moment. The **moment** represents the product of weight multiplied by its arm distance from the datum. When all individual moments are summed and divided by the total weight, the result determines the aircraft's center of gravity location. This CG location must fall within the manufacturer's specified envelope for safe flight operations.

### Computational Method with Step-by-Step Procedures

The computational method provides the most direct approach to weight and balance calculations using basic mathematical operations. This method requires pilots to manually calculate moments for each weight item and systematically determine the aircraft's total weight and center of gravity location.

The step-by-step procedure begins with establishing the aircraft's current empty weight and moment from the weight and balance records. The **basic empty weight** includes the airframe, engines, unusable fuel, undrainable oil, and all permanently installed equipment. This information appears in the aircraft's equipment list and weight and balance documentation.

**Step 1: List all weight items** including the basic empty weight, occupants, fuel, and baggage. Aviation gasoline weighs 6 pounds per gallon, while Jet A fuel weighs 6.8 pounds per gallon. Use actual weights when available rather than standard weights. For occupants, use actual weights rather than assuming standard weights of 170 pounds for adults.

**Step 2: Determine the arm for each weight item** using the aircraft's loading information. Arms are measured in inches from the reference datum, with positive values aft of the datum and negative values forward of the datum. Typical arm values for small aircraft include pilot and front passenger seats at 85 inches, rear seats at 121 inches, and baggage areas between 140-160 inches.

**Step 3: Calculate moments for each item** by multiplying weight times arm (Weight × Arm = Moment). Express moments in inch-pounds. For example, 340 pounds of front seat occupants at arm 85 inches produces a moment of 28,900 inch-pounds. Negative arms produce negative moments that reduce the total moment.

**Step 4: Sum all weights and moments** to determine totals. Add all individual weights to find total aircraft weight. Add all individual moments (considering algebraic signs for negative moments) to determine total moment. Verify that total weight does not exceed maximum allowable gross weight.

**Step 5: Calculate center of gravity location** by dividing total moment by total weight (CG = Total Moment ÷ Total Weight). Express the result in inches from the datum reference point. For the previous example with total weight 3,320 pounds and total moment 281,430 inch-pounds: CG = 281,430 ÷ 3,320 = 84.8 inches.

**Step 6: Verify CG falls within limits** by comparing the calculated CG location to the manufacturer's specified forward and aft CG limits. If the CG falls outside these limits, weight must be redistributed or removed before flight. The aircraft is acceptable for flight only when both weight and CG are within approved limits.

> **Computational Method Advantages**: Provides direct calculation results, requires no special charts or graphs, works for any aircraft configuration, and builds understanding of weight and balance relationships.

### Graph Method Using Manufacturer Charts

The graph method simplifies weight and balance calculations by using manufacturer-provided charts that eliminate manual moment calculations. This method proves particularly useful for complex aircraft or when performing multiple loading scenarios quickly and accurately.

**Loading graphs** allow pilots to determine moments by plotting weight values against specific aircraft components [Figure 10-7: Loading Graph - PHAK Ch 10, Fig 10-7]. These graphs typically show weight on one axis and moment on the other axis, with separate lines or curves for different aircraft components such as occupants, fuel, and baggage.

To use loading graphs, locate the weight value on the horizontal axis and draw a vertical line upward until it intersects the appropriate component line (pilot/front passenger, rear passengers, fuel, or baggage). From this intersection point, draw a horizontal line to the vertical moment axis to read the moment value. The **moment index** concept often divides moments by 1,000 to produce more manageable numbers.

For fuel calculations, separate curves represent different fuel tank configurations. Standard tanks, long-range tanks, and auxiliary tanks each have distinct moment characteristics based on their physical locations in the aircraft. Pilots must select the appropriate fuel line that matches their aircraft's tank configuration.

The **CG envelope graph** provides visual verification of loading limits [Figure 10-8: CG Moment Envelope - PHAK Ch 10, Fig 10-8]. Plot total aircraft weight on one axis and total moment (or moment index) on the other axis. The intersection point must fall within the enclosed envelope area representing acceptable weight and balance combinations.

**Normal category** and **utility category** aircraft may have different envelope boundaries reflecting operational limitations. Normal category operations permit standard maneuvers, while utility category allows limited aerobatic flight. The utility category envelope is typically more restrictive, requiring a more forward CG range.

**Interpolation techniques** become necessary when exact weight values do not appear on the graph lines. For intermediate values, estimate proportionally between the nearest plotted points. For example, if seeking the moment for 325 pounds when the graph shows 300 and 350 pounds, interpolate halfway between those moment values.

> **Graph Method Benefits**: Visual representation of loading limits, reduced calculation errors, quick comparison of multiple loading scenarios, and immediate envelope verification.

### Table Method and Loading Schedule Placards

The table method utilizes manufacturer-provided loading schedules and moment tables to determine weight and balance [Figure 10-9: Loading Schedule Placard - PHAK Ch 10, Fig 10-9]. These tables appear as placards in the aircraft cabin or within the pilot's operating handbook, providing quick reference for routine loading calculations.

**Loading schedule placards** contain tabulated moment values for standard weight increments of occupants, fuel, and baggage. These tables eliminate the need for manual moment calculations by providing pre-calculated values. Pilots simply locate the appropriate weight value and read the corresponding moment directly from the table.

Occupant moment tables typically provide moments for weight ranges in 10 or 20-pound increments. For example, a front seat occupant table might show moments for weights from 100 to 400 pounds. **Interpolation** is required for weights falling between tabulated values. When loading 185 pounds where the table shows 180 and 190 pounds, calculate the proportional moment between those values.

**Fuel moment tables** account for fuel weight at 6 pounds per gallon for aviation gasoline. These tables show both gallons and corresponding weights with their associated moments. Pilots must ensure they reference the correct fuel tank configuration, as wing tanks and auxiliary tanks have different arm distances and moment characteristics.

**Maximum moment limits** appear in the loading tables, defining the forward and aft CG boundaries for various aircraft weights. These limits typically vary with total aircraft weight, showing more restrictive CG ranges at higher weights. The table format presents minimum and maximum allowable moments for weight ranges, typically in 50 or 100-pound increments.

**Zero fuel weight limitations** appear in many transport category aircraft tables, restricting the maximum weight of the aircraft without fuel. This limitation prevents structural damage from excessive wing bending moments when fuel tanks are empty. Total weight of occupants, baggage, and cargo cannot exceed the specified zero fuel weight.

### CG Envelope Interpretation

The **CG envelope** represents the approved combinations of aircraft weight and center of gravity location throughout the operational weight range [Figure 10-8: CG Moment Envelope - PHAK Ch 10, Fig 10-8]. Understanding envelope interpretation is critical for determining acceptable loading conditions and operational categories.

**Forward CG limits** typically follow a straight or slightly curved line on the left side of the envelope. These limits ensure adequate elevator authority for landing flare and prevent excessive nose-wheel loads during ground operations. The forward limit may become more restrictive at higher weights due to elevator effectiveness limitations.

**Aft CG limits** form the right boundary of the envelope and may vary significantly with aircraft weight. These limits maintain adequate longitudinal stability and prevent dangerous tail-heavy conditions. The aft limit often becomes more restrictive at lighter weights to preserve stability margins during normal operations.

**Weight limitations** appear as horizontal boundaries on the envelope. The upper boundary represents maximum gross weight for the applicable operation category. Some aircraft have different maximum weights for takeoff, landing, and zero fuel conditions. The lower boundary typically represents minimum weight for certification compliance.

**Envelope shape variations** reflect different operational categories and configurations. Normal category envelopes permit standard flight operations, while utility category envelopes allow limited aerobatic maneuvers with more restrictive CG limits. Transport category aircraft may have additional envelope restrictions for specific operational phases.

**Loading point verification** requires plotting the aircraft's total weight and moment (or moment index) as coordinates on the envelope graph. The intersection point must fall completely within the enclosed envelope area. Points on the boundary lines are acceptable, while any point outside the envelope represents an unsafe loading condition requiring correction before flight.

**Multiple envelope interpretation** applies to aircraft with different operational categories or landing gear configurations. Some aircraft have separate envelopes for gear-up and gear-down conditions, reflecting the CG shift when landing gear retracts. Pilots must reference the appropriate envelope for the intended flight phase.

> **Critical Envelope Considerations**: Always verify both weight and moment coordinates fall within limits, understand category-specific restrictions, account for fuel burn effects during flight, and recognize that envelope boundaries represent absolute safety limits that must not be exceeded.

**Interpolation for intermediate values** becomes necessary when aircraft weight or moment values fall between printed grid lines on the envelope graph. Use proportional estimation to determine the precise location relative to the envelope boundaries. When in doubt, use conservative interpretation that maintains adequate safety margins from the envelope limits.

The envelope method provides immediate visual confirmation of loading acceptability and helps pilots understand the relationship between weight distribution and operational limitations. Proper envelope interpretation ensures compliance with both regulatory requirements and manufacturer limitations while maintaining optimal flight safety margins.

## SPECIAL CALCULATION SCENARIOS

Weight and balance calculations become more complex when dealing with special scenarios that deviate from standard loading configurations. These situations require careful analysis and precise calculations to ensure flight safety and regulatory compliance. Pilots must understand how to handle negative arm computations, zero fuel weight limitations, multiple weight configurations, and the effects of fuel burn during flight.

### Negative Arm Computations

**Negative arms** occur when components or cargo are located forward of the reference datum. This creates unique computational challenges that require careful attention to algebraic signs and mathematical precision.

Items with negative arms most commonly include engine oil systems, nose-mounted baggage compartments, and certain electronic equipment installations. In the standard weight formula (Weight × Arm = Moment), a positive weight multiplied by a negative arm produces a negative moment that must be subtracted from total aircraft moments.

Consider an aircraft with oil located at station -31 (31 inches forward of datum). Six quarts of oil weighing 11 pounds creates a moment of -341 in-lb (11 × -31 = -341). This negative moment reduces the total aircraft moment, effectively moving the center of gravity forward.

> **Critical Point**: Always verify the algebraic sign when computing moments with negative arms. A calculation error here directly affects CG location and flight safety.

When calculating total moments with negative arms present, subtract the negative moments from positive moments. For example, if positive moments total 125,000 in-lb and negative moments total -2,000 in-lb, the actual total moment is 123,000 in-lb. The final CG calculation divides this net moment by total aircraft weight.

Some aircraft have multiple items with negative arms, particularly complex installations with forward-mounted equipment. Each negative moment must be calculated separately and properly accounted for in the total moment summation before determining the final CG location.

### Zero Fuel Weight Limitations

**Maximum zero fuel weight (MZFW)** represents the maximum allowable aircraft weight excluding usable fuel. This limitation protects aircraft structural integrity by preventing excessive bending loads on wings and fuselage during flight operations.

Zero fuel weight calculations require determining the weight of the aircraft with all occupants, baggage, and cargo loaded, but without any usable fuel. This weight must not exceed the MZFW specified in the aircraft's Type Certificate Data Sheet or approved flight manual.

The calculation sequence follows this pattern: Start with basic empty weight, add pilot and passenger weights, add baggage and cargo weights, then verify this subtotal does not exceed the published MZFW. Only after confirming MZFW compliance can fuel be added to reach the desired takeoff weight.

Consider an aircraft with MZFW of 4,400 pounds. With basic empty weight of 3,230 pounds, occupants totaling 885 pounds, and baggage of 125 pounds, the zero fuel weight equals 4,240 pounds. This falls within the 4,400-pound limitation, allowing fuel addition up to maximum takeoff weight limits.

> **Regulatory Note**: 14 CFR Part 25 certified aircraft commonly specify MZFW limitations, while many smaller aircraft under Part 23 may not have this restriction published.

If zero fuel weight exceeds the MZFW, passengers or cargo must be reduced before flight. Fuel reduction cannot solve MZFW violations since the limitation specifically excludes fuel weight from consideration.

### Multiple Weight Configurations

Aircraft operations frequently involve multiple weight configurations throughout a single flight, requiring calculations for **ramp weight**, **takeoff weight**, and **landing weight**. Each configuration has specific limitations and operational considerations.

Ramp weight (also called taxi weight) represents the maximum weight for ground operations including taxi and engine run-up procedures. This weight exceeds takeoff weight by the amount of fuel consumed during ground operations, typically 20-30 pounds for most general aviation aircraft.

The progression from ramp to takeoff weight involves subtracting fuel burn during taxi operations. Standard fuel consumption for start, taxi, and run-up procedures is approximately 24 pounds for typical light aircraft operations, though actual consumption varies with ground time and engine type.

Takeoff weight must not exceed maximum takeoff weight limitations specified in the aircraft documentation. This weight determines takeoff performance calculations including ground roll distance, obstacle clearance requirements, and initial climb performance capabilities.

Landing weight calculations subtract fuel consumed during flight from takeoff weight. Maximum landing weight limitations, when specified, protect against excessive structural loads during landing operations and ensure adequate landing performance.

### Fuel Burn Effects During Flight

Fuel consumption during flight creates continuous weight and balance changes that affect aircraft handling characteristics and performance. Understanding these effects is crucial for flight planning and in-flight management decisions.

**CG shift during fuel consumption** depends on fuel tank locations relative to the aircraft's center of gravity. Most general aviation aircraft position fuel tanks near the CG, minimizing balance changes as fuel burns. However, some aircraft experience significant CG shifts requiring pilot awareness and potential loading adjustments.

Fuel located forward of the CG causes aft CG movement as it burns, while fuel aft of the CG causes forward CG movement during consumption. Aircraft with wing tanks positioned at the CG typically experience minimal balance changes throughout the flight.

Calculate fuel burn effects by determining the moment change as fuel weight decreases. For example, if 30 gallons (180 pounds) of fuel burns from tanks located 6 inches forward of the CG, the moment change equals 1,080 in-lb, causing an aft CG shift that must remain within approved limits.

> **Flight Planning Consideration**: Always verify that CG remains within approved limits at both takeoff weight with full fuel and landing weight with reserve fuel remaining.

Long-range flights or aircraft with significant fuel capacity require careful monitoring of CG changes throughout flight operations. Some aircraft flight manuals specify CG limits that vary with weight, requiring continuous compliance verification as fuel burns and aircraft weight decreases.

**Landing weight calculations** must account for planned fuel consumption plus required reserves. The final CG location at landing must fall within approved limits while maintaining adequate fuel reserves for approach, landing, and emergency requirements as specified in 14 CFR 91.151 or 91.167.

## WEIGHT SHIFTING, ADDITION, AND REMOVAL

Understanding how to calculate the effects of weight changes is essential for safe aircraft operation. Pilots frequently encounter situations requiring weight redistribution, addition, or removal to achieve proper balance. These calculations ensure the aircraft remains within approved center of gravity limits throughout all phases of flight.

### Weight Shifting Calculations and CG Changes

**Weight shifting** involves moving cargo, passengers, or equipment from one location to another without changing the aircraft's total weight. The total moments change proportionally to the direction and distance the weight moves. When weight moves forward, total moments decrease; when weight moves aft, total moments increase.

The **weight shifting proportion formula** provides a direct method for calculating center of gravity changes:

**Weight shifted ÷ Total weight = ΔCG ÷ Distance weight is shifted**

This fundamental relationship allows pilots to determine the exact CG change without recalculating total moments. The **delta CG (ΔCG)** represents the change in center of gravity position measured in inches from the original location.

Consider an aircraft weighing 8,000 pounds with a CG at station 77. If 100 pounds shifts from station 30 to station 150, the distance moved equals 120 inches (150 - 30). Using the proportion formula:

100 ÷ 8,000 = ΔCG ÷ 120
ΔCG = (100 × 120) ÷ 8,000 = 1.5 inches

The new CG location becomes 77 + 1.5 = 78.5 inches aft of datum.

> **Critical Point**: Always verify the direction of CG movement. Aft movement increases the CG station number; forward movement decreases it.

### Proportional Formulas for Weight Movement

The proportional relationship works in reverse to determine required weight shifts for specific CG corrections. When the aircraft CG exceeds limits, pilots can calculate the minimum weight that must be redistributed to achieve compliance.

For an aircraft weighing 7,800 pounds with CG at station 81.5, exceeding the aft limit of 80.5, the required forward CG movement equals 1.0 inch. To shift cargo from station 150 to station 30 (120-inch movement):

Weight to be shifted ÷ 7,800 = 1.0 ÷ 120
Weight to be shifted = (7,800 × 1.0) ÷ 120 = 65 pounds

Moving 65 pounds forward repositions the CG exactly at the aft limit. This calculation prevents excessive weight redistribution while ensuring regulatory compliance.

**Baggage compartment load redistribution** commonly utilizes these principles. Many aircraft feature forward and aft baggage areas with different arm distances from the datum. Strategic redistribution between compartments provides an effective method for CG control without reducing useful load.

The proportion formula applies regardless of weight amounts or distances involved. The relationship remains constant whether shifting 10 pounds or 500 pounds, making it universally applicable across aircraft types and loading scenarios.

### Adding Weight and CG Displacement

**Adding weight** to an aircraft changes both total weight and center of gravity location. The CG shifts toward the added weight's location, with displacement magnitude depending on the weight amount and its distance from the original CG.

The formula for weight addition effects is:

**Added weight ÷ New total weight = ΔCG ÷ Distance between added weight and old CG**

Consider an aircraft weighing 6,860 pounds with CG at station 80. Adding 140 pounds of baggage at station 150:

New total weight = 6,860 + 140 = 7,000 pounds
Distance from old CG = 150 - 80 = 70 inches
140 ÷ 7,000 = ΔCG ÷ 70
ΔCG = (140 × 70) ÷ 7,000 = 1.4 inches aft

The new CG location becomes 80 + 1.4 = 81.4 inches aft of datum.

**Passenger seating optimization** utilizes weight addition principles when determining seat assignments for balance purposes. Loading heavy passengers in forward seats counteracts tail-heavy conditions, while aft seating helps correct nose-heavy situations.

Fuel loading represents the most common weight addition scenario. Fuel weight significantly affects aircraft balance, with aviation gasoline weighing 6 pounds per gallon. Adding 50 gallons increases weight by 300 pounds, potentially shifting the CG depending on fuel tank locations relative to the current balance point.

> **Fuel Consideration**: Most small aircraft position fuel tanks near the CG, minimizing balance changes during fuel consumption. Larger aircraft may experience significant CG shifts requiring trim adjustments throughout flight.

### Removing Weight and Forward CG Shift

**Weight removal** causes the center of gravity to shift away from the removed weight's location. This creates a forward CG movement when weight is removed from aft positions, or aft movement when removing forward weight.

The calculation formula for weight removal is:

**Weight removed ÷ New total weight = ΔCG ÷ Distance between removed weight and old CG**

For an aircraft weighing 6,100 pounds with CG at station 80, removing 100 pounds from station 150:

New total weight = 6,100 - 100 = 6,000 pounds
Distance from old CG = 150 - 80 = 70 inches
100 ÷ 6,000 = ΔCG ÷ 70
ΔCG = (100 × 70) ÷ 6,000 = 1.17 inches forward

The new CG location becomes 80 - 1.17 = 78.83 inches aft of datum.

Fuel consumption during flight represents the primary weight removal scenario pilots encounter. As fuel burns, the aircraft becomes lighter and the CG shifts away from fuel tank locations. For aircraft with wing-mounted tanks near the CG, this shift remains minimal throughout the flight.

**Mathematical relationships for CG correction** require understanding directional effects. Removing aft weight moves the CG forward; removing forward weight moves the CG aft. The magnitude depends on the proportional relationship between removed weight and remaining aircraft weight.

Emergency situations may require rapid weight removal to maintain controllability or performance. Understanding these calculations enables pilots to make informed decisions about cargo jettison or fuel dumping when aircraft systems permit such actions.

Cargo loading and unloading operations frequently involve weight removal calculations. Ground crews and pilots must verify that unloading sequences maintain CG within limits throughout the process, preventing dangerous out-of-balance conditions during intermediate loading states.

> **Safety Alert**: Always recalculate weight and balance after any weight changes. Even minor adjustments can potentially place the aircraft outside approved limits, compromising safety and controllability.

These weight shifting, addition, and removal calculations form the foundation of practical weight and balance management. Mastery of these principles enables pilots to make real-time loading decisions that maintain aircraft within safe operating parameters while maximizing mission effectiveness.

## Summary

Review the key points from this unit:

- Weight and balance compliance is critical for flight safety, as operating outside approved limits can result in structural failure, unrecoverable flight characteristics, or complete loss of control.
- Aircraft weight directly affects lift requirements, with heavier aircraft requiring higher angles of attack and potentially exceeding the wing's ability to generate sufficient lift.
- Excess weight degrades all aspects of performance including longer takeoff distances, reduced climb rates, higher stall speeds, and increased landing distances.
- Aviation gasoline weighs 6 pounds per gallon while jet fuel weighs 6.8 pounds per gallon (Jet A/A-1), making fuel load a significant factor in weight calculations.
- The center of gravity represents the theoretical balance point of the aircraft and directly affects stability, control response, and performance characteristics.
- Forward CG conditions create nose-heavy aircraft with increased stability but reduced elevator effectiveness, particularly dangerous during landing flare.
- Aft CG conditions result in reduced longitudinal stability, light control forces, and potentially violent or unrecoverable stall characteristics.
- CG limits are established by manufacturers through flight testing and published in the Type Certificate Data Sheet and Aircraft Flight Manual.
- Improper balance loading can cause structural overstress, particularly with aft CG conditions where light control forces mask excessive control inputs.
- Emergency procedures become more difficult or impossible to execute when aircraft are loaded outside approved weight and balance limits.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Center of Gravity (CG)** | The theoretical point where all aircraft weight is concentrated and the aircraft would balance if suspended |
| **Datum** | An imaginary vertical plane established by the manufacturer as the zero reference point for weight and balance measurements |
| **Moment** | The product of weight times arm (distance from datum), used to calculate center of gravity location |
| **Arm** | The horizontal distance from the datum to an item's center of gravity, measured in inches |
| **Forward CG Limit** | The most forward center of gravity position where safe flight operations can be conducted |
| **Aft CG Limit** | The most rearward center of gravity position where adequate stability margins exist for safe flight |
| **Type Certificate Data Sheet (TCDS)** | Official document containing weight and balance limitations established by the FAA for each aircraft model |
| **Wing Loading** | Aircraft weight divided by wing area, typically expressed in pounds per square foot |
| **Longitudinal Stability** | The aircraft's tendency to return to equilibrium following a pitch disturbance |
| **Mean Aerodynamic Chord (MAC)** | The average distance from leading edge to trailing edge of the wing, used as reference for CG limits |
| **Static Stability** | The aircraft's initial tendency to return to equilibrium after a disturbance |
| **Usable Fuel** | Fuel available for flight planning purposes, excluding unusable fuel trapped in tank systems |
| **Ballast** | Weight added to aircraft to achieve proper balance, either permanent (part of empty weight) or temporary |
| **Service Ceiling** | Maximum altitude where an aircraft can maintain 100 feet per minute climb rate |
| **Critical Angle of Attack** | The angle of attack at which maximum lift is generated before airflow separation causes a stall |
