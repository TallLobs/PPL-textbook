# Unit D: Sources of Weather Information

Weather information is the foundation of every safe flight decision, yet with dozens of available sources—from traditional Flight Service Stations to modern weather apps—knowing where to find reliable, current data can mean the difference between a smooth flight and a dangerous encounter with unexpected conditions. As a pilot, you'll need to navigate this landscape of weather services efficiently, understanding not just what information is available, but which sources provide the most accurate and timely data for your specific flight needs. Mastering these resources will transform you from a pilot who simply checks the weather to one who strategically gathers comprehensive meteorological intelligence.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this unit, you will be able to:

- Identify the primary sources of aviation weather information and their specific applications
- Utilize Flight Service Stations effectively for weather briefings and flight planning
- Access and interpret automated weather information from AWOS, ASOS, and ATIS systems
- Navigate 1800WXBRIEF services and online weather platforms for comprehensive flight planning
- Understand how weather data is collected and transmitted through various monitoring systems
- Submit and interpret Pilot Weather Reports (PIREPs) to enhance safety for all aviators
- Evaluate weather apps and digital tools for their reliability and appropriate use in flight planning
- Select the most appropriate weather information sources based on flight requirements and conditions

## OVERVIEW OF AVIATION WEATHER SERVICES

Aviation weather services represent one of the most critical safety systems supporting flight operations worldwide. The complexity of weather phenomena and their potential impact on aircraft operations requires a comprehensive, coordinated approach to weather data collection, analysis, and dissemination. Understanding the structure and capabilities of this multi-layered system is essential for pilots to make informed flight decisions.

### Multi-agency Weather Service System

The United States aviation weather system operates as a **multi-agency collaborative network** that combines the expertise and resources of several government organizations and private entities. This integrated approach ensures comprehensive coverage, redundant data sources, and specialized services tailored to aviation needs.

The **National Weather Service (NWS)**, **Federal Aviation Administration (FAA)**, and **Department of Defense (DOD)** form the core of this system, with each agency contributing unique capabilities and resources. Private weather service providers and international organizations further enhance the system's reach and accuracy. This collaborative structure eliminates single points of failure and provides pilots with access to weather information from multiple independent sources.

The system operates on a 24-hour basis, with continuous data collection, processing, and dissemination. Weather observations are taken every hour at most locations, with special observations issued when significant changes occur. Forecasts are updated multiple times daily, and hazardous weather advisories are issued immediately when conditions warrant.

> **Critical Safety Note**: While weather forecasts are not 100 percent accurate, the multi-source approach significantly improves reliability and gives pilots access to the most comprehensive weather picture available.

### Role of NWS, FAA, DOD, and International Organizations

The **National Weather Service** serves as the primary meteorological authority for the United States, responsible for collecting surface and upper-air observations, issuing forecasts, and providing specialized aviation weather products. The NWS operates the nationwide network of **Weather Surveillance Radar-1988 Doppler (WSR-88D)** sites, commonly known as **NEXRAD**, which provides detailed precipitation and wind information across the continental United States.

NWS responsibilities include issuing **Terminal Aerodrome Forecasts (TAFs)**, **Area Forecasts (FAs)**, and hazardous weather advisories such as **SIGMETs** and **AIRMETs**. The service operates **Weather Forecast Offices (WFOs)** strategically located across the country, each responsible for weather services in their designated area. The NWS also maintains the upper-air observation network, launching approximately 75,000 radiosondes annually to gather critical atmospheric data.

The **Federal Aviation Administration** integrates weather information into air traffic control systems and provides specialized aviation weather services. The FAA operates **Terminal Doppler Weather Radar (TDWR)** systems at major airports to detect wind shear, microbursts, and other hazardous weather phenomena in terminal areas. These systems provide real-time alerts to air traffic controllers, who can immediately warn pilots of dangerous conditions.

FAA responsibilities extend to operating **Flight Service Stations (FSS)**, which serve as the primary source for pilot weather briefings. The agency also maintains **Automated Weather Observing Systems (AWOS)** and **Automated Surface Observing Systems (ASOS)** at airports nationwide. The FAA's **Center Weather Service Units (CWSUs)** provide specialized weather support to Air Route Traffic Control Centers, ensuring controllers have current weather information for en route traffic management.

The **Department of Defense** contributes significant resources to the aviation weather system through military weather services. The **Air Force Weather Agency (AFWA)** operates weather stations worldwide and provides specialized forecasting support for military operations. DOD's global network of weather observations enhances the overall database available to civilian aviation, particularly for international flight planning.

**International organizations** play crucial roles in global weather services coordination. The **International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO)** establishes worldwide standards for aviation weather services, ensuring consistency in observation procedures, forecast formats, and communication protocols. The **World Meteorological Organization (WMO)** coordinates global weather data exchange, enabling seamless sharing of observations and forecasts across national boundaries.

Regional organizations contribute specialized services for their geographic areas. **Environment and Climate Change Canada** provides weather services for Canadian airspace, while **Météo-France** serves European regions. These organizations follow ICAO standards while adapting services to local conditions and requirements.

### Weather Data Collection and Distribution Network

The aviation weather data collection network represents one of the most comprehensive monitoring systems ever developed. **Surface weather observations** form the foundation of this network, with over 1,000 locations across the United States providing hourly reports of current conditions. These observations include wind, visibility, precipitation, cloud coverage, temperature, dew point, and atmospheric pressure.

**Automated weather stations** provide the majority of surface observations. **ASOS** installations at major airports can detect and report precipitation type, intensity, and accumulation. These systems use sophisticated sensors to measure cloud ceiling heights up to 12,000 feet and visibility conditions. When unusual or rapidly changing conditions occur, **SPECI** reports supplement the regular hourly observations.

**Upper-air observations** provide critical three-dimensional weather data through multiple collection methods. **Radiosonde observations** involve releasing instrument packages attached to weather balloons twice daily from approximately 90 locations across North America. These balloons ascend to altitudes exceeding 100,000 feet, transmitting temperature, humidity, pressure, and wind data throughout their flight.

The **Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay (AMDAR)** program utilizes commercial aircraft as mobile weather stations. Participating airlines automatically transmit wind, temperature, and turbulence data during all phases of flight. This system provides approximately 230,000 weather observations daily on a worldwide basis, significantly enhancing the upper-air database in areas with limited conventional observations.

**Pilot Reports (PIREPs)** remain the only real-time source of information about turbulence, icing, and actual cloud conditions. These reports provide ground truth that cannot be obtained through automated systems. When ceilings are below 5,000 feet or visibility is five miles or less, ATC facilities actively solicit PIREPs from pilots in the affected areas.

The distribution network ensures weather information reaches users through multiple pathways. **Flight Service Stations** provide personalized briefings via the national toll-free number 1-800-WX-BRIEF. **Hazardous Inflight Weather Advisory Service (HIWAS)** broadcasts continuous updates of hazardous weather information over selected VOR navigation aids, identified on charts by an "H" symbol in the upper right corner of the facility identification box.

Digital distribution methods include **Flight Information Service-Broadcast (FIS-B)**, which transmits weather products through the **Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast (ADS-B)** network. This system provides cockpit displays of METARs, TAFs, NEXRAD imagery, and other critical weather products directly to properly equipped aircraft.

### Importance of Weather Information for Flight Safety

Weather information serves as the cornerstone of aviation safety, directly influencing every aspect of flight operations from preflight planning through post-flight analysis. The relationship between weather conditions and aircraft performance affects all phases of flight, making accurate, timely weather information essential for safe operations.

**Preflight planning** relies heavily on comprehensive weather analysis to determine route feasibility, fuel requirements, and alternate airport selection. Pilots must evaluate current conditions along the entire route, forecast conditions at estimated arrival times, and potential weather developments that could affect the flight. This analysis helps pilots make the fundamental **go/no-go decision** that represents the first critical safety checkpoint.

Weather information enables pilots to select appropriate altitudes for optimal conditions and fuel efficiency while avoiding hazardous phenomena. **Winds aloft forecasts** allow accurate flight time calculations and fuel planning. Knowledge of forecast turbulence levels helps pilots choose altitudes that provide passenger comfort and reduce aircraft stress. **Freezing level information** is crucial for avoiding structural icing conditions that can severely degrade aircraft performance.

**En route weather updates** provide dynamic information that allows pilots to adapt to changing conditions. Real-time weather radar displays show precipitation intensity and movement, enabling course deviations around hazardous areas. **SIGMETs** and **AIRMETs** alert pilots to developing dangerous conditions such as severe turbulence, thunderstorms, or widespread low visibility.

The economic impact of weather information extends beyond safety considerations. Accurate forecasts enable airlines to optimize routes, reduce fuel consumption, and minimize delays. **Terminal Aerodrome Forecasts** help pilots determine when conditions will improve sufficiently for departure or arrival, reducing costly diversions and delays.

Modern cockpit weather displays provide unprecedented access to comprehensive weather information, but they also require thorough understanding of their limitations and proper interpretation. **NEXRAD** displays show where precipitation was located when the data was collected, typically 5-20 minutes before display. Pilots must understand this latency and use the information for strategic planning rather than tactical storm penetration decisions.

The integration of multiple weather information sources creates a comprehensive safety net that dramatically reduces weather-related accidents. However, this system requires pilots to understand each source's strengths, limitations, and appropriate applications. The most effective weather decision-making combines official forecasts, real-time observations, pilot reports, and onboard weather detection systems to create a complete operational picture.

> **Regulatory Requirement**: FAR 91.103 requires pilots to become familiar with all available information concerning the flight, including weather reports and forecasts. This regulation emphasizes that weather information gathering is not optional but a mandatory part of flight planning and safety management.

The continuous evolution of weather detection and reporting technology provides increasingly accurate and timely information to support flight operations. Understanding how to effectively use this information represents one of the most important skills pilots must develop and maintain throughout their aviation careers.

## FLIGHT SERVICE STATIONS (FSS)

**Flight Service Stations (FSS)** represent the cornerstone of the aviation weather service system, serving as the primary source for preflight weather information and inflight weather advisory services. These government-operated facilities provide comprehensive weather briefing services to pilots 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, ensuring that critical weather information is always available when needed for safe flight operations.

The FSS network operates as part of the broader aviation weather service infrastructure, working in coordination with the National Weather Service (NWS), Federal Aviation Administration (FAA), and other weather service providers. This integrated approach ensures that pilots receive the most current and accurate weather information available from multiple sources and observation networks.

### FSS Structure and Operations

The modern FSS system operates through a consolidated network of facilities that provide nationwide coverage from strategically located centers. Each FSS maintains **area of responsibility** coverage that encompasses multiple states or large geographical regions, replacing the previous system of individual airport-based flight service stations.

**Automated Flight Service Stations (AFSS)** utilize advanced computer systems and databases to access real-time weather information from multiple sources simultaneously. These systems integrate data from METAR observations, pilot reports, radar imagery, satellite data, and forecast products to provide comprehensive weather briefings.

The consolidation of FSS operations has enhanced efficiency while maintaining the personal service that pilots depend on. Each facility is staffed by certified **Flight Service Specialists** who undergo extensive training in meteorology, aviation weather products, and briefing procedures. These specialists have access to the same weather databases used by meteorologists and can interpret complex weather patterns for pilots of all experience levels.

FSS facilities maintain direct communication links with Air Route Traffic Control Centers (ARTCC), Terminal Radar Approach Control (TRACON) facilities, and airport traffic control towers. This connectivity enables real-time coordination of weather information and ensures that hazardous weather advisories reach pilots through multiple channels.

> **Important**: FSS facilities are designated in the Chart Supplement U.S. (formerly Airport/Facility Directory) with complete contact information, radio frequencies, and operational details for each facility's area of responsibility.

### 1-800-WX-BRIEF Telephone Briefing Service

The **1-800-WX-BRIEF** service provides universal access to FSS weather briefing services from anywhere within the United States and Puerto Rico. This toll-free telephone system automatically routes calls to the appropriate FSS facility based on the caller's location or intended departure point.

When calling 1-800-WX-BRIEF, the system may initially connect callers to an automated menu that helps direct calls to the appropriate service. Pilots can typically bypass automated systems by pressing "0" or stating "briefer" to reach a flight service specialist directly. The system maintains high availability standards, with backup routing ensuring that calls reach an operational FSS even if the primary facility experiences temporary outages.

**Required Information for Weather Briefings** includes specific details that enable flight service specialists to provide the most relevant and comprehensive briefing possible. Pilots should be prepared to provide the following information:

- **Aircraft identification** (N-number or call sign)
- **Type of aircraft** (affects icing, turbulence, and performance considerations)
- **Departure point** (airport identifier)
- **Route of flight** (including intermediate stops)
- **Destination** (airport identifier)
- **Flight altitude** (planned cruising altitude)
- **Estimated time of departure (ETD)**
- **Estimated time en route (ETE)**
- **Type of flight operation** (VFR or IFR)
- **Type of briefing requested** (standard, abbreviated, or outlook)

This information is recorded in the FSS system and can be referenced later for flight plan filing, amendments, or search and rescue operations if an aircraft becomes overdue. The specialist uses this data to tailor the briefing to the specific route, aircraft capabilities, and flight conditions.

### Types of Weather Briefings Available

FSS specialists provide three distinct types of weather briefings, each designed for specific circumstances and timing relative to the planned departure.

**Standard Briefing** provides the most comprehensive weather information and should be requested for all flights during the preflight planning stage. This briefing follows a specific sequence to ensure all critical information is covered systematically:

1. **Adverse Conditions** - The briefer begins with any information that might influence the decision to cancel or substantially alter the route of flight. This includes significant meteorological conditions such as thunderstorms, aircraft icing, turbulence, or low ceilings and visibility. Non-weather items such as airport closures, NOTAM information affecting airports or navigation aids, and temporary flight restrictions are also highlighted.

2. **VFR Flight Not Recommended** - When weather conditions along the route are below VFR minimums or when conditions make VFR flight doubtful, the specialist will specifically state "VFR flight not recommended." This advisory carries significant weight and should be carefully considered by pilots planning VFR operations.

3. **Synopsis** - A brief overview of the larger weather picture, including the location and movement of fronts, pressure systems, and significant weather patterns that affect the general area of the proposed flight.

4. **Current Conditions** - Surface weather observations for departure, destination, and intermediate airports along the route. Current conditions are omitted if the proposed departure time is more than 2 hours away from the briefing time.

5. **En Route Forecast** - A summary of forecast weather conditions along the proposed route of flight, including expected ceilings, visibility, precipitation, and other significant weather phenomena.

6. **Destination Forecast** - Specific forecast weather conditions expected at the destination airport at the estimated time of arrival, including any significant changes expected during the arrival timeframe.

7. **Winds and Temperatures Aloft** - Forecast wind direction and velocity at specific altitudes along the route of flight. Temperature information is provided upon request and is particularly important for performance calculations and icing considerations.

8. **NOTAMs** - Current Notice to Airmen information pertinent to the route of flight that has not been published in the NOTAM publication. Published NOTAMs are provided only upon specific request.

9. **ATC Delays** - Information about any known Air Traffic Control delays that might affect the flight, particularly at major terminal areas.

10. **Additional Information** - FSS radio frequencies for opening flight plans, contacting En Route Flight Advisory Service (EFAS), and any other requested information.

**Abbreviated Briefing** serves as an updated or shortened version of the standard briefing. This type of briefing should be requested when:
- A previous briefing has been obtained and only updated information is needed
- Departure has been delayed and current information is required
- Only specific weather elements are needed (such as current conditions at the destination)

When requesting an abbreviated briefing, pilots must inform the specialist of the time and source of the previous briefing to ensure that no critical information is inadvertently omitted. Specialists will focus on any significant changes since the previous briefing and provide updates on the specific items requested.

**Outlook Briefing** is designed for flight planning purposes when the proposed departure time is 6 hours or more in the future. This briefing provides:
- General weather patterns and trends
- Forecast information limited in scope due to the extended time frame
- Initial planning information that can influence route selection and go/no-go decisions

Outlook briefings are based on weather trends and existing conditions but may not include specific terminal forecasts or precise timing of weather events. Pilots who receive outlook briefings should obtain a current standard briefing closer to the actual departure time to ensure they have the most recent and accurate information available.

> **Best Practice**: Even experienced pilots should request standard briefings for unfamiliar routes or when significant weather is forecast. The systematic approach ensures no critical information is overlooked.

### Inflight Weather Advisory Services

FSS facilities provide continuous inflight weather advisory services to aircraft operating within their area of responsibility. These services include real-time weather updates, hazardous weather advisories, and assistance with route deviations due to weather.

**En Route Flight Advisory Service (EFAS)**, commonly known as "Flight Watch," operates on the discrete VHF frequency 122.0 MHz. This service is specifically designed to provide weather information to aircraft in flight and operates during daylight hours in most areas. Flight Watch specialists have access to the same weather databases as preflight briefers but focus specifically on current conditions and short-term forecasts relevant to airborne aircraft.

Pilots contact Flight Watch using the format: "Flight Watch, [aircraft identification], [location], [altitude]." The service provides:
- Current weather observations along the route of flight
- Pilot reports from other aircraft in the area
- Radar weather information and intensity levels
- Hazardous weather advisories and updates
- Assistance with weather deviation routing

**Hazardous Inflight Weather Advisory Service (HIWAS)** provides continuous broadcast of hazardous weather information over selected VOR navigation aids. VOR facilities equipped with HIWAS capability are identified on aeronautical charts with the letter "H" in the upper right corner of the frequency box [Figure 13-4: HIWAS availability shown on sectional chart - PHAK Ch 13, Fig 13-4].

HIWAS broadcasts include:
- AIRMET (WA) advisories
- SIGMET (WS) advisories  
- Convective SIGMET (WST) advisories
- Urgent pilot reports (PIREP)
- Center Weather Advisories (CWA)

The broadcasts are automatically updated as new information becomes available. However, HIWAS is designed as an alerting service, and pilots should contact FSS or Flight Watch for detailed weather information and specific recommendations.

### FSS Responsibilities and Limitations

FSS specialists operate within specific regulatory and operational frameworks that define both their capabilities and limitations. Understanding these parameters helps pilots use FSS services most effectively.

**Primary Responsibilities** of FSS include:
- Providing weather briefings based on current observations and forecasts
- Accepting and processing flight plans
- Providing inflight weather advisory services
- Coordinating search and rescue operations for overdue aircraft
- Disseminating NOTAMs and airport operational information
- Assisting pilots with flight planning and navigation questions

**Weather Information Sources** available to FSS specialists include:
- Current surface weather observations (METAR/SPECI)
- Terminal aerodrome forecasts (TAF)
- Area forecasts (FA)
- Pilot reports (PIREP)
- Radar weather imagery (NEXRAD)
- Satellite weather imagery
- Winds and temperatures aloft forecasts
- All categories of inflight weather advisories

**Limitations and Constraints** that pilots should understand:
- FSS specialists interpret and relay weather information but do not generate original forecasts
- Weather briefings are based on the best available information at the time, but conditions can change rapidly
- Specialists cannot provide specific recommendations about whether to conduct a flight - this decision remains with the pilot-in-command
- Radar weather information available to FSS may have time delays and resolution limitations
- FSS services may be affected during periods of high demand or equipment outages

**24-Hour Availability** is maintained through the consolidated FSS network, with backup systems ensuring continuous service availability. During overnight hours or periods of reduced staffing, calls may be answered by FSS facilities in different time zones, but specialists have access to the same weather databases and can provide complete services for any area within the United States.

The **Chart Supplement U.S.** contains detailed information about FSS facilities, including:
- Primary and backup telephone numbers
- Radio frequencies for air-to-ground communication
- Hours of operation for specific services
- Area of responsibility boundaries
- Special procedures or limitations for each facility

> **Regulatory Requirement**: While weather briefings are not legally required for all flights, obtaining appropriate weather information is a fundamental responsibility of every pilot-in-command. FSS briefings provide documented evidence that proper preflight planning was conducted.

FSS weather briefing services represent a critical component of aviation safety infrastructure. The combination of 24-hour availability, comprehensive weather information access, and trained specialist interpretation provides pilots with professional weather support throughout all phases of flight operations. Proper utilization of these services significantly enhances flight safety and operational efficiency.

## AUTOMATED WEATHER INFORMATION SERVICES

Beyond the traditional Flight Service Station briefings covered in the previous section, pilots have access to several automated weather information services that provide continuous, updated weather data through various delivery methods. These automated services complement standard briefings by offering readily accessible preliminary weather information and continuous updates during flight operations.

**Automated weather information services** represent a network of unattended systems that broadcast meteorological data through telephone recordings and radio transmissions. While these services provide valuable weather information, they are designed to supplement, not replace, comprehensive weather briefings from qualified Flight Service Station specialists.

### TELEPHONE INFORMATION BRIEFING SERVICE (TIBS)

The **Telephone Information Briefing Service (TIBS)** provides automated telephone recordings of meteorological and aeronautical information through the Flight Service Station network. This service offers pilots convenient access to preliminary weather data without requiring direct interaction with FSS specialists.

TIBS operates as a system of prerecorded weather briefings that are automatically updated as conditions change. The recordings typically include area and route briefings covering surface observations, terminal forecasts, winds aloft, and airspace procedures. Special announcements regarding airport closures, navigation aid outages, or significant weather developments are also incorporated into TIBS recordings when relevant.

Access to TIBS requires a touchtone telephone and is available 24 hours daily. The service provides menu-driven options allowing callers to select specific geographic areas or route-oriented briefings. Telephone numbers for TIBS services are published in the Chart Supplement U.S. (formerly the Airport/Facility Directory) and are typically organized by geographic regions or major metropolitan areas.

> **Important Limitation**: TIBS is designed as a preliminary briefing tool only and should not be considered a substitute for obtaining a standard weather briefing from an FSS specialist, particularly for complex or potentially hazardous weather situations.

The recordings include current surface observations from automated weather stations, terminal aerodrome forecasts for major airports, and area forecasts covering broader geographic regions. Pilots planning cross-country flights can access route-specific information that encompasses multiple forecast areas along their intended path of flight.

TIBS updates occur automatically when new weather data becomes available from the National Weather Service or when significant changes in conditions warrant immediate revision of recorded information. The system provides time stamps indicating when recordings were last updated, allowing users to assess the currency of the information.

### HAZARDOUS INFLIGHT WEATHER ADVISORY SERVICE (HIWAS)

The **Hazardous Inflight Weather Advisory Service (HIWAS)** provides continuous automated broadcasts of hazardous weather information over selected VOR navigational aids throughout the 48 contiguous United States. This service enables pilots to receive critical weather updates during flight without requiring radio contact with Flight Service Stations or Air Traffic Control facilities.

HIWAS broadcasts include **AIRMETs**, **SIGMETs**, **Convective SIGMETs**, and urgent pilot weather reports (PIREPs) that describe potentially hazardous flight conditions. The system automatically updates these broadcasts as new advisories are issued or existing advisories are amended or cancelled. This continuous updating ensures that pilots receive the most current hazardous weather information available.

VOR facilities equipped with HIWAS capability are clearly identified on sectional charts and other aeronautical charts by the letter "**H**" displayed in the upper right corner of the VOR identification box. [Figure 13-4: HIWAS availability is shown on sectional chart - PHAK Ch 13, Fig 13-4] This symbolic designation allows pilots to quickly identify which navigation aids provide HIWAS broadcasts during flight planning and en route operations.

To access HIWAS information, pilots tune their VOR receivers to the appropriate frequency and listen for the continuous weather broadcasts. The broadcasts operate on the same frequency as the VOR navigation signal but utilize the voice communication capability of the facility. HIWAS transmissions do not interfere with the navigation functions of the VOR.

The service covers hazardous weather phenomena that affect aviation safety, including moderate or severe icing conditions, moderate or severe turbulence, convective activity, and low-level wind shear. Surface wind conditions of 30 knots or greater, low ceilings and visibilities that create instrument flight conditions, and mountain obscuration are also included in HIWAS broadcasts when applicable.

> **Operational Note**: While HIWAS provides valuable hazardous weather information, pilots should contact Flight Service Stations or En Route Flight Advisory Service (EFAS) for additional details, clarification, or expanded weather briefings based on HIWAS advisories.

HIWAS broadcasts are structured to provide concise, aviation-specific information about weather hazards. The broadcasts identify the type of advisory (AIRMET, SIGMET, or Convective SIGMET), the geographic area affected, the time period of validity, and specific details about the hazardous conditions. This standardized format enables pilots to quickly assess the relevance of broadcast information to their specific flight operations.

### TRANSCRIBED WEATHER BROADCAST (TWEB) - ALASKA ONLY

**Transcribed Weather Broadcast (TWEB)** services operate exclusively in Alaska, providing continuous automated broadcasts of meteorological and aeronautical data over selected low/medium frequency (L/MF) and VHF omnidirectional range (VOR) navigational aid facilities. This service addresses the unique weather briefing challenges faced by pilots operating in Alaska's vast and often remote geographic areas.

TWEB broadcasts in Alaska include comprehensive weather information covering adverse weather conditions, surface weather observations, pilot reports, and density altitude statements when applicable. The recordings may also incorporate synoptic weather information, winds aloft forecasts, en route and terminal forecast data, and radar weather reports when available from Alaska weather facilities.

The automated nature of TWEB ensures continuous availability of weather information, particularly important in Alaska where Flight Service Station coverage may be limited in remote areas. Broadcasts are automatically updated as new weather data becomes available from National Weather Service offices and automated weather observation stations throughout Alaska.

**TEL-TWEB** provides telephone access to TWEB recordings at selected locations throughout Alaska. This service allows pilots to access the same weather information broadcast over navigation aids through standard telephone connections. TEL-TWEB telephone numbers are published in the Alaska Chart Supplement U.S. and provide an alternative method for accessing TWEB information during preflight planning.

TWEB broadcasts typically cover large geographic areas due to Alaska's size and the sparse distribution of weather observation points. Pilots must understand that broadcast information may encompass hundreds of square miles, and local weather conditions can vary significantly within the coverage area of a single TWEB transmission.

The service includes special emphasis on weather phenomena particularly relevant to Alaska operations, including mountain obscuration, icing conditions, and extreme temperature variations. Coastal weather information addresses unique maritime influences on weather patterns, while interior broadcasts focus on continental weather characteristics and seasonal variations.

### AUTOMATED WEATHER BROADCAST LIMITATIONS

All automated weather information services operate within specific limitations that pilots must understand to use these services effectively and safely. These limitations stem from the automated nature of the systems, the age of weather data, and the inherent constraints of broadcast formats.

**Data Currency** represents a primary limitation of automated weather services. Weather observations and forecasts included in automated broadcasts reflect conditions at the time of data collection and processing, which may be several minutes to hours old depending on the specific weather product. Surface observations may be relatively current, while area forecasts could be several hours old.

**Geographic Coverage** limitations vary among different automated services. TIBS provides regional coverage but may not include specific details about local weather phenomena or rapidly changing conditions affecting individual airports. HIWAS covers broad areas but focuses specifically on hazardous weather rather than complete weather pictures.

**Content Limitations** affect all automated weather services. These systems cannot provide the interpretive analysis and pilot-specific recommendations available from Flight Service Station specialists. Automated broadcasts present weather data in standardized formats that may not address unique aspects of individual flight operations or aircraft capabilities.

**Update Frequency** varies among different weather products included in automated broadcasts. Some information, such as surface observations from automated weather stations, may update every few minutes, while other products like area forecasts may remain unchanged for several hours. Pilots cannot determine update frequency for specific weather elements without consulting publication schedules.

**Technical Limitations** can affect the reliability and accessibility of automated weather services. Telephone-based services may experience busy signals during periods of high demand, particularly when weather conditions create increased pilot demand for weather information. Radio-based services may be affected by propagation conditions or equipment maintenance.

### INTEGRATION WITH STANDARD BRIEFING SERVICES

Automated weather information services function most effectively when integrated with comprehensive weather briefings from qualified Flight Service Station specialists. This integration approach maximizes the benefits of both automated convenience and human expertise in weather interpretation and flight advisory services.

**Preflight Planning** should begin with automated weather services to obtain preliminary weather information and identify potential weather hazards or favorable conditions along the intended route of flight. TIBS recordings can provide initial area and route information, while sectional charts can identify HIWAS-equipped VOR facilities for en route weather monitoring.

Following preliminary review of automated weather information, pilots should obtain standard, abbreviated, or outlook briefings from FSS specialists. The FSS briefer can provide interpretive analysis of weather data, identify potential weather developments not fully addressed in automated broadcasts, and recommend specific actions based on individual flight characteristics.

**En Route Weather Monitoring** combines HIWAS broadcasts with Flight Watch (En Route Flight Advisory Service) contacts to maintain current weather awareness throughout flight operations. HIWAS provides continuous hazardous weather updates, while Flight Watch offers pilot-specific weather interpretation and recommendations for route deviations or altitude changes.

**Weather Decision Making** benefits from combining automated weather information with specialist interpretation and pilot judgment. Automated services provide factual weather data, FSS specialists offer professional interpretation and recommendations, and pilots apply this information to their specific aircraft capabilities and operating limitations.

The most effective weather briefing strategy utilizes automated services for preliminary information gathering and continuous monitoring while relying on FSS specialists for comprehensive analysis and flight-specific recommendations. This approach ensures that pilots receive both the convenience of automated access and the expertise of professional weather briefers.

> **Best Practice**: Use automated weather services to identify general weather patterns and potential hazards, then consult with FSS specialists for detailed interpretation and flight-specific recommendations before making critical weather-related flight decisions.

## 1800WXBRIEF AND ONLINE WEATHER RESOURCES

Modern aviation weather services have evolved beyond traditional telephone briefings and automated broadcasts to encompass comprehensive online platforms and digital resources. These web-based services provide pilots with instant access to current weather data, forecasts, and flight planning tools from any location with internet connectivity. Understanding these digital resources and their capabilities is essential for effective flight planning and weather decision-making in today's aviation environment.

### 1800wxbrief.com Portal Overview

The **1800wxbrief.com portal**, operated by Leidos Flight Service, serves as the primary web-based interface for official FAA weather briefing services. This comprehensive platform extends the capabilities of the traditional 1-800-WX-BRIEF telephone service into the digital realm, providing pilots with 24/7 access to the same weather information and briefing services available through FSS specialists.

The portal requires user registration and maintains detailed records of all weather briefings and flight plans filed through the system. Upon logging in, pilots can access their briefing history, saved routes, and aircraft profiles to streamline the flight planning process. The system integrates seamlessly with the national flight plan database, allowing users to file, amend, or close flight plans directly through the web interface.

**Key features** of the 1800wxbrief.com portal include interactive weather maps, customizable briefing formats, route-specific weather analysis, and automated NOTAM filtering. The system provides the same three types of briefings available by telephone: standard, abbreviated, and outlook briefings. Each briefing is automatically saved and can be referenced later for flight plan amendments or accident investigation purposes.

The portal displays weather data using both graphical and textual formats, allowing pilots to visualize weather patterns along their route of flight while accessing detailed METAR, TAF, and forecast information. **Real-time updates** ensure that displayed information reflects the most current conditions available from the National Weather Service and automated observation systems.

> **Important Note**: Weather briefings obtained through the 1800wxbrief.com portal satisfy the regulatory requirement for obtaining weather information prior to flight, provided the pilot reviews all relevant weather products for the proposed route and time of flight.

### Online Briefing Capabilities and Features

Online weather briefing services offer several advantages over traditional telephone briefings, including simultaneous access to multiple weather products, interactive mapping capabilities, and the ability to save and modify flight plans. These systems integrate various weather data sources to provide comprehensive situational awareness for flight planning.

**Interactive weather maps** allow pilots to overlay multiple weather products simultaneously, including NEXRAD precipitation data, surface observations, pilot reports, and forecast information. Users can zoom into specific areas of interest, animate radar displays to observe weather movement, and click on individual reporting stations to access detailed observations. This visual approach to weather analysis helps pilots identify patterns and trends that may not be apparent from textual products alone.

**Route-specific analysis tools** automatically extract weather information relevant to a planned flight path. By entering departure and destination airports along with proposed route and altitude information, the system generates customized briefings that focus on conditions along the flight track. This targeted approach reduces information overload while ensuring that critical weather factors are highlighted.

**Automated NOTAM processing** filters thousands of daily notices to present only those relevant to the specific flight. The system categorizes NOTAMs by importance and highlights critical items such as runway closures, navigation aid outages, and airspace restrictions that directly affect the planned flight.

Advanced briefing systems also provide **trend analysis** capabilities, showing how weather conditions have changed over recent hours and how they are forecast to evolve. This temporal perspective helps pilots make better decisions about departure timing and route selection.

### DUATS and NOAA Aviation Weather Services

The **Direct User Access Terminal System (DUATS)**, while no longer operated by the FAA, established the foundation for modern online weather services. Current NOAA aviation weather services continue this legacy through the **Aviation Weather Center (AWC)** and **Aviation Digital Data Service (ADDS)** platforms.

The **NOAA Aviation Weather Center** (aviationweather.gov) provides comprehensive access to all aviation weather products issued by the National Weather Service. The AWC website organizes information by product type and geographic region, making it easy to locate specific forecasts, observations, or warnings. The site features interactive maps for surface analysis, upper-air charts, and significant weather forecasts.

**ADDS (Aviation Digital Data Service)** offers both graphical and textual weather products through a user-friendly web interface. The system provides real-time access to METARs, TAFs, AIRMETs, SIGMETs, pilot reports, and upper-air data. Interactive features allow users to plot weather stations on maps, create custom data queries, and export information for use in other applications.

Key ADDS capabilities include **station plots** that display current conditions using standard meteorological symbols, **composite charts** that combine multiple weather elements on a single map, and **time-series plots** that show how conditions at a specific location have changed over recent hours. The system also provides **forecast verification statistics** that help users understand the typical accuracy of different forecast products.

The NOAA aviation weather services complement official FSS briefings by providing detailed access to source weather data. While these services offer comprehensive information, they do not provide the interpretive guidance and regulatory compliance documentation that comes with official FSS briefings.

### Commercial Weather Service Providers

**Commercial weather service providers** offer enhanced weather products and analysis tools that go beyond basic government services. These companies often provide higher resolution data, specialized forecasts for aviation operations, and advanced analytical capabilities tailored to specific user needs.

Popular commercial aviation weather providers include **ForeFlight**, **Garmin Pilot**, **Jeppesen**, and **WSI (Weather Services International)**. These services typically offer subscription-based access to comprehensive weather databases, flight planning tools, and mobile applications that provide weather access from anywhere.

Commercial providers often enhance basic weather data through **value-added processing**, such as improved resolution, specialized algorithms for aviation hazards, and integration with aircraft performance data. For example, some services provide turbulence forecasts based on atmospheric models specifically tuned for aviation operations, or icing potential analyses that consider aircraft-specific factors.

**Advanced features** commonly offered by commercial providers include three-dimensional weather visualization, route optimization based on weather conditions, automated flight plan amendments for weather avoidance, and integration with aircraft systems for in-flight weather updates. Many services also provide **historical weather data** for post-flight analysis and training purposes.

When selecting commercial weather services, pilots should verify that the provider offers access to all required regulatory weather products and maintains appropriate data quality standards. Some commercial services are **approved as official weather sources** for regulatory compliance, while others are intended for supplemental use only.

### Mobile and Tablet Weather Applications

**Mobile weather applications** have revolutionized aviation weather access by providing real-time information directly to smartphones and tablets. These applications range from simple METAR displays to comprehensive flight planning suites with full briefing capabilities.

Leading aviation weather apps include **ForeFlight Mobile**, **Garmin Pilot**, **FltPlan Go**, and **AvWxWorx**. These applications typically provide access to all standard aviation weather products, including surface observations, forecasts, radar imagery, satellite data, and pilot reports. **Key features** to evaluate in mobile weather applications include data currency indicators, offline capability for areas with poor connectivity, customizable display options, and integration with other aviation tools. **Data connectivity** represents a critical consideration for mobile weather apps. Applications may receive data through cellular networks, Wi-Fi connections, or aviation-specific data links such as ADS-B FIS-B services. Pilots should understand the connectivity requirements and limitations of their chosen applications, particularly for in-flight use.

> **Regulatory Consideration**: While mobile weather applications provide convenient access to weather information, pilots must ensure that their weather briefing meets regulatory requirements. However, pilots must understand the limitations of in-flight weather data, including update frequencies and coverage areas, to use this information effectively.

## WEATHER DATA COLLECTION SYSTEMS

The foundation of all weather forecasts, advisories, and briefings lies in the systematic collection of atmospheric data from multiple sources across the globe. Weather observations are gathered through four primary types of collection systems: surface, upper air, radar, and satellite. These interconnected networks provide the comprehensive data necessary for meteorologists to analyze current conditions and develop accurate forecasts that pilots depend upon for flight safety decisions.

Modern weather data collection represents a sophisticated blend of automated systems and human observations, creating a continuous stream of information that feeds into the global weather analysis and forecasting system. Understanding these collection methods helps pilots appreciate both the capabilities and limitations of the weather information they receive.

### Surface Weather Observation Networks

Surface aviation weather observations form the backbone of weather reporting at airports and weather stations worldwide. These observations provide critical information about conditions at specific locations, creating a network of data points that meteorologists use to construct broader weather patterns.

**Automated Weather Observing System (AWOS)** represents one of the primary automated surface observation systems used throughout the United States. AWOS installations provide continuous, real-time weather observations at airports where human observers may not be available around the clock. These systems automatically measure and report wind speed and direction, visibility, precipitation, cloud ceiling heights, and temperature and dewpoint.

AWOS systems are classified into different levels based on their sensing capabilities. AWOS-1 provides basic measurements including altimeter setting, wind data, temperature, dewpoint, and density altitude. AWOS-2 adds visibility sensing capability, while AWOS-3 incorporates cloud ceiling measurement and precipitation identification. The most advanced AWOS-3P systems can also detect and report the type of precipitation occurring.

**Automated Surface Observing System (ASOS)** serves as the primary surface weather observing network for the National Weather Service, Department of Defense, and Federal Aviation Administration. ASOS represents a more sophisticated automated observation system compared to AWOS, providing enhanced measurement capabilities and more comprehensive weather reporting.

ASOS systems measure the same basic parameters as AWOS but with greater precision and additional capabilities. These systems can detect and report present weather phenomena including precipitation type and intensity, fog, haze, and other visibility restrictions. ASOS installations also measure pressure tendency, which indicates whether barometric pressure is rising, falling, or remaining steady.

> **Key Distinction**: ASOS systems include precipitation discriminators that can distinguish between liquid and frozen precipitation, while not all AWOS installations have this capability. This distinction appears in METAR reports as "AO2" for systems with precipitation discriminators and "AO1" for those without.

Both AWOS and ASOS systems operate continuously, updating observations typically every minute and issuing routine reports every 20 minutes or when significant changes occur. These automated systems have revolutionized surface weather observation by providing consistent, objective measurements that are immediately available to pilots, forecasters, and other aviation users.

Surface observations from both automated and human-staffed locations are compiled into **Aviation Routine Weather Reports (METARs)**, which provide standardized weather information for specific airports. The network of surface observation points across the United States and internationally creates a comprehensive picture of surface weather conditions that serves as the foundation for all weather analysis and forecasting.

### Upper Air Observation Methods

Upper air weather observations present unique challenges compared to surface measurements, requiring specialized techniques to gather atmospheric data at various altitudes. These observations are essential for understanding the three-dimensional structure of weather systems and providing the data necessary for aviation forecasts at different flight levels.

**Radiosonde observations** represent the primary method for systematic upper air data collection. A **radiosonde** consists of a small instrumentation package suspended beneath a weather balloon filled with hydrogen or helium. These balloons are launched twice daily from approximately 75 locations across the United States and hundreds of sites worldwide, typically at 0000Z and 1200Z.

The radiosonde ascends at approximately 1,000 feet per minute, continuously measuring and transmitting atmospheric pressure, temperature, humidity, wind speed, and wind direction back to ground stations via a 300-milliwatt radio transmitter. As the balloon rises through the atmosphere, it expands due to decreasing atmospheric pressure until it reaches its elastic limit at approximately 20 feet in diameter, typically at altitudes exceeding 100,000 feet.

During its ascent, which can last over two hours, the radiosonde may drift as far as 125 miles from the launch point while experiencing temperatures as low as -130°F and pressures only a few thousandths of sea level values. When the balloon bursts, a parachute deployed with the radiosonde slows its descent to minimize hazards to people and property on the ground. Approximately 20 percent of the 75,000+ radiosondes launched annually are recovered and refurbished for reuse.

**Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay (AMDAR)** provides automated weather observations from commercial aircraft through international cooperation. This program utilizes onboard sensors and probes on participating aircraft to measure wind, temperature, humidity, turbulence, and icing conditions during normal flight operations.

AMDAR contributes approximately 220,000 to 230,000 aircraft observations daily worldwide, providing valuable data during climb, cruise, and descent phases of flight. These observations are particularly valuable because they provide real-time atmospheric data from altitudes and locations where radiosondes may not be available. The data is transmitted via **Very High Frequency (VHF)** communications through the **Aircraft Communications Addressing and Reporting System (ACARS)** or via satellite through **Aircraft to Satellite Data Acquisition and Relay (ASDAR)**.

**Meteorological Data Collection and Reporting System (MDCRS)** serves as the United States' automated airborne weather observation program. More than 1,500 participating aircraft collect and disseminate real-time upper-air weather observations, with data managed by Aeronautical Radio, Inc. (ARINC) and forwarded to the National Weather Service and research organizations.

MDCRS data collection occurs through each aircraft's **Flight Data Acquisition and Monitoring System (FDAMS)** and transmits via ACARS when within range, or ASDAR when beyond ACARS coverage. When aircraft operate outside communication range, observations are buffered and downloaded when connectivity is restored.

**Pilot Reports (PIREPs)** remain the only real-time source of information about certain atmospheric conditions, particularly turbulence, icing, and actual cloud heights encountered in flight. These reports provide ground truth verification of forecast conditions and alert other pilots to unexpected weather phenomena that automated systems cannot detect.

### Radar Weather Detection Systems

Radar technology provides real-time detection and analysis of precipitation and atmospheric phenomena over large geographic areas. Multiple types of radar systems serve different aspects of weather detection and aviation safety, each with specific capabilities and limitations.

**WSR-88D NEXRAD (Next Generation Weather Radar)** represents the backbone of the United States weather radar network. This Doppler radar system consists of 159 sites strategically positioned across the United States and selected overseas locations. The system operates as a joint venture between the Department of Commerce, Department of Defense, and Department of Transportation, with operational control by the National Weather Service, Air Force Weather Agency, and FAA.

NEXRAD radar operates in two distinct modes based on atmospheric conditions. In **clear air mode**, the radar operates at maximum sensitivity with slower antenna rotation, updating images approximately every 10 minutes to detect weak atmospheric returns when precipitation is not present. When precipitation develops, the system switches to **precipitation mode**, featuring faster antenna rotation that updates images every 4 to 6 minutes while focusing on stronger precipitation returns.

Precipitation intensity is measured in **decibels of Z (dBZ)** and color-coded on radar displays. The intensity levels are categorized as light (less than 30 dBZ), moderate (30-40 dBZ), heavy (greater than 40-50 dBZ), and extreme (50+ dBZ). These measurements correlate directly with Air Traffic Control precipitation intensity terminology used in pilot communications.

**Terminal Doppler Weather Radar (TDWR)** installations at major airports provide specialized detection of hazardous weather phenomena in terminal areas. TDWR systems focus specifically on detecting and alerting controllers to wind shear, gust fronts, and heavy precipitation that pose immediate threats to arriving and departing aircraft. These radars complement NEXRAD by providing enhanced resolution and faster updates in critical airport environments.

**FAA Airport Surveillance Radar** serves dual purposes of aircraft detection and weather observation. While primarily designed for air traffic control, these radars also detect precipitation location and intensity, enabling controllers to route aircraft around severe weather in airport environments. However, these systems typically have more limited weather detection capabilities compared to dedicated weather radars.

**Airborne Weather Radar** carried by individual aircraft provides pilots with real-time weather detection capabilities. These systems typically operate in C-band (around 6 GHz) or X-band (around 10 GHz) frequencies, offering both precipitation penetration capability for assessing thunderstorm extent and sufficient reflection from lighter precipitation for general weather detection.

> **Important Limitation**: All radar systems detect precipitation, not clouds. Areas of precipitation-free clouds, including those that may contain severe turbulence or icing conditions, will not appear on radar displays.

### Satellite Weather Monitoring

Satellite technology provides comprehensive weather monitoring capabilities that complement surface, upper air, and radar observations. Modern weather satellites offer continuous surveillance of atmospheric conditions over vast areas, including remote regions where conventional observations are not available.

**Geostationary satellites** positioned approximately 22,300 miles above the equator maintain constant watch over the same geographic areas, providing continuous monitoring of cloud patterns, temperature distributions, and atmospheric moisture. These satellites can detect developing weather systems hours before they affect surface locations, giving forecasters extended lead times for weather warnings and forecasts.

**Polar-orbiting satellites** circle the Earth at lower altitudes, typically around 500 miles, providing detailed atmospheric soundings and global coverage twice daily. These satellites carry sophisticated instruments that measure temperature and moisture profiles through the atmosphere, supplementing radiosonde observations with broader geographic coverage.

Satellite data contributes to aviation weather services through several applications. **Satellite-derived winds** supplement conventional wind observations, particularly over oceanic areas where other upper air observations are sparse. **Water vapor imagery** reveals atmospheric moisture patterns and jet stream locations that influence weather development and aviation turbulence.

Commercial satellite weather services now provide real-time weather data transmission to aircraft through satellite data links, enabling pilots to receive current weather information throughout flight, including NEXRAD radar data, METARs, TAFs, and other weather products directly in the cockpit.

### Automated vs. Human Weather Observations

The evolution from human weather observers to automated systems has fundamentally changed how aviation weather data is collected, with important implications for pilots understanding weather information sources and limitations.

**Human weather observers** historically provided comprehensive weather observations including subjective assessments of visibility, cloud types, and weather phenomena. Trained observers could identify subtle weather conditions and make qualitative judgments about changing conditions that automated systems might miss. However, human observations were limited by observer availability, consistency between different observers, and the practical constraints of 24-hour staffing at hundreds of locations.

**Automated weather stations** provide several significant advantages over human observations. They operate continuously without breaks, provide consistent measurement standards across all locations, and can detect and report changes more frequently than human observers. Automated systems also eliminate subjective interpretation differences between observers and reduce operational costs significantly.

However, automated systems also have important limitations that pilots must understand. Automated stations cannot identify all cloud types, may have difficulty distinguishing between different types of precipitation, and cannot make subjective assessments about rapidly changing conditions that an experienced human observer might detect.

**Augmented observations** represent a hybrid approach where automated systems provide basic measurements while human observers add supplementary information about conditions the automated systems cannot detect or properly categorize. This combination provides the consistency of automated measurements with the interpretive capabilities of human observers for critical weather phenomena.

> **METAR Identification**: Automated weather reports include "AUTO" in the METAR to indicate fully automated observations, while augmented stations may include specific remarks indicating which elements were automated versus human-observed.

The transition to automated systems has enabled expansion of weather observation networks to many airports that previously had no regular weather reporting, significantly improving weather information availability for flight planning and operations. However, pilots must remain aware of automated system limitations when interpreting weather reports and making flight decisions based on automated observations.

Understanding these various data collection systems helps pilots appreciate both the comprehensive nature of modern weather information and the limitations inherent in each collection method. This knowledge enables more informed interpretation of weather products and better aeronautical decision-making based on the strengths and weaknesses of different weather data sources.

## PILOT WEATHER REPORTS (PIREPS)

**Pilot Weather Reports (PIREPs)** represent one of the most critical sources of real-time weather information in aviation. These reports provide unique intelligence about actual flight conditions that cannot be obtained from any other source, including conditions between weather observation stations and at altitudes not covered by surface observations.

PIREPs are filed by pilots to report weather conditions encountered during flight. Unlike surface weather observations or forecasts, PIREPs provide real-time information about conditions as they actually exist in the air. This information proves invaluable for flight planning and in-flight decision making for other pilots operating in the same area.

> **Critical Note**: PIREPs remain the only real-time source of information regarding turbulence, icing, and cloud height conditions actually encountered in flight.

### PIREP Importance and Requirements

PIREPs serve multiple essential functions in the aviation weather system. They fill gaps in weather coverage, particularly between automated weather stations and at altitudes where surface observations cannot provide accurate information. Most importantly, they report conditions that automated systems cannot detect, such as turbulence intensity, icing severity, and actual cloud tops and bases.

**ATC PIREP Solicitation Requirements** mandate that when the ceiling is below 5,000 feet or visibility is at or below 5 miles, ATC facilities are required to solicit PIREPs from aircraft in the affected area. This requirement ensures continuous collection of critical weather information during marginal conditions.

Controllers and flight service specialists actively request PIREPs under specific conditions:
- Ceiling below 5,000 feet AGL
- Visibility 5 miles or less
- Significant weather phenomena reported or forecast
- When conditions differ significantly from forecasts

When pilots encounter unexpected weather conditions during flight, they are strongly encouraged to file PIREPs with either Flight Service Stations or ATC facilities. These reports are immediately added to the weather distribution system to brief other pilots and provide timely in-flight weather advisories.

### PIREP Format and Encoding Standards

PIREPs follow a standardized format that ensures consistent reporting and easy interpretation by weather personnel and other pilots. The standard reporting format uses specific elements and contractions that are universally understood throughout the aviation community.

**Required PIREP Elements** include items 1 through 5 of the standard form, plus at least one weather phenomenon encountered:

1. **UA** - Type of report (UA for routine, UUA for urgent)
2. **/OV** - Location relative to VOR or airport
3. **/TM** - Time in UTC (4 digits)
4. **/FL** - Altitude or flight level
5. **/TP** - Aircraft type

Additional elements reported as encountered:
- **/SK** - Sky cover and cloud information
- **/WX** - Flight visibility and weather
- **/TA** - Air temperature in Celsius
- **/WV** - Wind direction and speed
- **/TB** - Turbulence intensity and type
- **/IC** - Icing intensity and type
- **/RM** - Remarks for additional information

**UA Routine vs UUA Urgent PIREPs** distinguish between standard reports and those containing urgent weather information. UUA (Urgent PIREP) reports are used when conditions pose an immediate hazard to flight safety, such as severe turbulence, severe icing, wind shear, or other dangerous phenomena. These urgent reports receive priority handling and immediate distribution.

### Filing Procedures and Distribution

Filing a PIREP is straightforward and can be accomplished through multiple channels. Pilots can submit reports to Air Traffic Control facilities, Flight Service Stations, or approach/departure control. The key is providing accurate, timely information using the standard format.

**Location Reporting** uses three-letter NAVAID identifiers when possible. Reports specify position as either over a fix (**/OV ABC**) or on a radial and distance from a fix (**/OV ABC 090025** for 25 nautical miles on the 090-degree radial from station ABC).

**Time Reporting** always uses four-digit UTC format (**/TM 1450** for 1450 Zulu). This ensures universal understanding regardless of local time zones.

**Altitude Reporting** uses three digits for hundreds of feet (**/FL095** for 9,500 feet, **/FL310** for FL310). If altitude is unknown, the report uses **/FLUNKN**.

Once filed, PIREPs enter the weather distribution system immediately. ATC facilities and Flight Service Stations add these reports to weather dissemination networks, making them available to weather briefers and other pilots. PIREPs may be transmitted as individual reports or appended to routine surface weather observations.

### ATC PIREP Solicitation Requirements

Federal Aviation Administration regulations establish specific requirements for ATC facilities to actively solicit PIREPs during certain weather conditions. These requirements ensure systematic collection of critical weather information when conditions may affect flight safety.

**Mandatory Solicitation Conditions** require controllers to request PIREPs when:
- Ceiling is reported below 5,000 feet AGL
- Surface visibility is 5 statute miles or less
- Thunderstorms or other significant weather is reported or forecast
- Pilot reports indicate conditions significantly different from official observations

Controllers must actively solicit reports from aircraft operating in affected areas, not simply wait for voluntary reports. This proactive approach ensures continuous flow of real-time weather information during critical conditions.

**Distribution Priority** gives urgent PIREPs (UUA) immediate handling. These reports receive priority transmission and are quickly incorporated into weather advisories and briefings. Routine PIREPs (UA) are processed promptly but with standard priority.

The systematic collection and distribution of PIREPs creates a comprehensive real-time weather picture that significantly enhances flight safety. Each report contributes to the overall understanding of current conditions, helping pilots make informed decisions about route planning, altitude selection, and weather avoidance strategies.

> **Pilot Responsibility**: Every pilot shares responsibility for contributing to the PIREP system. Filing accurate, timely reports helps ensure all aviators have access to current weather conditions that may affect flight safety.

Example PIREP: **UA/OV GGG 090025/TM 1450/FL 060/TP C182/SK 080 OVC/WX FV04SM RA/TA 05/WV 270030KT/TB LGT/RM HVY RAIN**

This translates to: Routine pilot report, 25 nautical miles out on the 090-degree radial from Gregg County VOR at 1450 Zulu, 6,000 feet altitude, Cessna 182, 8,000-foot overcast ceiling, flight visibility 4 miles in rain, temperature 5 degrees Celsius, wind 270 degrees at 30 knots, light turbulence, with heavy rain reported in remarks.

## WEATHER APPS AND DIGITAL TOOLS

The aviation weather landscape has been revolutionized by **digital weather applications** and integrated flight planning tools. Modern pilots have access to sophisticated weather data through tablets, smartphones, and panel-mounted displays that provide real-time weather information, graphical presentations, and integrated flight planning capabilities. These tools supplement traditional weather briefing methods and offer enhanced situational awareness throughout all phases of flight.

### Mobile Weather Application Categories

**Aviation-specific weather apps** fall into several distinct categories based on their primary functions and data sources. **Electronic Flight Bag (EFB) applications** such as ForeFlight, Garmin Pilot, and Jeppesen FliteDeck Pro provide comprehensive flight planning with integrated weather displays. These apps combine aeronautical charts, approach plates, airport information, and weather data in a single platform.

**Specialized weather applications** focus primarily on meteorological data presentation. Apps like WeatherTap Aviation, Pilot Weather, and AeroWeather Pro emphasize weather visualization and analysis tools. These applications often provide detailed radar imagery, satellite loops, and forecast models specifically tailored for aviation use.

**General weather apps** with aviation features include Weather Underground, Weather.com, and RadarScope. While not designed exclusively for aviation, these apps often include features useful to pilots such as detailed radar imagery, severe weather alerts, and hourly forecasts. However, pilots must ensure these apps provide aviation-formatted data such as METARs and TAFs rather than public weather formats.

### Tablet-Based Weather Tools for Aviation

**Tablet-based applications** have become the standard for modern flight planning and weather analysis. The iPad remains the dominant platform, with most aviation apps optimized for iOS systems. Android tablets also support major aviation weather applications, though with sometimes limited functionality compared to their iOS counterparts.

**ForeFlight** leads the market with comprehensive weather integration including graphical METARs, overlay capabilities for precipitation, winds aloft, turbulence, and icing forecasts. The application provides **synthetic vision technology** combined with weather radar displays, allowing pilots to visualize weather relative to terrain and flight paths. ForeFlight's **Hazard Advisor** feature analyzes planned routes against current and forecast weather conditions, highlighting potential hazards along the route of flight.

**Garmin Pilot** offers similar comprehensive weather displays with tight integration to Garmin avionics systems. The application provides **3D weather visualization** that allows pilots to view weather layers at different altitudes relative to their planned flight path. Garmin Pilot's **Smart Airspace** feature displays temporary flight restrictions, NOTAMs, and special use airspace status integrated with weather displays.

**Weather display customization** in tablet apps allows pilots to layer multiple weather products simultaneously. Pilots can overlay NEXRAD radar returns with pilot reports, AIRMETs/SIGMETs, surface observations, and forecast models. However, pilots must be cautious about **information overload** when displaying multiple weather layers, as too much information can create confusion rather than clarity.

> **Critical Usage Note**: Tablet-based weather applications are approved for planning and situational awareness but may not meet requirements for official weather briefings under 14 CFR 91.103 unless specifically approved by the FAA.

### Real-Time Weather Radar Applications

**NEXRAD radar data** forms the foundation of most aviation weather applications. The **Weather Surveillance Radar-1988 Doppler (WSR-88D)** network provides precipitation intensity data that apps display using standardized color schemes. Light precipitation appears in green, moderate in yellow, heavy in red, and extreme in magenta or purple.

**Radar data limitations** must be understood by pilots using mobile applications. NEXRAD data can be **5 to 20 minutes old** by the time it appears on mobile devices due to processing, transmission, and display delays. The **mosaic creation process** combines data from multiple radar sites, potentially introducing additional delays. Fast-moving or rapidly developing weather systems may have moved significantly from their displayed positions.

**Base reflectivity displays** show precipitation intensity but cannot differentiate between rain, snow, or hail. The minimum elevation angle of NEXRAD radars creates **cone of silence** areas directly over radar sites where high-altitude precipitation may not be detected. Pilots must understand these limitations when using radar displays for weather avoidance decisions.

**Lightning detection** supplements radar data in many applications. The **National Lightning Detection Network (NLDN)** provides cloud-to-ground lightning strike data, typically displayed as symbols or dots on weather displays. Lightning data helps identify thunderstorm activity, especially in areas where precipitation may be minimal but electrical activity is significant.

### Integrated Flight Planning Weather Tools

**Route-specific weather analysis** represents one of the most valuable features of modern weather applications. These tools analyze planned flight routes against current and forecast weather conditions, providing **graphical weather profiles** along the intended path. Pilots can view ceiling heights, visibility, precipitation, turbulence, and icing conditions relative to their planned altitude and route.

**Vertical weather profiles** show atmospheric conditions at different altitudes along the route of flight. These displays help pilots select optimal altitudes to avoid adverse weather, such as climbing above icing layers or descending below turbulent conditions. The profiles integrate data from forecast models, pilot reports, and atmospheric soundings.

**Time-based weather animation** allows pilots to view how weather systems will affect their route at different times during the planned flight. This capability proves especially valuable for longer flights where weather conditions may change significantly between departure and arrival. Pilots can adjust departure times or routing based on forecast weather evolution.

**Alternate airport analysis** integrated into flight planning tools evaluates weather conditions at potential alternate airports. The applications can automatically suggest alternates based on forecast conditions and regulatory requirements, ensuring compliance with alternate airport weather minimums.

### Evaluation Criteria for Weather Apps

**Data source reliability** forms the primary evaluation criterion for aviation weather applications. Apps should clearly identify their weather data sources, update frequencies, and any data processing or interpretation performed. **FAA-approved data sources** include the Aviation Weather Center, National Weather Service, and certified commercial weather providers.

**Update frequency and currency** directly impact the utility of weather information. Applications should clearly display the **age of weather data** and provide automatic updates when newer information becomes available. The timestamp or age indication should be prominently displayed for all weather products.

**User interface design** affects both usability and safety. Weather displays should use **standardized color schemes** consistent with official weather products. Text should be readable in various lighting conditions, and controls should be accessible during flight operations. The interface should minimize the number of steps required to access critical weather information.

**Integration capabilities** with other avionics systems add significant value. Applications that can share flight plans, weather data, and hazard information with panel-mounted systems provide seamless cockpit integration. **Data connectivity options** including WiFi, cellular, and satellite links ensure weather access throughout the flight environment.

### Satellite-Based Weather Uplink Services

**SiriusXM Weather** provides satellite-delivered weather data to properly equipped aircraft and portable devices. The service offers **real-time weather updates** including NEXRAD radar, METARs, TAFs, winds aloft, AIRMETs, SIGMETs, and pilot reports. SiriusXM weather data typically updates every **2.5 to 5 minutes** for radar products and varies for other meteorological products.

**Satellite coverage areas** for SiriusXM extend throughout the continental United States and parts of Canada and Mexico. However, terrain masking in mountainous areas or at low altitudes can interrupt satellite reception. The **line-of-sight requirement** for satellite reception means that aircraft attitude and geographic location affect data availability.

**Data compression techniques** used in satellite transmission can affect weather display quality. Radar images may show **pixelation or smoothing** that could mask small-scale weather features. Pilots should understand these limitations when using satellite weather data for tactical weather avoidance decisions.

### ADS-B Weather Information Reception

**Flight Information Service-Broadcast (FIS-B)** provides weather data through the ADS-B ground station network on the **978 MHz Universal Access Transceiver (UAT)** frequency. FIS-B services are available to aircraft equipped with appropriate ADS-B receivers at no additional subscription cost beyond the required equipment.

**FIS-B weather products** include METARs, SPECIs, TAFs, NEXRAD precipitation maps, NOTAMs, AIRMETs, SIGMETs, winds and temperatures aloft, pilot reports, and temporary flight restriction information. The service provides **regional and CONUS-wide** NEXRAD coverage with update intervals varying by product type.

**Coverage limitations** for FIS-B relate to the ground station network and line-of-sight requirements. Coverage is generally available above **5,000 feet AGL** throughout most of the continental United States, with reduced coverage at lower altitudes in mountainous terrain. Pilots should verify FIS-B coverage for their intended route and altitude.

**Equipment compatibility** varies among different ADS-B receivers and display systems. Some portable devices can receive FIS-B data when connected to compatible ADS-B receivers, while others require integrated systems. Pilots must verify their equipment capabilities and limitations before relying on FIS-B weather services.

### Weather App Accuracy and Currency Verification

**Cross-reference verification** represents the most critical aspect of using weather applications. Pilots should compare app-displayed weather data with official sources such as FSS briefings, DUATS, or 1800wxbrief.com. Discrepancies may indicate data transmission errors, processing delays, or application-specific interpretations of weather information.

**Time stamp awareness** requires pilots to understand when weather data was collected, processed, and displayed on their devices. The **observation time** for surface weather may differ significantly from the **display time** on mobile applications due to processing and transmission delays. Pilots should account for these delays when making weather-related decisions.

**Data quality indicators** in professional weather applications help pilots assess information reliability. These may include confidence levels for forecast products, data age warnings, or system status indicators. Applications should clearly identify when weather data is unavailable, outdated, or potentially unreliable.

**Regulatory compliance** considerations apply when using weather applications for required weather information. While weather apps provide valuable situational awareness, they may not substitute for official weather briefings required by **14 CFR 91.103**. Pilots should understand which app-provided weather products meet regulatory requirements and which serve only as supplementary information.

**Backup systems and redundancy** become essential when relying heavily on electronic weather information. Equipment failures, data connectivity issues, or power problems can eliminate access to critical weather data during flight. Pilots should maintain alternative weather information sources and procedures for continued safe operations when primary weather systems fail.

## SELECTING APPROPRIATE WEATHER SOURCES

Selecting the right weather sources for flight operations is a critical skill that directly impacts aviation safety. With numerous weather products and services available, pilots must understand how to match specific sources to their flight requirements while ensuring compliance with regulatory standards. The process involves evaluating the **currency**, **reliability**, and **official status** of weather information sources.

### MATCHING WEATHER SOURCES TO FLIGHT REQUIREMENTS

The first step in weather information gathering involves matching the appropriate sources to specific flight characteristics and operational requirements. Different flight types demand different weather information priorities.

For **local VFR flights** within 25 nautical miles of the departure airport, a standard briefing from Flight Service Station (FSS) combined with current METAR observations provides adequate information. These flights primarily require current conditions, short-term forecasts, and any applicable NOTAMs or temporary flight restrictions.

**Cross-country VFR flights** require more comprehensive weather planning. Pilots need area forecasts (FA), winds and temperatures aloft forecasts (FB), and multiple METAR observations along the route. Significant weather prognostic charts help identify potential weather systems that may affect the flight path over time.

**IFR operations** demand the most thorough weather analysis. Beyond the standard VFR requirements, IFR flights require detailed terminal aerodrome forecasts (TAF) for departure and arrival airports, plus alternates. Pilots must analyze approaches available, ceiling and visibility trends, and potential conditions requiring alternate airport selection.

**High-altitude operations** above 24,000 feet require specialized products including high-level significant weather prognostic charts, jet stream analysis, and clear air turbulence forecasts. Upper-level SIGMET information becomes particularly important for these operations.

> **Flight Planning Consideration**: Always consider the **total flight time** when selecting weather sources. Flights exceeding 2-3 hours require examination of forecast trends and potential weather system movement during the planned flight duration.

### PRIMARY VS. SUPPLEMENTARY WEATHER INFORMATION

Weather sources are classified into two distinct categories that serve different operational purposes and regulatory requirements.

**Primary weather sources** include those recognized by the FAA as official weather information suitable for regulatory compliance. The Flight Service Station briefing system represents the gold standard for primary weather information. FSS specialists provide standard, abbreviated, or outlook briefings that include all relevant weather data for the proposed flight.

Automated Flight Service Station products constitute primary sources, including the telephone briefing service (1-800-WX-BRIEF) and the 1800wxbrief.com portal. These systems provide official weather data suitable for meeting regulatory requirements under Federal Aviation Regulations.

National Weather Service products accessed through official channels represent primary information sources. This includes METAR observations, TAF forecasts, area forecasts, winds and temperatures aloft forecasts, and all forms of inflight weather advisories (AIRMETs, SIGMETs, Convective SIGMETs).

**Supplementary weather sources** enhance situational awareness but cannot substitute for official weather briefings. Commercial weather applications, while providing valuable real-time data, fall into this category. Popular applications like ForeFlight, Garmin Pilot, and others aggregate official weather data but are not themselves official sources.

Pilot reports (PIREPs) serve a unique role as both primary and supplementary information. While PIREPs are official weather observations, they supplement other weather data by providing real-time conditions that automated systems cannot detect, such as turbulence intensity, icing conditions, and cloud tops.

In-flight weather radar displays, whether ground-based NEXRAD or onboard systems, provide supplementary information. These systems help pilots navigate around precipitation but cannot replace official weather briefings or updates from Air Traffic Control.

> **Regulatory Reminder**: FAR 91.103 requires pilots to become familiar with "all available information" concerning the flight, but does not specify exclusive use of primary sources. However, for IFR operations, official weather sources provide the regulatory foundation for flight planning decisions.

### CURRENCY AND RELIABILITY OF DIFFERENT SOURCES

Understanding the **age** and **update frequency** of weather information is crucial for safe flight operations. Different weather products have varying update cycles and latency periods that affect their operational utility.

METAR observations update every hour, with special observations (SPECI) issued when conditions change significantly. These reports typically reflect conditions observed within the previous 10-15 minutes, making them highly current for tactical flight decisions.

Terminal Aerodrome Forecasts (TAF) issue every six hours at 0000Z, 0600Z, 1200Z, and 1800Z, covering 24-30 hour periods. TAFs provide strategic planning information but may not reflect rapidly changing conditions between issuance times.

Area Forecasts (FA) update every 12 hours and remain valid for 18 hours with a 6-hour categorical outlook. These products help pilots understand regional weather patterns but lack the detail needed for specific airport planning.

Radar weather information presents unique currency challenges. Ground-based NEXRAD data displayed in aircraft systems may be 5-15 minutes old by the time it reaches the cockpit display. This latency becomes critical when dealing with rapidly moving or intensifying weather systems.

Winds and temperatures aloft forecasts, based on radiosonde data collected at 0000Z and 1200Z, provide 12-hour forecasts issued twice daily. These products remain reasonably accurate for general planning but may not reflect local wind variations or sudden changes due to weather system movement.

> **Currency Rule**: Weather information over 2 hours old should be verified against current observations before making critical flight decisions. Rapidly changing weather conditions may require more frequent updates.

### REGULATORY REQUIREMENTS FOR WEATHER INFORMATION

Federal Aviation Regulations establish specific requirements for weather information that vary based on flight rules and operational complexity.

**FAR 91.103** mandates that pilots become familiar with all available information concerning any flight. For flights not in the vicinity of an airport, this explicitly includes weather reports and forecasts. The regulation applies to all flights, regardless of flight rules, making weather information gathering a universal requirement.

**Part 91 VFR operations** do not specify particular weather sources, but pilots must ensure compliance with VFR weather minimums throughout the planned flight. This requires understanding both current conditions and forecast weather along the route.

**IFR operations under FAR 91.169** require specific weather information for flight planning. Pilots must have weather reports and forecasts for departure, destination, and alternate airports (when required). The regulations specify minimum ceiling and visibility requirements for various airport categories.

**Commercial operations** under Parts 121 and 135 have more stringent weather information requirements, including designated weather sources and specific briefing protocols. These operations typically require use of approved weather services and may prohibit reliance on certain supplementary sources.

For **alternate airport selection**, IFR flights must consider forecast weather conditions at the estimated time of arrival. The regulations specify that if weather conditions are forecast to be below certain minimums, an alternate airport must be designated and meet specific weather criteria.

Flight Service Station briefings provide regulatory compliance for weather information requirements. The briefing process creates a record of weather information provided, which can be referenced if questions arise about preflight planning decisions.

> **Compliance Note**: While regulations require familiarity with weather information, they do not mandate exclusive use of government sources. However, using official sources provides the strongest regulatory compliance position.

### BEST PRACTICES FOR WEATHER INFORMATION GATHERING

Effective weather information gathering follows a systematic approach that combines multiple sources and verification techniques to build a comprehensive weather picture.

**Sequential Information Building** starts with the broad weather picture and narrows to specific details. Begin with surface analysis charts and area forecasts to understand large-scale weather patterns. Progress to route-specific information including winds aloft and area forecasts for the planned flight path. Conclude with airport-specific information from METARs, TAFs, and local weather observations.

**Multiple Source Cross-Verification** enhances weather information reliability. Compare forecast conditions with current observations to assess forecast accuracy. Verify radar weather displays against pilot reports and official weather advisories. Cross-reference automated weather observations with nearby reporting stations to identify local variations or equipment malfunctions.

**Trend Analysis** involves examining weather information over time to identify patterns and changes. Compare current conditions with previous observations to understand weather system movement and intensification. Analyze forecast trends to anticipate conditions during different phases of the planned flight.

**Contingency Planning** requires identifying weather scenarios that could affect the flight and planning appropriate responses. This includes identifying alternate airports, considering fuel requirements for weather deviations, and establishing personal weather minimums that exceed regulatory requirements.

For **pre-flight weather gathering**, start the process 2-4 hours before departure to allow time for thorough analysis and flight planning adjustments. Update weather information immediately before departure to ensure currency of critical information.

**In-flight weather information** requires different strategies due to communication and time constraints. Establish a pattern of regular weather updates through Flight Watch (122.0 MHz) or Flight Service. Use ATC weather radar information and pilot reports to verify and supplement other weather data.

**Documentation practices** should include recording weather briefing times and sources, particularly for IFR flights. Note any significant weather features or decisions made based on weather information. This documentation proves valuable for post-flight analysis and regulatory compliance.

> **Best Practice Summary**: Effective weather information gathering combines official sources with supplementary information, emphasizes cross-verification, and maintains currency throughout all phases of flight operations. The goal is building a comprehensive, accurate picture of weather conditions that supports safe flight decision-making.

Weather source selection represents a fundamental pilot skill that improves with experience and systematic practice. Understanding the strengths and limitations of various weather products enables pilots to make informed decisions that enhance flight safety while maintaining regulatory compliance.

## Summary

Review the key points from this unit:

- The U.S. aviation weather system operates as a multi-agency collaborative network involving the NWS, FAA, DOD, and international organizations to ensure comprehensive weather coverage and eliminate single points of failure.
- The National Weather Service serves as the primary meteorological authority, operating NEXRAD radar systems and issuing TAFs, SIGMETs, and AIRMETs for aviation operations.
- Flight Service Stations provide 24-hour weather briefing services through 1-800-WX-BRIEF, serving as the cornerstone of aviation weather information delivery to pilots.
- Three types of FSS weather briefings are available: standard (comprehensive preflight), abbreviated (updates to previous briefings), and outlook (planning for departures 6+ hours away).
- Weather data collection involves surface observations from over 1,000 locations, upper-air radiosonde releases, aircraft meteorological data relay (AMDAR), and pilot reports (PIREPs).
- En Route Flight Advisory Service (Flight Watch) operates on 122.0 MHz during daylight hours to provide real-time weather information to aircraft in flight.
- Automated weather services including TIBS and HIWAS supplement FSS briefings by providing continuous telephone and radio broadcasts of weather information.
- HIWAS broadcasts hazardous weather advisories over selected VORs identified by an "H" symbol on aeronautical charts.
- Weather information serves as the cornerstone of flight safety, directly influencing go/no-go decisions, route planning, altitude selection, and fuel requirements.
- FAR 91.103 requires pilots to become familiar with all available weather information, making weather briefings a mandatory part of preflight planning rather than optional.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Flight Service Station (FSS)** | Government-operated facilities providing 24-hour weather briefing services and flight planning assistance to pilots |
| **NEXRAD** | Weather Surveillance Radar-1988 Doppler system operated by NWS providing detailed precipitation and wind information |
| **PIREP** | Pilot Report providing real-time information about turbulence, icing, and actual flight conditions that cannot be obtained through automated systems |
| **AMDAR** | Aircraft Meteorological Data Relay program using commercial aircraft as mobile weather stations to provide upper-air observations |
| **Standard Briefing** | Comprehensive weather briefing following a systematic sequence covering all aspects of weather conditions for a planned flight |
| **EFAS (Flight Watch)** | En Route Flight Advisory Service operating on 122.0 MHz providing weather information to aircraft in flight |
| **HIWAS** | Hazardous Inflight Weather Advisory Service broadcasting weather advisories over selected VOR navigation aids |
| **TIBS** | Telephone Information Briefing Service providing automated recordings of meteorological and aeronautical information |
| **SPECI** | Special weather observation issued when significant weather changes occur between regular hourly reports |
| **Radiosonde** | Instrument package attached to weather balloons providing upper-air temperature, humidity, pressure, and wind data |
| **ASOS/AWOS** | Automated Surface/Weather Observing Systems providing continuous automated weather observations at airports |
| **Center Weather Service Unit (CWSU)** | FAA facilities providing specialized weather support to Air Route Traffic Control Centers |
| **Terminal Doppler Weather Radar (TDWR)** | FAA-operated radar systems at major airports detecting wind shear, microbursts, and hazardous terminal area weather |
| **Flight Information Service-Broadcast (FIS-B)** | Digital weather information transmitted through the ADS-B network to properly equipped aircraft |
